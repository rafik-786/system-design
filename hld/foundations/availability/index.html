Section 1

TL;DR — The One-Minute Version

Mental Model: Availability is the percentage of time your system actually works when someone tries to use it. Think of a store’s opening hours — if you’re closed when customers show up, it doesn’t matter how great your products are. Nobody cares how fast your app is if it’s down.

Here’s the idea in plain English. You build a service. People depend on it. They expect it to be there when they need it — 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. AvailabilityThe fraction of time a system is operational and accessible. Usually expressed as a percentage like 99.9%. Higher availability = less downtime = happier users. is a single number that captures how well you deliver on that promise. It’s expressed as a percentage: 99.9% means your system was reachable for 99.9% of the time, and down for the remaining 0.1%.

That 0.1% sounds tiny, right? Let’s do the math. There are 8,760 hours in a year. 0.1% of that is 8.76 hours of downtime. That’s an entire workday where your users are locked out. Now compare that to 99.99% — that’s only 52 minutes for the whole year. One extra digit of nines just bought you 8 hours of uptime. That single digit is the difference between "our site goes down sometimes" and "our site basically never goes down."

Engineers talk about availability using a shorthand called "the nines"A shorthand for availability percentages. "Three nines" = 99.9%. "Four nines" = 99.99%. "Five nines" = 99.999%. Each additional nine means 10x less allowed downtime — and roughly 10x more engineering effort and cost.. Three nines (99.9%), four nines (99.99%), five nines (99.999%). Each additional nine is 10 times harder to achieve — it demands better architecture, more redundancy, smarter monitoring, and a lot more money. Most consumer apps aim for three or four nines. Only mission-critical systems (banks, hospitals, emergency services) push for five.

Every extra nine they squeeze out is worth tens of millions.

One-line takeaway: Availability is simple — "was your system working when the user showed up?" Everything else on this page is about getting that answer closer to "always."

Section 3

First Attempt — "Just Keep the Server Running"

When most teams first think about availability, their instinct is simple: "just make sure the server doesn’t go down." They set up a monitoring dashboard. They have someone check it every few hours. If something crashes, they SSH into the box and restart it. Problem solved, right?

This approach has a name: reactive operations. Wait for something to break, then fix it by hand. It works fine when you’re small — one server, a few hundred users, and the developer is always near a laptop. But it falls apart spectacularly as you grow.

Here’s why the "just keep it running" approach crumbles:

  • Humans are slow. Even the fastest on-call engineer takes 10–20 minutes to wake up, read the alert, open a laptop, VPN in, and start diagnosing. That’s 10–20 minutes of downtime before the fix even begins.
  • Humans are unavailable. People sleep. People go on vacation. People are in meetings, on planes, or just in the shower. A system that depends on a human being immediately available 24/7/365 is not a reliable system — it’s a ticking time bomb.
  • Restarts don’t fix root causes. You restart the server, traffic floods back in, and the same thing that crashed it before crashes it again 5 minutes later. Now you’re stuck in a restart loop at 3 AM.
  • One server = one point of failure. While that server is restarting — even for 30 seconds — every single user gets an error page. There’s no backup to handle traffic during the gap.

In the real world, this is exactly what small teams use. They sign up for UptimeRobot (uptimerobot.com — free tier monitors 50 URLs every 5 minutes) or Better Stack (betterstack.com). These tools ping your endpoint every few minutes and send you an SMS or Slack message when it's down. That's the entire "monitoring" setup for thousands of startups.

The simplest health check you can run right now from any terminal:

terminal — health-check.sh 
# The simplest health check — returns HTTP status code
curl -o /dev/null -s -w "%{http_code}" https://your-api.com/health
# Returns: 200 (healthy) or 500/503 (unhealthy) or 000 (unreachable)

# Run it in a loop every 30 seconds — poor man's monitoring
while true; do
  STATUS=$(curl -o /dev/null -s -w "%{http_code}" https://your-api.com/health)
  if [ "$STATUS" != "200" ]; then
    echo "$(date): DOWN! Status=$STATUS" | tee -a /var/log/health.log
    # Send yourself a Slack webhook, email, etc.
  fi
  sleep 30
done

This is genuinely what people start with. But it has all the problems we listed — when it detects a problem, a human still has to wake up and fix it. The tools get fancier (Pingdom, Datadog, PagerDuty for on-call scheduling) but the fundamental model is the same: detect → alert human → human fixes. The bottleneck is always the human.

The fundamental flaw: Manual monitoring creates a floor on your downtime. No matter how fast your team is, you’ll never get below 5–10 minutes of downtime per incident. For 99.99% availability, your total annual downtime budget is 52 minutes. A single manually-recovered incident can eat 80% of your entire year’s budget.
Real tools teams actually use at each stage:
Free/Starter: UptimeRobot (free, 50 monitors), Better Stack (free tier), curl cron jobs
Growing: Pingdom ($15/mo), StatusCake, Checkly (also tests APIs)
Scale: Datadog ($15/host/mo), New Relic, PagerDuty (on-call rotation + escalation)
Public status pages: Atlassian Statuspage (statuspage.io — used by Twilio, Cloudflare, Dropbox), Instatus, Cachet (self-hosted)
Think First

Manual recovery takes ~40 minutes per incident. Your target is 99.99% availability (52 min/year). How many incidents can you afford per year before breaking your SLA?

52 ÷ 40 = 1.3 incidents. You can barely afford ONE manually-recovered outage per year. That’s clearly unsustainable. You need a way to recover in seconds, not minutes.
Section 4

Where It Breaks — The Four Availability Killers

In 2023, Datadog analyzed 3.5 billion containers across 85,000+ organizations and found that 73% of outages fall into just four categories. Knowing which killer took down your system tells you exactly which defense to build — because the fix for a bad deployment is completely different from the fix for an expired TLS certificate.

Planned maintenance is the sneakiest killer because teams think it doesn’t count. "We scheduled it for 2 AM, so it’s fine." But your users in another timezone are wide awake at 2 AM. And your SLA doesn’t care why you were down — only that you were. If your system needs to go offline for patches, upgrades, or migrations, that downtime eats into your availability number just like any crash would. AWS handles this transparently for managed services — when they patch an RDS database, they do a multi-AZ failoverRDS multi-AZ maintains a synchronous standby replica in a different Availability Zone. During maintenance, AWS patches the standby first, promotes it to primary, then patches the old primary. Downtime is typically under 60 seconds.: patch the standby, promote it, then patch the old primary. Downtime: under 60 seconds. That's the gold standard for planned maintenance.

Hardware failures are inevitable but relatively rare for any single machine. The problem is scale: if you run 100 servers, the probability that at least one fails in a given month is surprisingly high. You can’t prevent them, only prepare for them. Google published a study on their data centers: in a cluster of 10,000 servers, they see ~1,000 hard drive failures per year and ~5 machine reboots per day from various hardware issues. At that scale, failure isn't an exception — it's a constant background hum.

Software deployments are the #1 cause of outages at most companies. Studies consistently show that 60–70% of outages are caused by changeIn operations, "change" means any modification to a running system — code deployments, config updates, infrastructure changes. Change is necessary for improvement but is also the biggest source of failures. That’s why deployment strategies (canary, blue-green) exist. — someone pushed new code, updated a config, or changed an infrastructure setting. The system was working fine until someone touched it. The Facebook BGP outage (Oct 2021, 6 hours down) was a config change. The AWS us-east-1 outage (Dec 2021, 7 hours) was triggered by an internal network automation tool. Cloudflare's June 2022 outage affecting 19 data centers? A network config change during routine maintenance.

Certificate and config expiry is the most embarrassing category because it’s 100% preventable. Your TLS certificateA digital certificate that encrypts traffic between users and your server (the "S" in HTTPS). Certificates have expiration dates. When one expires, browsers show scary security warnings and many users can’t access the site at all. expires, browsers show users a scary security warning, and nobody can reach your site. Microsoft Teams went down in February 2020 because a TLS certificate expired. Slack had a similar certificate outage. Ericsson's expired certificate caused a 4G outage for 32 million O2 users in the UK in 2018. These companies have thousands of engineers — and still forgot to renew a certificate. The fix is automation: Let's Encrypt issues free 90-day certificates that auto-renew, and AWS Certificate Manager handles renewal automatically for certificates attached to ALBs and CloudFront distributions.

Think First

If 70% of outages come from software deployments, what’s the safest thing to do — deploy less often, or deploy more often?

Counterintuitively, deploying MORE often is safer. Small, frequent deployments are easier to test, easier to roll back, and affect less code. A massive deployment every 3 months is a giant gamble. A tiny deployment every day is a controlled risk. This is the core insight behind continuous deployment.
Section 5

The Breakthrough — Redundancy Kills Single Points of Failure

Think First

Your architecture has: 1 load balancer, 2 web servers, 1 database, 1 cache server. Draw the request path. How many single points of failure can you count? If each component has 99.9% uptime independently, what’s the combined availability of the full chain?

Hint: Components in series multiply their availabilities: 0.999 × 0.999 × 0.999 × 0.999 = 99.6%. Three single points of failure (LB, DB, cache). The 2 web servers help — but the chain is only as strong as its weakest single-instance link.

The manual approach fails because it relies on one of everything — one server, one database, one human to fix things. When that one thing breaks, everything stops. The breakthrough insight is embarrassingly simple: have two of everything.

If you have two web servers and one crashes, the other keeps serving traffic. If you have two database replicas and one goes offline, the other answers queries. If you have two data centers and one loses power, the other takes over. At every layer of your system, the question is: "if this component dies right now, is there another one ready to pick up the work?"

This idea — redundancyHaving more copies of a component than you need so that if one fails, others take over. It’s the same reason a car has a spare tire — you don’t need it until you really, really need it. — is the single most important concept in availability engineering. It turns every "single point of failure" into a "we’re fine, the backup took over."

Notice what changed. On the left, every single component is a domino — knock one over and the whole system falls. On the right, every layer has spares. The load balancer dies? The other one is already routing traffic. A server crashes? The remaining two absorb the load. The primary database fails? The replica takes over.

And here’s the key: the switchover happens automatically, in seconds. No human needed. No 3 AM phone call. The system detects the failure (via health checks), removes the dead component from the pool, and keeps running. The engineer can investigate the root cause on Monday morning with a coffee in hand.

What does "detects the failure" actually look like in practice? Here are three real health check configurations used in production systems today:

AWS Route 53 can health-check your endpoints from multiple global locations. If enough checks fail, Route 53 stops sending DNS traffic to that endpoint. Here's the CLI command to create one:

terminal — route53-health-check.sh 
# Create a Route 53 health check — pings /health every 30s from 8 global locations
aws route53 create-health-check \
  --caller-reference "my-api-$(date +%s)" \
  --health-check-config '{
    "IPAddress": "203.0.113.50",
    "Port": 443,
    "Type": "HTTPS",
    "ResourcePath": "/health",
    "RequestInterval": 30,
    "FailureThreshold": 3,
    "EnableSNI": true
  }'
# FailureThreshold=3 means: 3 consecutive failures = mark unhealthy
# Cost: $0.50/month per health check (non-AWS endpoints: $0.75/month)

Once the health check detects failure, Route 53 failover routingA Route 53 routing policy where DNS queries are sent to a primary resource when it's healthy, and automatically redirected to a secondary resource when the primary fails its health check. Failover happens at the DNS level — no load balancer needed. automatically redirects traffic to your backup endpoint. Users see a brief DNS propagation delay (usually 60–90 seconds), then they're hitting the healthy backup.

Kubernetes uses two types of probes to decide if your container is alive and ready to receive traffic:

deployment.yaml — readiness + liveness probes 
apiVersion: apps/v1
kind: Deployment
metadata:
  name: my-api
spec:
  replicas: 3
  template:
    spec:
      containers:
      - name: api
        image: my-api:latest
        ports:
        - containerPort: 8080
        # Liveness: "Is the process alive?" If not, Kubernetes restarts the container.
        livenessProbe:
          httpGet:
            path: /health/live
            port: 8080
          initialDelaySeconds: 10   # wait 10s after startup before checking
          periodSeconds: 15          # check every 15 seconds
          failureThreshold: 3        # 3 failures = restart container
        # Readiness: "Can it handle traffic?" If not, remove from load balancer.
        readinessProbe:
          httpGet:
            path: /health/ready
            port: 8080
          initialDelaySeconds: 5
          periodSeconds: 10
          failureThreshold: 2        # 2 failures = stop sending traffic

The liveness probe answers "is the process alive?" — if it fails, Kubernetes kills and restarts the container. The readiness probe answers "can it handle traffic right now?" — if it fails, the pod is removed from the Service's endpoint list so no new requests are routed to it. This is how Kubernetes achieves automatic failover without any human intervention.

HAProxy is one of the most widely deployed load balancers (used by GitHub, Stack Overflow, Reddit). Its health check configuration is straightforward:

haproxy.cfg — backend health checks 
backend api_servers
    balance roundrobin
    option httpchk GET /health          # Which endpoint to check
    http-check expect status 200        # What "healthy" looks like

    # Three backend servers — HAProxy checks each every 5 seconds
    server api1 10.0.1.10:8080 check inter 5s fall 3 rise 2
    server api2 10.0.1.11:8080 check inter 5s fall 3 rise 2
    server api3 10.0.1.12:8080 check inter 5s fall 3 rise 2
    # inter 5s  = check every 5 seconds
    # fall 3    = 3 consecutive failures = mark DOWN (stop sending traffic)
    # rise 2    = 2 consecutive successes = mark UP (resume sending traffic)

When api2 fails 3 consecutive health checks, HAProxy immediately stops sending it traffic. The remaining two servers absorb the load. When api2 recovers and passes 2 checks in a row, HAProxy adds it back to the pool. Total time to remove a failed server: 15 seconds (3 checks × 5 second interval). No human involved.

Notice the pattern across all three tools: check a health endpoint at regular intervals, mark the target as unhealthy after N consecutive failures, and automatically reroute traffic. The details differ — DNS-level (Route 53), container-level (Kubernetes), or load-balancer-level (HAProxy) — but the core mechanism is identical. This is the automation that replaces the "human wakes up at 3 AM" step from Section 3.

The breakthrough in one sentence: Stop trying to prevent failures — they’re inevitable. Instead, make sure every component has a backup that takes over automatically in seconds.
Think First

If one server has 99.9% availability (8.7 hours downtime/year), and you add a second identical server as a backup, what’s the combined availability?

Both servers have to be down simultaneously for an outage. Probability both are down = 0.001 × 0.001 = 0.000001. So availability = 1 - 0.000001 = 99.9999%. You went from 8.7 hours of downtime to 32 seconds — just by adding one spare server. That’s the power of redundancy.
Section 6

How It Works — The Availability Toolkit

Redundancy is the core idea, but there’s a whole toolkit of techniques that work together to keep your system available. Here are six tools every highly available system uses. Think of this as your "uptime toolbox" — each tool solves a different piece of the availability puzzle.

Tool 1: The Nines — Measuring Availability

Think First

Your system had 3 outages last month: 12 minutes, 8 minutes, and 2 minutes. The month had 30 days. What’s the availability percentage? Is it three nines (99.9%) or four nines (99.99%)?

Hint: Total downtime = 22 minutes. Total minutes in 30 days = 43,200. Availability = (43,200 − 22) / 43,200 = 99.949%. That’s between three and four nines — closer to 99.95%. You missed four nines by 21 minutes.

Before you can improve availability, you need to measure it. Engineers use a simple formula: take the total time your system was supposed to be running, subtract the time it was actually down, and divide by the total time. The result is a percentage — your availability number.

Availability Formula
Availability = (Total Time − Downtime) / Total Time × 100%
Example: 8,760 hours in a year. 8.76 hours of downtime. Availability = (8760 − 8.76) / 8760 = 99.9%

These percentages are grouped into "nines" — and the difference between each level is dramatic:

Level Availability Downtime/Year Downtime/Month Real-World Example
Two nines 99% 3.65 days 7.3 hours Internal dashboards
Three nines 99.9% 8.7 hours 43.8 min SaaS products, APIs
Three-and-a-half 99.95% 4.4 hours 21.9 min E-commerce sites
Four nines 99.99% 52.6 min 4.4 min Payment systems
Five nines 99.999% 5.3 min 26.3 sec Emergency services, telecom
Interview tip: When a system design interviewer says "design for high availability," they usually mean 99.99% (four nines) unless specified otherwise. That means your system can only be down for about 4 minutes per month. Keep that number in your head.

Tool 2: SLA, SLO, SLI — Making Promises You Can Keep

The nines give you a way to measure availability. But how do you turn that number into a promise to your customers? That’s where three related terms come in, and they’re easy to mix up — so let’s use a restaurant analogy to keep them straight.

Imagine you open a restaurant. You tell customers: "Your food will arrive within 15 minutes, or the meal is free." That public promise is your SLAService Level Agreement — a formal contract with customers that defines the minimum acceptable level of service. If you miss the SLA, there are consequences (refunds, credits, penalties). (Service Level Agreement) — the formal contract with consequences if you break it.

Internally, you tell your kitchen staff: "Aim for 10 minutes per dish." That internal target — tighter than the public promise — is your SLOService Level Objective — an internal target that’s stricter than the SLA. You aim for the SLO so you have a safety buffer before breaking the SLA. If your SLA promises 99.9%, your SLO might target 99.95%. (Service Level Objective). It’s stricter than the SLA to give you a safety buffer.

And the actual stopwatch measuring how long each dish takes? That’s your SLIService Level Indicator — the actual measurement. It’s the raw data (response time, error rate, uptime percentage) that tells you whether you’re meeting your SLO and SLA. (Service Level Indicator) — the real measurement from your monitoring system.

The relationship is: SLI measures reality. SLO sets the internal bar (tighter). SLA sets the public promise (looser, with penalties). You aim for the SLO so you have a cushion before the SLA gets violated. If your SLA promises 99.9%, your SLO might target 99.95%, giving you a buffer for bad months.

Tool 3: Active-Passive Failover

The simplest form of redundancy is active-passive failoverA setup where one server (active) handles all traffic while a second server (passive) sits idle as a hot standby. If the active server dies, the passive one takes over. Simple and reliable, but the standby server is "wasted" money during normal operation.. One server does all the work (the "active" or "primary"). A second server sits idle, doing nothing, just waiting (the "passive" or "standby"). If the active server dies, the passive one takes over. Like having a backup goalkeeper sitting on the bench — they don’t play unless the starter gets injured.

Pros: Simple to set up and reason about. Only one server handles writes, so you don’t get data conflicts. Works great for databases.

Cons: The passive server is expensive — you’re paying for a machine that does nothing 99.99% of the time. Failover isn’t instant (5–30 seconds of gap). And you still have a brief period where both servers might think they’re the primary (the dreaded "split-brain" problem).

Tool 4: Active-Active — Everyone Works

Why let a perfectly good server sit idle? In active-activeA setup where ALL servers handle traffic simultaneously. If one dies, the others absorb its share. No wasted capacity, but you need to handle data conflicts when multiple servers accept writes for the same data. mode, all servers handle traffic all the time. A load balancer distributes requests evenly across them. If one server dies, the load balancer stops sending it traffic and the remaining servers absorb the extra load.

Pros: No wasted capacity — every server earns its keep. Failover is nearly instant (just stop sending traffic to the dead one). Scales naturally by adding more servers.

Cons: More complex when dealing with stateful data. If two servers accept conflicting writes to the same record, you need a conflict resolution strategy. Works best for stateless services (APIs, web servers) where any server can handle any request.

Tool 5: Health Checks & Heartbeats

Redundancy and failover are useless if your system doesn’t know something is broken. That’s where health checks come in. A health checkA simple endpoint (usually /health or /healthz) that returns "OK" if the service is working. Load balancers and monitoring tools ping this endpoint every few seconds. If it stops responding, traffic is redirected to healthy servers. is a tiny question your system asks each server every few seconds: "Are you alive? Are you healthy?" If the server doesn’t respond (or responds with "I’m not OK"), it gets pulled out of the rotation.

There are two flavors:

  • Active health checks (pull model): The load balancer pings each server every 5–10 seconds. "Hey Server B, are you OK?" If Server B doesn’t reply 3 times in a row, it’s marked as dead.
  • Heartbeats (push model): Each server sends a "I’m alive" signal to a central monitor every few seconds. If the monitor stops receiving heartbeats from Server B, it’s marked as dead.

Real health checks go deeper than just "is the process running?" A good health check verifies that the server can actually do its job — connect to the database, read from the cache, write to disk. A server that’s technically running but can’t reach the database is effectively dead.

Common gotcha: Don’t make health checks too expensive. If your health check runs a complex database query every 5 seconds across 100 servers, that’s 1,200 extra queries per minute just for health checking. Keep it lightweight — check connectivity, not business logic.

Tool 6: Zero-Downtime Deployments

Remember that 70% of outages come from software deployments? Zero-downtime deployments solve that. The idea is: never take down all your servers at once. Instead, update them gradually so there’s always a healthy version running.

There are three main strategies, and they’re all about managing risk:

Rolling deployment: Update servers one at a time. Take Server 1 out of the pool, deploy the new version, put it back, then do Server 2, and so on. At any point, most of your servers are still running the old version and handling traffic normally.

Risk: For a brief period, some servers run the old version and some run the new version. If the two versions aren’t compatible (e.g., different database schemas), users might get inconsistent behavior.

Blue-green deployment: You have two identical environments — "blue" (current) and "green" (new). Deploy the new version to green. Test it. When you’re confident, flip all traffic from blue to green in one switch. If something’s wrong, flip back to blue instantly.

Pro: Instant rollback — just point traffic back to blue. Con: You need double the infrastructure (two full environments), which is expensive.

Canary deployment: Named after the "canary in a coal mine." Roll out the new version to just 1–5% of your servers. Watch error rates, latency, and user complaints closely. If everything looks good after 30 minutes, gradually increase to 10%, 25%, 50%, then 100%. If anything looks wrong, roll back just that 1%.

This is the gold standard used by Netflix, Google, and Amazon. It gives you real-world validation with minimal blast radius. If the new code has a bug, only 1% of users are affected, not 100%.

Section 7

Going Deeper — The Math Behind Availability

You know the tools now. But when an interviewer asks "what’s the availability of this design?" you need to crunch actual numbers. This section covers the math that separates hand-wavy answers from precise, impressive ones.

Series Availability — A Chain Is Only as Strong as Its Weakest Link

When components are in series — meaning a request has to pass through ALL of them to succeed — the total availability is the product of each component’s availability. If any one component is down, the whole request fails.

Think of it like a chain of Christmas lights wired in series. If any single bulb burns out, the entire string goes dark.

Series Availability
Atotal = A1 × A2 × A3 × ... × An
Total availability can only go DOWN as you add more components in series.
Example: Web Server → App Server → Database
Web: 99.99% × App: 99.99% × DB: 99.99% = 0.9999 × 0.9999 × 0.9999 = 0.9997 = 99.97%
Three "four nines" components in series only give you 99.97% — that’s 2.6 hours of downtime per year instead of 52 minutes.
Parallel Availability — Redundancy to the Rescue

When components are in parallel — meaning the request succeeds if any one of them works — availability goes UP. Both components have to be down simultaneously for an outage. This is the math behind redundancy.

Parallel Availability
Atotal = 1 − (1 − A1) × (1 − A2)
Read as: "both have to fail simultaneously." The more parallel copies, the higher the availability.
Example: Two servers, each 99.9%
Atotal = 1 − (1 − 0.999) × (1 − 0.999) = 1 − (0.001 × 0.001) = 1 − 0.000001 = 99.9999% (six nines!)
Two "three nines" servers in parallel give you six nines — from 8.7 hours of downtime to 32 seconds.
Error Budgets — Spending Your Downtime Wisely

Here’s a question most teams struggle with: you have a 99.9% SLO, and you haven’t had any outages this quarter. Should you celebrate? Or should you ship faster, since you have "room" for some failures?

Google popularized the concept of an error budgetThe maximum amount of downtime (or errors) your SLO allows in a given period. If your SLO is 99.9% and the month has 43,200 minutes, your error budget is 43.2 minutes. You can "spend" this budget on risky deployments, experiments, or migrations. to answer exactly this. Your error budget is the flip side of your SLO — it’s the allowed amount of unreliability.

Error Budget Calculation
SLO: 99.9% → Error budget = 100% − 99.9% = 0.1% Monthly minutes: 30 days × 24 hrs × 60 min = 43,200 min Error budget = 43,200 × 0.001 = 43.2 minutes/month
You can "spend" 43 minutes of downtime per month on deployments, experiments, and maintenance.

The brilliance of error budgets is that they create a shared language between engineering and product teams. When the error budget is healthy (plenty of remaining budget), the team ships aggressively — more features, more experiments, faster deployments. When the budget is nearly exhausted, the team freezes new features and focuses on stability.

It turns the endless argument of "should we ship faster or be more careful?" into a data-driven decision: check the error budget.

Real-world practice: At Google, if a service has burned through its error budget for the month, feature deployments are frozen and the team must focus on reliability improvements until the budget resets. This makes reliability everyone’s problem, not just the ops team’s.
The Cost of Each Additional Nine

Each nine you add doesn’t just cost 10× more engineering effort — it costs exponentially more money. Going from 99% to 99.9% might mean adding a load balancer and a second server. Going from 99.9% to 99.99% might mean multi-region deployment, automatic failover, and a full-time SRE team. Going from 99.99% to 99.999% might mean custom hardware, globally distributed databases, and a team of 50 reliability engineers.

The key insight for interviews and real life: don’t chase nines you don’t need. An internal admin tool doesn’t need five nines. A blog doesn’t need four nines. But a payment system handling millions of dollars per hour? That absolutely needs four or five nines, and the cost is justified.

The right question is always: "what’s the cost of downtime vs. the cost of preventing it?" If an hour of downtime costs you $500K and achieving the next nine costs $200K/year, that’s an obvious investment. If downtime costs you $500 and the next nine costs $200K, you’re wasting money.

Think First

Your system has a web server (99.99%), an API gateway (99.95%), and a database (99.99%) — all in series. What’s the total system availability?

0.9999 × 0.9995 × 0.9999 = 0.9993 = 99.93%. The API gateway at 99.95% is the bottleneck — it dragged the whole system below four nines. Your system is only as available as its weakest link in the chain.
Section 8

Variations — Availability Isn’t One-Size-Fits-All

Slack reported 99.99% uptime in 2023 — but users in Australia saw 45 minutes of downtime that US users never experienced. The global number looked great; the regional reality was different. That’s because "available" has multiple meanings, and the one you measure determines whether your users agree with your status page.

Regional Availability

Your system might be perfectly healthy in the US while completely down in Europe — because the European data center lost power, or a submarine cable was cut, or a cloud region had an outage. Regional availability measures uptime from the perspective of users in a specific geography.

This matters because multi-region deploymentRunning your system in two or more geographically separated data centers (e.g., US-East and EU-West). If one region goes down, traffic is routed to the other. This protects against datacenter-level failures and also reduces latency for users in each region. is one of the most powerful (and expensive) availability strategies. If you run in two regions and one goes down, users in that region get routed to the other. It’s like having two restaurants in different neighborhoods — if one has a fire, customers can go to the other.

Cloud provider guarantees are regional. When AWS promises 99.99% uptime for EC2, that’s per-region. A whole-region outage (like US-East-1 going down in December 2021) is rare but not impossible. Multi-region is how you protect against it.

Service-Level Availability

In a microservicesAn architecture where your application is split into many small, independent services (user service, payment service, notification service, etc.). Each can be deployed, scaled, and fail independently. architecture, different services can have different availability targets. Your payment service probably needs 99.99% because money is involved. Your recommendation engine? Maybe 99.9% is fine — if recommendations are down for a few minutes, users can still browse and buy.

This is called tiered availability. You invest the most redundancy and engineering effort into the services on the critical path (auth, payments, core data), and accept lower availability for non-critical services (analytics, notifications, recommendations). This is much more cost-effective than trying to make everything five nines.

The trick is knowing which services are on the critical path. A good test: "if this service goes down for 5 minutes, can users still complete their primary task?" If yes, it’s non-critical. If no, it’s on the critical path and needs higher availability.

Data Availability

Service availability means "can users reach the app?" Data availability is different — it means "can users access their data?" Your API might respond perfectly, but if the database is down and all responses are "sorry, can’t load your data right now," the service is technically available but practically useless.

Data availability adds another dimension: durability. It’s not just "can I read the data right now?" but "is the data still there?" A system with high availability but low durability might be up 99.99% of the time but occasionally lose data permanently. Amazon S3 famously promises 99.999999999% durability (eleven nines!) — meaning if you store 10 million objects, you might lose one every 10,000 years.

Availability ≠ Durability. A database replica can make data available (readable from two places) but not more durable unless the replica is in a different physical location. If both replicas are on the same rack and the rack catches fire, high availability didn’t save your data.

Network Availability

Your servers might be running perfectly, but if users can’t reach them, the system is effectively down. Network availability measures whether the path between users and your system is working — DNS resolution, internet routing, load balancer connectivity, CDN edge nodes, and everything in between.

Network issues are especially tricky because they’re often partial. Users in Japan might have perfect connectivity while users in Brazil experience 50% packet loss due to a routing issue at an intermediate ISP. Your monitoring in the US shows everything green, but half your Brazilian users are seeing timeout errors.

Defenses include: multiple DNS providers (if one goes down, the other resolves your domain), CDNs that serve cached content from edge nodes (users get content even if your origin server is unreachable), and anycastA networking technique where the same IP address is announced from multiple locations. Users are automatically routed to the nearest one. If that location goes down, traffic automatically flows to the next nearest. Most CDNs and DNS providers use anycast. routing that automatically directs users to the nearest healthy endpoint.

Section 9

At Scale — How the Giants Do It

Think First

Netflix deliberately kills production servers during business hours (Chaos Monkey). Google runs “DiRT” disaster recovery tests that take down entire data centers. Why would a company intentionally cause outages? Doesn’t that hurt their availability numbers?

Hint: If you only discover failures during real emergencies, you fix them under maximum stress with maximum stakes. Controlled failure during business hours means you find weaknesses while the full team is awake, alert, and ready to learn. The short-term downtime from testing prevents the long-term catastrophic outage you never saw coming.

Theory is nice, but how do the biggest companies on Earth actually keep their systems alive? Each one has scars, lessons, and surprisingly different philosophies about what "available" means. Let’s walk through four real stories.

AWS — The Nines Factory

Amazon Web Services publishes specific SLAService Level Agreement — a contract promising a minimum level of availability, with financial penalties if broken numbers for every service. S3 promises 99.99% availability. EC2 in a single Availability Zone gets 99.5%, but spread across multiple AZs and you jump to 99.99%.

The secret sauce is blast-radius isolation. Each Availability Zone is a physically separate data center with independent power and networking. A fire in one AZ doesn't touch the others. AWS further groups AZs into Regions, so even a natural disaster affecting an entire metro area can be survived if you replicate across regions.

Key lesson: AWS doesn't try to make one data center invincible — they assume every single component will fail and build so that failures stay contained. The wall between AZs is the real product.

Google — Error Budgets Changed Everything

Google's SRE team invented the concept of the error budgetThe allowed amount of downtime or errors before a team must stop shipping new features and focus on reliability. The idea is beautifully simple: if your SLOService Level Objective — an internal reliability target, usually stricter than the customer-facing SLA is 99.95% uptime, that means you're "allowed" about 22 minutes of downtime per month. That's your budget.

Spend it all on a bad deploy? Fine — but no more feature launches until the budget resets. This creates a natural tension between shipping fast and staying reliable. Product teams stop arguing with SRE teams because the math is clear.

Stripe — Five Nines for Payments

When real money is flowing through your system, "mostly available" isn't good enough. Stripe targets 99.999% for their payment processing API — that's about 26 seconds of downtime per month.

How do they pull it off? Three big strategies: idempotency keys on every payment request (so retries don't double-charge anyone), a multi-datacenter mesh that routes around failures automatically, and graceful degradation — if a non-critical internal service is down, payments still go through, you just might lose analytics for a few minutes.

Key lesson: Stripe prioritizes the critical path ruthlessly. Not every internal system needs five nines — just the one that moves money.

CockroachDB — Survive a Region Going Dark

CockroachDB is a distributed SQL database designed to survive the loss of an entire cloud region without any downtime. It uses the Raft consensus protocol — data is replicated across at least 3 nodes, and as long as a majority (2 out of 3) are alive, reads and writes continue normally.

The clever part is automatic rebalancing. When a node dies, CockroachDB immediately starts copying data to healthy nodes so you're back to full replication. No human needs to intervene. If an entire region disappears (say, US-East goes down), the remaining regions in US-Central and US-West keep serving traffic. When US-East comes back, data re-syncs automatically.

This is the database equivalent of a self-healing system — it doesn't just tolerate failure, it actively repairs itself.

Section 10

Anti-Lessons — What NOT to Do

Sometimes the best teacher is a cautionary tale. These are real traps that teams fall into when they think about availability — mistakes that waste money, create false confidence, or both.

Chasing 99.999% When 99.9% Is Enough

Going from three nines (99.9%) to five nines (99.999%) sounds like a small jump — just two more digits, right? In reality, it's a 100x reduction in allowed downtime: from 43 minutes per month to 26 seconds.

That means you need automated failoverAutomatically switching traffic to a healthy backup system when the primary one fails — no human action required that completes in seconds, multi-region replication, zero-downtime deployments, and a team on-call 24/7. The engineering cost is enormous. For an internal dashboard or a content blog, 99.9% is plenty.

The trap: Teams set ambitious availability targets to impress leadership without calculating the cost. A single 9 can double your infrastructure bill. Always ask: "What happens to my business if we're down for 40 minutes this month?" If the answer is "not much," you don't need five nines.
Gold Plating — Redundancy That Backfires

"Let's add another replica! And a hot spare! And a load balancer in front of the load balancer!" More redundancy sounds safer, but every extra component is another thing that can fail, another thing that needs monitoring, and another thing that can have subtle configuration drift.

There's a real-world pattern where teams add so many layers of redundancy that the system becomes impossible to reason about. When something breaks, no one knows which failover path actually fired, and debugging takes longer than the outage would have lasted with a simpler setup.

The trap: Complexity is the enemy of availability. A system you can't understand is a system you can't fix quickly. Keep your architecture as simple as your availability target allows.
Ignoring Planned Downtime in SLA Calculations

Some teams proudly report "zero unplanned downtime" — but conveniently exclude the 2-hour maintenance window every Sunday night. If your SLAService Level Agreement — a contract promising a minimum level of availability, with financial penalties if broken says 99.9%, that's total availability, including planned maintenance — unless you explicitly exclude maintenance windows in the contract.

Customers don't care whether the outage was planned or unplanned. If they can't use your service, you're down. The honest move is to either include planned downtime in your calculations or use zero-downtime deployment strategies (rolling deploys, blue-green deploymentRunning two identical production environments; deploy to the idle one, test it, then switch traffic over — instant rollback if anything goes wrong) so maintenance windows become unnecessary.

The trap: Excluding planned downtime from SLA math gives you a false sense of reliability. If you need a maintenance window, it means your deployment process isn't mature enough for your availability target.
Section 11

Common Mistakes

These are the mistakes that show up again and again in real teams. Each one seems reasonable on the surface but leads to painful surprises when an outage hits.

Measuring Only Uptime, Not Correctness

Your server is up, responding to every request with 200 OK — but it's returning stale data from a broken cache. Is that "available"? Technically yes. Practically no.

True availability means the system is up AND correct. Your SLIService Level Indicator — a measurable metric (like latency, error rate, or throughput) that tells you how well a service is performing should include correctness checks, not just "is the process running." Monitor response content, not just response codes.

Fix: Add synthetic probes that verify response content — not just that the server responded, but that the response was correct and timely.
Forgetting Dependent Service Availability

Your service is 99.9% available. Your database is 99.9%. Your payment provider is 99.9%. So your end-to-end availability is... 99.7%. Each dependency multiplies, not adds.

If you have three services in series, each at 99.9%, the combined availability is 0.999 × 0.999 × 0.999 = 0.997 — that's 99.7%, which means over 2 hours of downtime per month instead of the 43 minutes you expected.

Fix: Map every critical-path dependency and multiply their availability numbers. Use circuit breakers and fallbacks to decouple from non-critical dependencies.
Not Testing Failover Regularly

You set up a beautiful failover system — secondary database, backup load balancer, standby region. Then you never test it. Six months later, a real outage happens and the failover doesn't work because the secondary database drifted out of sync three months ago and nobody noticed.

Failover that hasn't been tested is failover that doesn't exist. Netflix famously runs Chaos Monkey to kill random production instances — not because they enjoy chaos, but because untested redundancy is just expensive decoration.

Fix: Schedule regular failover drills. Actually kill the primary and verify the secondary takes over. Do this monthly, not yearly.
Ignoring Maintenance Windows

"We'll just take the system down at 2 AM on Sunday — nobody uses it then." Until the day a customer in another timezone has a critical workflow running at your 2 AM, and your maintenance window costs them real money.

For global services, there is no "quiet time." If your system has users across timezones, every hour is peak hour for someone. Maintenance windows are a crutch for systems that can't do zero-downtime deployments.

Fix: Invest in rolling deployments and blue-green strategies. Eliminate the need for maintenance windows entirely. If you must have them, factor the downtime into your SLA honestly.
Alert Fatigue — Too Many Alerts, No Action

The monitoring dashboard has 200 alerts configured. The on-call engineer gets paged 15 times a night. After a week, they start ignoring alerts — and the one that mattered gets lost in the noise.

Alert fatigue is one of the top causes of extended outages. When everything is critical, nothing is. Google's SRE handbook recommends that every alert should be actionable, urgent, and real. If an alert fires and the correct response is "ignore it," that alert shouldn't exist.

Fix: Ruthlessly prune alerts. Every alert must have a clear runbook action. Aim for fewer than 2 pages per on-call shift. Track alert-to-action ratio.
Having an SLA Without an SLO

You promised customers 99.9% in your SLA contract. But internally, your team has no SLO — no target, no error budget, no tracking. You'll only know you've violated the SLA after a customer complains and you calculate the numbers retroactively.

An SLA without an SLO is a promise without a plan. The SLO should be stricter than the SLA — if your SLA is 99.9%, your internal SLO should be 99.95%. That buffer gives you warning before you breach the contract.

Fix: Define SLOs before SLAs. Set the SLO tighter than the SLA. Track the error budget weekly and create escalation policies when the budget is running low.
Section 12

Interview Playbook

Availability questions are everywhere in system design interviews. Here's a framework for answering them confidently, no matter the difficulty level.

Typical question: "What does high availability mean? How would you make a web app highly available?"

What they're testing: Do you understand basic redundancy and why single points of failure are bad?

Sample answer: "High availability means the system stays usable even when some parts fail. For a web app, I'd start with at least two app servers behind a load balancer, so if one crashes, the other handles traffic. The database gets a primary-replica setup — the replica can be promoted if the primary dies. I'd also deploy across two availability zones so a data center outage doesn't take us offline."

Typical question: "Design a system that needs 99.99% availability. What are the trade-offs?"

What they're testing: Can you reason about nines quantitatively and make cost/benefit decisions?

Sample answer: "99.99% means about 4.3 minutes of downtime per month. Human response is too slow for that, so I need automated failover — health checks detect failures within seconds, and traffic reroutes automatically. I'd use multi-AZ deployment with at least 3 replicas. The trade-off is cost: cross-AZ data transfer isn't free, and maintaining hot standbys doubles infrastructure spend. I'd also set an internal SLO at 99.995% so we have a buffer before breaching the SLA."

Typical question: "How would you handle availability for a globally distributed payment system?"

What they're testing: Can you navigate CAP theorem trade-offs, multi-region architectures, and correctness under partition?

Sample answer: "For payments, correctness is non-negotiable — I'd rather reject a transaction than process it twice. So I'd use synchronous replication within a region for strong consistency, and async replication across regions for disaster recovery. Each region runs independently with its own database leader. Idempotency keys prevent double-charges on retries. I'd define the RTO as under 30 seconds and RPO as zero for committed transactions. The monitoring layer tracks SLIs per region — latency P99, error rate, and throughput — and we'd set error budgets per quarter."

Practice Exercises

Time to test your understanding. Try each exercise before opening the hints and solutions.

Calculate Composite Availability Beginner

A service depends on three components in series: an API gateway (99.95%), an app server (99.9%), and a database (99.95%). Calculate the overall availability. Then calculate it if you add a second app server in parallel.

Hint

Series: multiply all availabilities. Parallel: 1 − (1 − A₁)(1 − A₂).

Solution

Series (single app server): 0.9995 × 0.999 × 0.9995 = 0.998 → 99.8% (~87 min/month downtime)

Two app servers in parallel: Parallel availability = 1 − (1 − 0.999)² = 1 − 0.000001 = 0.999999

New composite: 0.9995 × 0.999999 × 0.9995 = 0.999 → 99.9% (~43 min/month). The parallel pair boosted the weakest link.

Error Budget Scenario Intermediate

Your team's SLO is 99.95% for the quarter. It's the end of month 2 and you've already used 18 minutes of your error budget. A product manager wants to ship a risky database migration. Should you approve it? Calculate the remaining budget and defend your decision.

Hint

Quarterly budget at 99.95% = 0.05% of total minutes in 90 days. Calculate total, subtract 18, see what's left for month 3.

Solution

Total quarterly budget: 90 days × 24h × 60min × 0.0005 = 64.8 minutes

Remaining: 64.8 − 18 = 46.8 minutes for the final month

Decision: You have 46.8 minutes left — that's reasonable, but a risky migration could easily eat 30+ minutes if something goes wrong. Recommend: proceed only if there's a tested rollback plan that completes in under 10 minutes. Otherwise, push to next quarter when the budget resets.

Design a Health Check System Intermediate

Design a health check endpoint for a microservice that depends on a database, a Redis cache, and an external payment API. Define what "healthy," "degraded," and "unhealthy" mean for this service. What should the health check response look like?

Hint

Think about which dependencies are critical (must be up) vs. nice-to-have (can work without). A degraded state means the service works but with reduced functionality.

Solution

Healthy: DB up + Redis up + Payment API responsive = all green, 200 OK

Degraded: DB up + Redis down (fall back to DB queries, slower but functional) = 200 OK with warning flag

Unhealthy: DB down = 503 Service Unavailable (can't serve any meaningful request)

The payment API being down might be "degraded" rather than "unhealthy" — users can still browse, just not pay.

RTO / RPO Trade-off Analysis Advanced

A fintech startup asks you to design their disaster recovery plan. They have two requirements: RTORecovery Time Objective — the maximum acceptable time to restore service after a disaster under 5 minutes and RPORecovery Point Objective — the maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time (e.g., RPO of 1 hour means you can lose up to 1 hour of data) of zero (no data loss). Propose an architecture and estimate monthly cost impact.

Hint

RPO of zero requires synchronous replication. RTO under 5 min requires hot standby, not cold. Think about what this means for your database, app servers, and DNS.

Solution

Architecture:

  • Database: Synchronous replication to a standby in a second AZ (RPO = 0). Use a managed service like Aurora with multi-AZ sync.
  • App servers: Hot standby cluster always running in AZ-2, receiving no traffic but ready to serve.
  • DNS/LB: Health-check-based failover with 30-second TTL so DNS switches fast.
  • Automated: No human in the loop — health checks trigger failover automatically.

Cost impact: Roughly 2x infrastructure cost (everything runs double). Synchronous replication adds ~5-15ms write latency. For a fintech handling money, this cost is justified — the alternative is losing transactions.

Your service has an SLO of 99.9% success rate over a 30-day rolling window. Design an alerting strategy that warns you when you're burning through your error budget too fast, without causing alert fatigue. Define at least three alert tiers with different burn rates and response expectations.

Hint

Think in terms of "burn rate" — how fast you're consuming your 43-minute monthly budget. A burn rate of 1x means you'll exactly exhaust it by month end. 10x means you'll exhaust it in 3 days.
