Section 1

TL;DR — The One-Minute Version

Mental Model: System design is breaking one impossible problem into ten solvable ones — then making sure each piece can stand on its own, scale on its own, and fail without killing the others.

Every system design question — "design Instagram," "design a rate limiter," "design a chat app" — follows the same five steps. The system changes, the steps don't. Here's the entire framework on one screen.

One-line takeaway: Don't try to design the whole system at once. Pick one piece, solve it, then move to the next. Baby steps beat giant leaps every single time.

The First Attempt — "I'll Just Start Drawing Boxes"

Let's watch what most candidates actually do. You're in that Meta interview. The silence feels unbearable, so you grab the marker (or open ExcalidrawA free, open-source virtual whiteboard used by engineers at Google, Meta, and Netflix. Available at excalidraw.com. A box labeled "Server." An arrow to a box labeled "Database." Another box: "Cache." Another: "Load Balancer." Maybe a "Message Queue" because you read about Kafka last week. Within 3 minutes, you have a diagram with 6 boxes and 8 arrows. It looks... impressive? Technical? Like a real architecture?

Then the interviewer asks a single question:

"Why did you choose a message queue here?"

And you can't answer. Because you drew it before you understood the problem. You don't know if any operation in this system needs to be asynchronous. You don't know the traffic patterns. You don't know the read/write ratio. You drew a message queue because architecture diagrams on Medium articles have message queues, not because this system needs one.

Look at the diagram above. The candidate drew 6 boxes and 8 arrows without asking a single question. Can you spot what's wrong? Hint: What problem is the "Server" box solving? What data does the "Database" store? What goes through the "Message Queue"?

This is the most common mistake in system design: drawing before thinking. It feels productive — look, I'm making an architecture diagram! — but it's actually the fastest way to fail. You're making technology decisions before you understand what problem those technologies need to solve.

Section 4

Where It Breaks — Why "Just Draw Boxes" Fails

Drawing boxes without understanding the problem leads to four predictable failures. Let's look at each one with a real example, because understanding WHY the naive approach fails is what makes the framework in Section 5 click.

Failure 1: Wrong Scope

The interviewer said "Design Instagram," but what they actually wanted was just the news feed. The candidate spent 20 minutes drawing boxes for uploads, stories, DMs, search, and notifications — and ran out of time before going deep on anything. The interviewer's feedback: "Covered everything at 10,000 feet, went deep on nothing. I couldn't evaluate their technical depth."

This is the scoping failure. It happens because the candidate never asked: "Which part of Instagram should I focus on?" That single question would have saved 20 minutes.

Failure 2: Wrong Technology Choices

A candidate designing a simple URL shortener puts Apache KafkaA distributed event streaming platform designed for high-throughput, real-time data pipelines. It can handle millions of messages per second. Massive overkill for a URL shortener that does 200 writes/second — a simple database insert handles that fine. in their diagram for "handling URL creation events." The interviewer asks: "What throughput do you expect?" The candidate does the math: 200 writes per second. Kafka handles millions per second. It's like renting a Boeing 747 to fly from your bedroom to your kitchen.

The root cause: Technology choices made BEFORE doing the math are almost always wrong. You don't know if you need Kafka until you know you're processing millions of events per second. You don't know if you need Redis until you know your read-to-write ratio. Numbers first, tools second.

Failure 3: No Justification

The dreaded follow-up question chain:

Interviewer: "Why NoSQL here?"
Candidate: "Because it scales."
Interviewer: "How do you know you need to scale?"
Candidate: "..."
Interviewer: "What's your expected data volume?"
Candidate: "..."

Every technology choice needs a reason rooted in the problem's constraints. "Because it scales" is not a reason — it's a hope. "Because we have 500M URLs and a 50:1 read ratio, which makes cache lookups critical, so Redis" — that's a reason. Amazon's evaluation rubric explicitly checks: "Did they justify each decision with data?"

Failure 4: The $100K Kubernetes Story

This is a real story from Arpit BhayaniA software engineer and educator known for teaching system design through practical, grounded examples. He previously worked at Amazon and now runs a popular system design course. His "baby steps" framework is the foundation of this page.: a startup with 500 users containerized everything in Kubernetes with auto-scaling, service mesh, distributed tracing, and a full observability stack. Their monthly AWS bill: $8,000+. Their traffic: 50 requests per minute. A single $20/month VPSVirtual Private Server — a small cloud server you can rent by the month. Popular options: DigitalOcean Droplets ($5-20/mo), Linode, Vultr. A $20 VPS typically has 2 CPU cores and 4GB RAM — more than enough for 500 users. could have handled their entire load with room to spare.

Over-engineering isn't just wasteful — it's actively harmful. More components means more failure points, more network calls, more deployment complexity, more things to debug at 3 AM. The startup spent 6 months building infrastructure instead of features. By the time they shipped, a competitor with a simple monolith had captured the market.

Look at the four failures above: wrong scope, wrong tech, no justification, over-engineering. They all share the same root cause. Can you spot what it is?

The root cause of all four failures is the same: they skipped understanding the problem. They didn't ask how many users, didn't calculate the traffic, didn't clarify the scope, didn't do the math. They jumped straight to drawing architecture diagrams — and every subsequent decision was built on guesses instead of data.

Key insight: The failures aren't caused by a lack of knowledge. They're caused by a lack of process. A candidate who knows every database in existence but skips Step 1 will still design the wrong system. A candidate who knows only PostgreSQL but follows the framework will design a system that actually works. Process beats knowledge every time.
Section 5

The Breakthrough — Arpit's Baby Steps Framework

Now you've seen what happens without a framework: wrong scope, wrong tools, no justification, over-engineering. All from skipping the first step. So what does a GOOD approach look like?

Arpit Bhayani's framework gives you five steps. They work for any system — Instagram, a rate limiter, a chat app, a payment gateway, anything. The system changes, the steps don't. Let's walk through all five, then apply them to a real URL shortener design so you can see exactly what to say, what to draw, and what the interviewer is evaluating at each step.

Notice the loop-back arrow from Step 5 to Step 3. System design is not a straight line — it's a spiralAn iterative process where you revisit earlier decisions with new understanding. Each pass through the cycle adds more detail and refinement. Unlike a linear process, you're expected to go back and improve.. You go broad first (Steps 1-2), then deep (Steps 3-4), and if you find something that's still too vague, you zoom in further and repeat.

Walkthrough: Designing a URL Shortener (Step by Step)

Let's apply all five steps to a real problem so you can see exactly what to say at each step, what to draw, and what the interviewer is evaluating. We'll design a URL shortener like BitlyA popular URL shortening service. You give it a long URL like "https://example.com/very/long/path?with=parameters" and it gives you a short one like "bit.ly/abc123". When someone clicks the short URL, Bitly redirects them to the original long URL..

Before reading this walkthrough, try it yourself. Set a 15-minute timer (use your phone — seriously, time it). Open excalidraw.com or grab paper. Design a URL shortener using the 5 steps. Then compare your approach to this one.

You say to the interviewer: "Before I start designing, let me understand the requirements." This one sentence sets the right tone for the entire interview. It tells the interviewer you're structured, thoughtful, and not impulsive.

Then you ask questions and learn:

  • 100M monthly active users — this tells you the system must handle real scale, not a toy project. It shapes every infrastructure choice.
  • 500M new URLs created per month — that's the write volume. You need this number to calculate writes per second and storage growth.
  • Each URL clicked on average 50 times over its lifetime — this is the read multiplier. It reveals the system is read-heavy, which means caching matters far more than write optimization.
  • Short URLs should be 6-8 characters — this constrains your encoding strategy. Base62 with 7 characters gives 62^7 = 3.5 trillion unique codes, far more than you'll ever need.
  • URLs never expire — this means storage grows forever. You need to calculate yearly growth to know when you'll need to shard or archive.
  • 99.9% availability (about 8.7 hours of downtime per year max) — this tells you redundancy is required. A single server won't cut it because any maintenance window would blow your budget.

Notice: every requirement directly shapes a design decision. That's WHY you gather them first — without these numbers, you'd be guessing. Now do the math — out loud, on the whiteboard. The interviewer wants to see this.

Back-of-Envelope (say this out loud) Writes: 500M/month / 30 / 86,400 = ~200 writes/sec Reads: 500M x 50 / 30 / 86,400 = ~10,000 reads/sec Read:Write ratio = 50:1 → heavily read-dominant Storage: 500M x 500 bytes avg = 250 GB/year → fits on one disk for years → Read-heavy system. Cache lookups will be critical. Write path is simple.

What the interviewer is evaluating: "Do they ask good questions? Do they drive the conversation? Do they use data to shape their design?" At Google, this is literally the top evaluation criterion: structured thinking.

Now — and only now — you start drawing. A URL shortener has two core operations: creating a short URL, and redirecting a short URL to its target. Those are two different workloads (write vs. read), so they become two components:

  • URL Creation Service — accepts a long URL, generates a short code, stores the mapping
  • Redirect Service — receives a short URL click, looks up the mapping, returns a 301 redirectAn HTTP status code meaning "Moved Permanently." When a browser receives a 301 from bit.ly/abc123, it automatically navigates to the original long URL. The browser may also cache this redirect to skip the lookup next time.

Plus the data layer: a URL Database (stores short_code to long_url mappings) and a Cache (hot lookups for popular URLs).

What the interviewer is evaluating: "Do the components have clear, non-overlapping responsibilities? Are there 4-7 pieces, not 15?" At Meta, this is the scoping check.

Pick the most interesting component — for a URL shortener, it's the Redirect Service because it handles 50x more traffic than creation. That 50:1 read ratio from Step 1 math is exactly WHY we focus here — this is where bottlenecks will appear first, and where your design decisions have the biggest impact. Walk through the data flow:

  1. User clicks bit.ly/abc123
  2. Request hits the Redirect Service
  3. Service checks the Redis cache first (0.1ms) — because Redis stores data in memory, and memory access is roughly 40x faster than disk. At 10,000 reads/sec, this speed difference is the reason your servers stay healthy instead of drowning. Cache hit? Return 301 redirect. Done.
  4. Cache miss? Query PostgreSQL (4ms) — slower because it reads from disk, but PostgreSQL is the durable source of truth that never loses data even if a server restarts. Store the result in cache so the next lookup is instant. Return 301.
  5. URL not found? Return 404.

For the Creation Service: accept the long URL, generate a unique 7-character code (base62 encoding of an auto-incrementing ID or a random hash), store the mapping in PostgreSQL, return the short URL to the user. Why base62? Because it uses letters (a-z, A-Z) and digits (0-9) — 62 URL-safe characters that need no escaping. Why PostgreSQL for writes? Because URL creation needs strong consistency — the moment you create a short URL, the very next redirect must find it. An eventually consistent store might temporarily return 404 for a URL created just seconds ago.

For each component, make four decisions — and say WHY for each one. This is where you prove you're not just name-dropping technologies.

The interviewer asks: "What happens at 10x traffic?" You don't panic. You adjust the numbers:

  • At 10x (100K reads/sec): A single Redis node tops out around 100K ops/sec, so we shard the cache across 3-4 nodes — each handles a subset of URL keys, spreading the load. Add 2 read replicas for PostgreSQL because cache misses now hit the DB 10x harder. The architecture stays the same — we scale the parts the math says need it.
  • At 100x (1M reads/sec): Even sharded Redis struggles cost-effectively at 1M reads/sec. Move popular URLs to a CDN at edge locations — CDNs serve from servers physically near users (5ms vs 50ms), offloading 80%+ of traffic before it hits your backend. Go multi-region so users in Asia don't make 200ms round trips to US-East. Switch to eventually consistent cache because strong consistency across continents adds 100ms+ per sync — too slow for a redirect that should take milliseconds.

The architecture didn't change. The numbers changed, and the scaling decisions evolved with them. This is what Netflix looks for: "Do they start simple and evolve, or jump to complex solutions?"

The 5-Step Framework in Detail — "Design a Notification System"

Section 5 gave you the framework overview with a URL shortener. Now let's go much deeper — walking through each step with a harder problem: "Design a notification system." This is a real interview question that shows up at Google, Amazon, and LinkedIn. It's messier than a URL shortener because it involves multiple delivery channels, user preferences, retry logic, and a firehose of traffic.

By the end of this section, you'll know exactly what to say, what to draw, and what the interviewer is writing on their scorecard at each step. We'll use five accordion cards — one per step — so you can focus on one step at a time.

Before reading the walkthrough: open excalidraw.com, set a 10-minute timer, and sketch how YOU would design a notification system. What components do you need? How does a notification go from "user got a new follower" to "push notification on their phone"? Sketch it, then compare to the walkthrough below.

You say: "Before I start designing, let me understand the requirements." Then you ask real questions — not generic "what are the requirements?" but targeted questions that reveal the shape of the system.

Here are the questions you'd ask for a notification system, grouped by what they unlock:

Scope Questions

  • "What channels do we need?" — Push notifications, email, SMS, in-app? All four? The interviewer says: "All four, but prioritize push and email."
  • "Who triggers notifications?" — Other users (e.g., "John liked your post"), the system itself (e.g., "Your subscription expires tomorrow"), or external services (e.g., "Your package shipped")? Answer: all three.
  • "Do users control their preferences?" — Can they mute a channel, set quiet hours, or opt out of specific notification types? Answer: yes to all.

Scale Questions

  • "How many users?" — 500 million registered, 100 million DAU.
  • "How many notifications per day?" — Average 20 per active user = 2 billion notifications/day.
  • "What latency is acceptable?" — Push: under 2 seconds. Email: under 30 seconds. SMS: under 60 seconds.

Now do the math on the whiteboard:

Back-of-Envelope (notification system) Daily: 2B notifications / 86,400 sec = ~23,000 notifications/sec average Peak (3x average): ~70,000 notifications/sec Storage: 2B x 200 bytes = 400 GB/day = 146 TB/year (if we keep history) Push payload: 2B x 4KB avg = 8 TB/day outbound bandwidth Write-heavy, async-friendly, high-throughput pipeline. Caching user preferences is critical (500M users x 100 bytes = 50 GB, fits in Redis).

What the interviewer writes: "Candidate asked targeted questions about channels, trigger sources, and user preferences. Did math unprompted. Strong Step 1."

Based on what you learned in Step 1, the notification system breaks into five components with mutually exclusiveNo overlap between components. Each component owns exactly one responsibility. Why these 5? Each component maps to one responsibility. The API validates and enqueues. The Router reads preferences and picks channels. Each Sender talks to one external provider. Pick the most interesting component. For a notification system, that's the Router Service — it's where the real logic lives. Walk the interviewer through the data flow:

  1. Router reads a notification event from Kafka: {user_id: 123, type: "new_follower", data: {follower: "Alice"}}
  2. Looks up user 123's preferences from Redis cache (cache hit: 0.1ms) or PostgreSQL (cache miss: 4ms)
  3. User 123 has push enabled, email enabled, SMS disabled, quiet hours 10PM-8AM
  4. Current time is 3PM — not quiet hours. Router creates two delivery jobs: one for push, one for email
  5. Picks the "new_follower" template from template store: "Alice just followed you!"
  6. Publishes two messages to channel-specific Kafka topics: push-notifications and email-notifications

Then do the same for the Push Sender:

  1. Reads from push-notifications topic
  2. Looks up user 123's device tokens (they have 2 devices: iPhone + Android)
  3. Sends to APNsApple Push Notification service — Apple's gateway for delivering push notifications to iPhones and iPads. You send a JSON payload to Apple's servers, and Apple delivers it to the device. Rate limit: ~100K concurrent connections. (iPhone) and FCMFirebase Cloud Messaging — Google's push notification service for Android devices (and also works for iOS and web). Free, handles millions of messages per second. (Android)

  5. If APNs returns a transient error — retry with exponential backoffA retry strategy where each retry waits longer: 1s, 2s, 4s, 8s, 16s... This prevents hammering a failing service with rapid retries, giving it time to recover. Most implementations cap at 5-10 retries with a maximum wait of 30-60 seconds. (1s, 2s, 4s, max 3 retries)

The interviewer asks: "What about rate limiting? Users are complaining about too many notifications." You zoom into the Router and split out a new sub-component:

  • Rate Limiter — sits between the Router and channel senders. Tracks "notifications sent to user X in the last hour." Rule: max 5 push notifications per hour, max 3 emails per day per user.
  • Implementation: Redis sorted set keyed by ratelimit:{user_id}:{channel}. Score = timestamp. Before sending, count entries in the last hour. Why a sorted set instead of a simple counter? Because a counter only tells you "5 notifications sent" — it can't tell you WHEN they were sent. A sorted set stores timestamps, so you can count "how many in the last 60 minutes" with a range query. O(log N) per check, which at our scale means sub-millisecond.
  • If rate limited: Drop low-priority notifications (marketing), queue and batch high-priority ones (security alerts always go through).

The interviewer then asks: "What if Kafka goes down?" You explain:

  • Kafka replication factor = 3: Every message is copied to 3 different brokers. Why 3? Because it's the minimum that lets you survive one broker dying while still having a majority (2 out of 3) agree on what data is correct. One broker dies? The other two continue serving without data loss.
  • If all of Kafka is down (rare): The API returns 503. Callers retry with backoff. No notifications are lost — they retry later. Eventual delivery, not guaranteed instant delivery.
  • Dead Letter Queue: Notifications that fail 3+ times go to a DLQDead Letter Queue — a separate queue where messages that can't be processed go. Instead of retrying forever (which wastes resources) or dropping them (which loses data), you park them for investigation. Engineers can manually replay them after fixing the root cause. for manual investigation. Prevents poison messages from blocking the pipeline.

What the interviewer writes: "Handled follow-up questions well. Decomposed the rate limiter naturally. Discussed failure modes without prompting. Senior-level thinking."

3. Technical depthFAILNo data flow explained. No math. No justification for why each piece is a separate service. "Because microservices" is not a reason.
4. Scale + fault toleranceFAIL12 services = 12 failure points, 12 deployments, 12 monitoring dashboards. No discussion of what happens when services fail. Network latency between 12 services adds up.

Interviewer verdict: "Candidate drew a lot of boxes but couldn't explain any of them deeply. Microservices for the sake of microservices. No data-backed reasoning."

Before you say "I think that covers it," run these six questions in your head. They take 30 seconds and catch 80% of gaps:

  1. "If I remove any box, what breaks?" — If nothing breaks, that box shouldn't exist.
  2. "Can each component be deployed independently?" — If two components must deploy together, they're really one component pretending to be two — you've added network calls and deployment complexity for zero benefit.
  3. "What's the single most likely failure?" — Usually it's the database or the message queue. Do you have a plan?
  4. "Where's the bottleneck at 10x traffic?" — Find it before the interviewer asks.
  5. "Did I use actual numbers, or did I just say 'it scales'?" — Numbers win. Handwaving loses.
  6. "Could I explain this to a colleague in 2 minutes?" — If your design requires a 10-minute explanation, it's too complex.

Section 8

Different Interview Formats — One Framework, Three Settings

The 5-step framework works everywhere, but the time allocation changes based on the interview format. Here are the three most common formats and exactly how to split your time in each one.

The most common format. One interviewer, one question, 45 minutes. The clock is your enemy.

Time split: 5 min requirements, 10 min high-level + components, 15 min deep dive on 2-3 components, 5 min scaling/evolution, 5 min Q&A from interviewer, 5 min buffer for detours.

Key insight: You will NOT cover everything. The interviewer knows this. They're evaluating whether you can prioritize — pick the most interesting 2-3 components and go deep, rather than skim all 6-7. At Google, a candidate who goes deep on the feed ranking algorithm for 10 minutes will outscore a candidate who mentions 8 components superficially.

Pro tip: Say out loud: "I have 45 minutes, so I'm going to focus on [X] and [Y] because they're the most architecturally interesting. I'll mention [Z] at a high level. Sound good?" This shows the interviewer you're managing scope deliberately.

Amazon and Netflix give you more time — but expect proportionally more depth. The extra 15 minutes should go to trade-off discussions and failure mode analysis, not more components.

Time split: 8 min requirements (Amazon wants more thorough scoping), 12 min high-level, 20 min deep dive (expect the interviewer to probe here), 8 min scaling + failure modes, 8 min trade-offs + alternatives, 4 min Q&A.

Key insight: Amazon specifically evaluates "Did they consider alternatives?" For every decision, be ready to say: "I chose X over Y because [reason]. If [condition changed], I'd switch to Y." For example: "I chose PostgreSQL for user preferences because 500M rows with complex queries favors a relational model. If we had 5 billion users with simple key-value lookups, I'd switch to DynamoDB."

Netflix-specific: They care deeply about operational cost. "How many engineers does it take to keep this running?" is a real question. A design with 3 services beats one with 12 if the 3-service version is equally capable.

Some companies — especially Stripe, Square, and well-funded startups — ask you to write a design document instead of doing a live whiteboard. You typically get 3-7 days and submit a written doc.

Structure (what they expect):

  1. Problem Statement (1 paragraph) — What you're designing and why
  2. Requirements & Constraints (1 page) — Functional requirements, non-functional requirements, back-of-envelope math
  3. High-Level Design (1 page) — Component diagram with explanations
  4. Detailed Design (2-3 pages) — Data models, API contracts, data flow per component, specific tech choices with justification
  5. Trade-offs & Alternatives (1 page) — What you considered and rejected, and why. This is where take-homes shine — you can show depth that's impossible in 45 minutes.

Pro tip: Include diagrams. Use excalidraw.com or draw.io to create clean architecture diagrams. A doc with zero diagrams is a red flag — it suggests you can't think visually about systems.

Common mistake: Writing 15 pages. The interviewer doesn't want a novel. 5-6 pages is the sweet spot. Every sentence must justify its existence.

Anti-Lessons — Three Ways to Guarantee Failure

We've talked about what to DO. Now let's talk about three patterns that guarantee failure in a system design interview. These aren't minor mistakes — they're structural errors that no amount of technical knowledge can overcome.

What it looks like: "Design Instagram" and the candidate tries to cover uploads, feed, stories, reels, DMs, search, notifications, ads, recommendations, content moderation, and live streaming — all in 45 minutes. They draw 15 boxes, 30 arrows, and go deep on zero of them.

Why it fails: The interviewer can't evaluate depth if there's no depth. Breadth without depth is a Wikipedia summary, not a design. The math: 45 minutes / 15 features = 3 minutes each. You can't justify a single technical decision in 3 minutes.

The fix: Deliberately scope. Pick 3-4 core features. Say: "I'll focus on [X], [Y], and [Z] because they cover the most interesting architectural challenges. I'll mention [A] and [B] at a high level if we have time. Does that scope work for you?" Then go deep on those 3-4 features.

Real quote from a Google interviewer: "I'd rather see a candidate design 3 features really well than 10 features poorly. Scoping is a senior engineering skill."

What it looks like: The problem says 10 million users, but the candidate immediately designs for 10 billion. Multi-region deployment, global load balancing, eventually consistent data replication, custom sharding algorithms — all for a system that could run on two servers.

Why it fails: Every optimization has a cost. Multi-region replication adds latency, complexity, and a 3x infrastructure bill. If you don't need it, you're paying the cost with zero benefit. The interviewer sees someone who can't assess what's actually needed.

The fix: Always start with the simplest architecture that handles the stated requirements. Then say: "At 10x traffic, here's what I'd change. At 100x, here's the next evolution." This shows you can think about scale without jumping to it prematurely.

Remember the $100K Kubernetes story from Section 4: The startup spent $8,000/month on infrastructure for 500 users. A $20/month VPS would have done the job. Architecture must match actual scale.

What it looks like: The interviewer says "Design a chat app." The candidate nods, turns to the whiteboard, and starts drawing in silence. No clarifying questions. No requirements gathering. No math. Just boxes and arrows.

Why it fails: The interviewer wanted to see questions like: "Is this 1-to-1 chat, group chat, or both? How many concurrent users? Do messages need to be persisted forever? Do we need read receipts? Do we need end-to-end encryption?" Without these, the candidate is designing a system for requirements they invented in their own head — which might be completely different from what the interviewer had in mind.

The fix: Spend the first 5 minutes asking questions. Write the answers on the whiteboard so both you and the interviewer can reference them. This serves two purposes: (1) you get the right constraints, and (2) you demonstrate that you think before you act — which is what senior engineers do.

Meta's rubric literally says: "Does the candidate drive the conversation or wait to be told what to do?" Asking zero questions is an instant signal that you're waiting to be told.
Section 11

Common Mistakes — Six Traps That Kill Good Candidates

These are the six most common mistakes in system design interviews, ranked by how often they cause otherwise-strong candidates to fail. Each one is fixable — once you know to watch for it.

The trap: The interviewer says "Design a rate limiter." Within 30 seconds, you're writing token bucket pseudocode. You skipped understanding who needs rate limiting, at what scale, and what happens when limits are hit.

Why candidates do it: Anxiety. The silence feels uncomfortable, so you fill it with action. Drawing feels productive even when it's counterproductive.

The fix: Force yourself to ask at least 5 questions before touching the whiteboard. Write the answers down. Only then start drawing. The 5-minute investment in understanding saves 20 minutes of redesigning later.

The trap: You've memorized the architecture for "Design Twitter" from a blog post. The interview asks "Design Twitter" — great! You regurgitate the memorized design. Then the interviewer asks: "What if we need to support 10x more users in a different geography?" You can't adapt because you memorized a specific solution, not the reasoning behind it.

Why this is dangerous: Interviewers can tell when you're reciting a memorized design. The flow is too smooth, the decisions come too fast, and when they probe with "why," you stumble. Plus, many interviewers deliberately twist the problem to see if you can think on your feet.

The fix: Learn the framework (5 steps), not specific architectures. If you understand WHY Twitter uses a fan-out-on-write strategy for the feed (because celebrities have millions of followers and computing feeds in real-time is too slow), you can adapt that reasoning to any social media feed — Instagram, LinkedIn, whatever.

The trap: You choose PostgreSQL for the database and move on. The interviewer asks: "What trade-offs are you making with that choice?" You can't answer because you never considered the alternative.

The reality: Every architectural decision is a trade-off. Choosing PostgreSQL means you get ACID transactions and complex queries, but you accept vertical scaling limits and schema rigidity. Choosing DynamoDB means you get infinite horizontal scaling, but you lose joins and complex queries. Neither is "right" — it depends on the workload.

The fix: For every major decision, mention the alternative and explain why you chose against it: "I'm using PostgreSQL here because we need joins for the user preferences query. If this were a write-heavy log store, I'd use Cassandra instead." Two sentences. Shows depth.

The trap: "We need a cache because we have a lot of reads." How many reads? "A lot." That's not engineering — that's hand-waving.

Why math matters: Math turns vague intuitions into concrete decisions. "A lot of reads" could mean 100/sec (no cache needed) or 1M/sec (definitely need a cache). The number changes the architecture. Without math, you're guessing.

The fix: Always do back-of-envelope calculations. Write them on the whiteboard. Say them out loud. "500M users, 20% daily active = 100M DAU. Average 10 reads per session = 1B reads/day = 11,500 reads/sec. At that volume, a cache with 95% hit rate means only 575 reads/sec hit the database. That's easily handled by a single PostgreSQL instance with connection pooling."

The complete guide to these calculations is in our Back-of-Envelope Estimation page.

The trap: You design a beautiful, clean architecture. Every component connects perfectly. The data flows smoothly. Then the interviewer asks: "What happens when the database goes down?" And you realize you never thought about it.

The reality: Everything fails. Databases crash, networks partition, services time out, disks fill up, certificates expire, DNS goes down. A design that doesn't account for failure is a design that doesn't work in production.

The fix: For each component, ask yourself: "What happens when this dies?" and "How does the system recover?" Common answers: redundancy (read replicas), retries with backoff, circuit breakers, dead letter queues, graceful degradation (show cached data when the real-time feed is down).

The trap: You've read 50 blog posts about system design. You've watched hours of YouTube videos. You feel prepared. Then in the actual interview, you can't verbalize your thoughts clearly. The ideas are in your head but they come out jumbled.

Why this happens: Reading and speaking use different parts of your brain. Reading system design content creates recognition — you can identify good designs when you see them. But interviews require production — you need to create a design from scratch while explaining it clearly. These are two completely different skills.

The fix: Practice out loud. Set a 45-minute timer. Open excalidraw.com. Talk to yourself (or a study partner) as if you're in an interview. Record yourself if you can stomach it. The first 3-5 practice sessions will feel awkward. By session 10, you'll be fluent. There is no shortcut for this.

Set a 45-minute timer. Design a 1-to-1 and group chat app like WhatsApp/Slack. Focus on message delivery, read receipts, and offline message storage. What protocol do you use for real-time messaging?

  • WebSockets for real-time delivery — because HTTP requires the client to keep asking "any new messages?" every few seconds (polling), which wastes bandwidth and adds latency. WebSockets keep a persistent connection open so the server can push messages instantly. HTTP is fine for everything else (auth, history, settings) because those are one-off requests, not continuous streams.
  • Message ordering is the hard problem. Use a per-conversation sequence number, not wall-clock time — because two servers in different data centers can disagree on what time it is by milliseconds, causing messages to appear out of order. A sequence number (1, 2, 3...) assigned by a single authority is unambiguous.
  • Group chat fan-out: if a group has 500 members, do you send 500 individual messages or use a pub/sub model?
  • Offline users: store messages in the database, deliver when they reconnect. Track "last seen" message ID per user per conversation.

Key components: WebSocket Gateway (maintains persistent connections, routes messages), Message Service (persists messages, assigns sequence numbers), Presence Service (tracks online/offline status), Message Store (Cassandra — because chat messages are write-heavy and time-series in nature: you rarely update a message after sending it, and you mostly read them in chronological order. Cassandra is optimized for exactly this pattern, handling millions of writes/sec across distributed nodes). For group chat, use a fan-out service that reads group membership and publishes to each member's connection. Read receipts: when a client acknowledges a message, update the "last_read" pointer in the database.

Set a 45-minute timer. Design a distributed rate limiter for an API gateway handling 100K requests/sec. Remember: this is a SYSTEM, not an algorithm. Where does it sit? How is it configured? What happens on failure?

  • Decide: per-user? Per-API-key? Per-IP? Per-endpoint? Each has different storage requirements.
  • Sliding window counter in Redis: O(1) per check — fast and simple, but allows a burst of 2x the limit at window boundaries (a user could send 100 requests at 11:59 and 100 more at 12:00). Sorted set for precision: O(log N) per check — no boundary burst problem, but more expensive per operation. Pick based on whether "approximately 100 requests/minute" or "exactly 100 requests/minute" matters more.
  • Distributed coordination: if you have 10 API gateway servers, each checking its local count, a user could exceed the limit by 10x. Solution: centralized counter in Redis.
  • Failure mode: if Redis goes down, do you allow all requests (fail-open) or reject all (fail-closed)? Answer depends on whether you're rate-limiting for security or cost.

Key components: API Gateway (first hop, checks rate limit before routing), Rate Limit Service (stateless, reads/writes Redis), Redis Cluster (centralized counter store, 100K reads+writes/sec), Configuration Store (per-API-key limits, stored in PostgreSQL, cached in Redis), Response Headers (X-RateLimit-Limit, X-RateLimit-Remaining, Retry-After). Algorithm: sliding window counter per API key. Failure mode: fail-open (allow traffic) for general rate limiting — because blocking all users when Redis hiccups is worse than briefly allowing extra requests. But fail-closed (block traffic) for security-sensitive endpoints like login — because allowing unlimited login attempts could let an attacker brute-force passwords. Monitoring: track limit-hit rate per API key, alert on sudden spikes.

Set a 45-minute timer. You already saw this in Section 6 — but now do it from memory. Can you recreate the architecture, the math, and the technical decisions without looking back? Time yourself and compare.

  • Don't forget: user preferences, rate limiting per user, quiet hours, priority levels.
  • The Router is the most interesting component — it's where all the logic lives.
  • Each channel sender (push, email, SMS) talks to a different external API with different rate limits and failure modes.
  • Dead Letter Queue for messages that fail 3+ times. Don't retry forever.

See the complete walkthrough in Section 6. Key architecture: API intake (validates + enqueues to Kafka), Router (reads preferences, picks channels, rate limits), Channel Senders (push/email/SMS/in-app), Data Stores (PostgreSQL for prefs, Cassandra for logs, Redis for caching). All decisions justified by 2B notifications/day math.

Set a 45-minute timer. Design the news feed for a social media platform like Twitter. The key challenge: a user follows 500 accounts. When they open the app, how do you generate their feed in under 200ms? What about users with 10 million followers?

  • Two approaches: fan-out-on-write (precompute feeds when a post is created — fast reads but expensive writes, because one post could update millions of follower caches) vs. fan-out-on-read (compute feeds when a user opens the app — cheap writes but slow reads, because generating a feed from 500 followed accounts in real-time takes time). The right choice depends on your read:write ratio and follower distribution.
  • The celebrity problem: if a user with 10M followers posts, fan-out-on-write means 10M cache updates. That's expensive. Solution: hybrid — fan-out-on-write for normal users, fan-out-on-read for celebrities.
  • Timeline cache: store just post IDs (not full posts) per user in Redis — because storing full post content in every follower's cache would multiply storage by the average follower count. IDs are tiny (8 bytes each), so a timeline of 800 posts costs only 6.4 KB per user. When a user opens the app, read their ID list, then batch-fetch post details from the post store.
  • Ranking: not just chronological — because a purely time-ordered feed buries great posts from accounts that post rarely under a flood of posts from accounts that post constantly. Mix in relevance scoring, engagement prediction, and diversity. This needs to be a separate ML service because ranking models get retrained frequently and have completely different scaling needs (GPU-heavy) from the feed delivery pipeline (I/O-heavy).

Key components: Post Service (writes posts to database + publishes event), Fan-out Service (for normal users: on post creation, push post ID to all followers' timeline caches in Redis; for celebrities: skip fan-out), Feed Service (reads timeline cache, merges with celebrity posts in real-time, applies ranking), Timeline Cache (Redis sorted set per user, score = timestamp, trimmed to last 800 posts), Ranking Service (ML model that scores posts by relevance, engagement prediction). Trade-off: fan-out-on-write uses more storage (every follower's cache gets updated) but makes reads fast. Facebook/Instagram use a hybrid approach. Twitter historically used fan-out-on-write.
