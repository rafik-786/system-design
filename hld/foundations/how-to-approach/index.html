Section 1

TL;DR — The One-Minute Version

Mental Model: System design is breaking one impossible problem into ten solvable ones — then making sure each piece can stand on its own, scale on its own, and fail without killing the others.

Every system design question — "design Instagram," "design a rate limiter," "design a chat app" — follows the same five steps. The system changes, the steps don't. Here's the entire framework on one screen.

One-line takeaway: Don't try to design the whole system at once. Pick one piece, solve it, then move to the next. Baby steps beat giant leaps every single time.

Section 4

Where It Breaks — Why "Just Draw Boxes" Fails

Drawing boxes without understanding the problem leads to four predictable failures. Let's look at each one with a real example, because understanding WHY the naive approach fails is what makes the framework in Section 5 click.

Failure 1: Wrong Scope

The interviewer said "Design Instagram," but what they actually wanted was just the news feed. The candidate spent 20 minutes drawing boxes for uploads, stories, DMs, search, and notifications — and ran out of time before going deep on anything. The interviewer's feedback: "Covered everything at 10,000 feet, went deep on nothing. I couldn't evaluate their technical depth."

This is the scoping failure. It happens because the candidate never asked: "Which part of Instagram should I focus on?" That single question would have saved 20 minutes.

Failure 2: Wrong Technology Choices

A candidate designing a simple URL shortener puts Apache KafkaA distributed event streaming platform designed for high-throughput, real-time data pipelines. It can handle millions of messages per second. Massive overkill for a URL shortener that does 200 writes/second — a simple database insert handles that fine. in their diagram for "handling URL creation events." The interviewer asks: "What throughput do you expect?" The candidate does the math: 200 writes per second. Kafka handles millions per second. It's like renting a Boeing 747 to fly from your bedroom to your kitchen.

The root cause: Technology choices made BEFORE doing the math are almost always wrong. You don't know if you need Kafka until you know you're processing millions of events per second. You don't know if you need Redis until you know your read-to-write ratio. Numbers first, tools second.

Failure 3: No Justification

The dreaded follow-up question chain:

Interviewer: "Why NoSQL here?"
Candidate: "Because it scales."
Interviewer: "How do you know you need to scale?"
Candidate: "..."
Interviewer: "What's your expected data volume?"
Candidate: "..."

Every technology choice needs a reason rooted in the problem's constraints. "Because it scales" is not a reason — it's a hope. "Because we have 500M URLs and a 50:1 read ratio, which makes cache lookups critical, so Redis" — that's a reason. Amazon's evaluation rubric explicitly checks: "Did they justify each decision with data?"

He previously worked at Amazon and now runs a popular system design course. His "baby steps" framework is the foundation of this page.: a startup with 500 users containerized everything in Kubernetes with auto-scaling, service mesh, distributed tracing, and a full observability stack. Over-engineering isn't just wasteful — it's actively harmful. More components means more failure points, more network calls, more deployment complexity, more things to debug at 3 AM. The startup spent 6 months building infrastructure instead of features. By the time they shipped, a competitor with a simple monolith had captured the market.

Think First

Look at the four failures above: wrong scope, wrong tech, no justification, over-engineering. They all share the same root cause. Can you spot what it is?

Every failure happened because the candidate (or team) skipped the same step. Which step?

The root cause of all four failures is the same: they skipped understanding the problem. They didn't ask how many users, didn't calculate the traffic, didn't clarify the scope, didn't do the math. They jumped straight to drawing architecture diagrams — and every subsequent decision was built on guesses instead of data.

Key insight: The failures aren't caused by a lack of knowledge. They're caused by a lack of process. A candidate who knows every database in existence but skips Step 1 will still design the wrong system. A candidate who knows only PostgreSQL but follows the framework will design a system that actually works. Process beats knowledge every time.
Section 5

The Breakthrough — Arpit's Baby Steps Framework

Now you've seen what happens without a framework: wrong scope, wrong tools, no justification, over-engineering. All from skipping the first step. So what does a GOOD approach look like?

Arpit Bhayani's framework gives you five steps. They work for any system — Instagram, a rate limiter, a chat app, a payment gateway, anything. The system changes, the steps don't. Let's walk through all five, then apply them to a real URL shortener design so you can see exactly what to say, what to draw, and what the interviewer is evaluating at each step.

Notice the loop-back arrow from Step 5 to Step 3. System design is not a straight line — it's a spiralAn iterative process where you revisit earlier decisions with new understanding. Each pass through the cycle adds more detail and refinement. Unlike a linear process, you're expected to go back and improve.. You go broad first (Steps 1-2), then deep (Steps 3-4), and if you find something that's still too vague, you zoom in further and repeat.

Walkthrough: Designing a URL Shortener (Step by Step)

Let's apply all five steps to a real problem so you can see exactly what to say at each step, what to draw, and what the interviewer is evaluating. We'll design a URL shortener like BitlyA popular URL shortening service. You give it a long URL like "https://example.com/very/long/path?with=parameters" and it gives you a short one like "bit.ly/abc123". When someone clicks the short URL, Bitly redirects them to the original long URL..

Think First

Before reading this walkthrough, try it yourself. Set a 15-minute timer (use your phone — seriously, time it). Open excalidraw.com or grab paper. Design a URL shortener using the 5 steps. Then compare your approach to this one.

Remember: Step 1 first. Don't draw boxes until you know the constraints. That's the whole point.

Step 1 — Understand (What to SAY)

You say to the interviewer: "Before I start designing, let me understand the requirements." This one sentence sets the right tone for the entire interview. It tells the interviewer you're structured, thoughtful, and not impulsive.

Then you ask questions and learn:

  • 100M monthly active users
  • 500M new URLs created per month
  • Each URL clicked on average 50 times over its lifetime
  • Short URLs should be 6-8 characters
  • URLs never expire
  • 99.9% availability (about 8.7 hours of downtime per year max)

Now do the math — out loud, on the whiteboard. The interviewer wants to see this.

Back-of-Envelope (say this out loud) Writes: 500M/month / 30 / 86,400 = ~200 writes/sec Reads: 500M x 50 / 30 / 86,400 = ~10,000 reads/sec Read:Write ratio = 50:1 → heavily read-dominant Storage: 500M x 500 bytes avg = 250 GB/year → fits on one disk for years → Read-heavy system. Cache lookups will be critical. Write path is simple.

What the interviewer is evaluating: "Do they ask good questions? Do they drive the conversation? Do they use data to shape their design?" At Google, this is literally the top evaluation criterion: structured thinking.

Step 2 — Break Down (What to DRAW)

Now — and only now — you start drawing. A URL shortener has two core operations: creating a short URL, and redirecting a short URL to its target. Those are two different workloads (write vs. read), so they become two components:

  • URL Creation Service — accepts a long URL, generates a short code, stores the mapping
  • Redirect Service — receives a short URL click, looks up the mapping, returns a 301 redirectAn HTTP status code meaning "Moved Permanently." When a browser receives a 301 from bit.ly/abc123, it automatically navigates to the original long URL. The browser may also cache this redirect to skip the lookup next time.

Plus the data layer: a URL Database (stores short_code to long_url mappings) and a Cache (hot lookups for popular URLs).

What the interviewer is evaluating: "Do the components have clear, non-overlapping responsibilities? Are there 4-7 pieces, not 15?" At Meta, this is the scoping check.

Step 3 — Go Deeper (What to EXPLAIN)

Pick the most interesting component — for a URL shortener, it's the Redirect Service because it handles 50x more traffic. Walk through the data flow:

  1. User clicks bit.ly/abc123
  2. Request hits the Redirect Service
  3. Service checks the Redis cache first (0.1ms). Cache hit? Return 301 redirect. Done.
  4. Cache miss? Query PostgreSQL (4ms). Store result in cache for next time. Return 301.
  5. URL not found? Return 404.

For the Creation Service: accept the long URL, generate a unique 7-character code (base62 encoding of an auto-incrementing ID or a random hash), store the mapping in PostgreSQL, return the short URL to the user.

Step 4 — Four Decisions (What to JUSTIFY)

For each component, make four decisions — and say WHY for each one. This is where you prove you're not just name-dropping technologies.

What the interviewer is evaluating: "Did they justify each decision with data? Or did they just say 'because it scales'?" At Amazon, every decision needs a data-backed reason.

Step 5 — Decompose Further (What to EVOLVE)

The interviewer asks: "What happens at 10x traffic?" You don't panic. You adjust the numbers:

  • At 10x (100K reads/sec): Shard the Redis cache across 3-4 nodes. Add 2 more read replicas for PostgreSQL. Architecture doesn't change — we just scale the pieces that need it.
  • At 100x (1M reads/sec): Put popular URLs on a CDN at edge locations. Multi-region deployment. Eventually consistent global state for the cache.

The architecture didn't change. The numbers changed, and the scaling decisions evolved with them. This is what Netflix looks for: "Do they start simple and evolve, or jump to complex solutions?"

Section 6

Step 2 — Break It Down Into Pieces

Now that you understand the problem, it's time to break it into pieces. This is where the magic happens. A giant, scary system becomes a handful of manageable components — each one small enough to reason about on its own.

The key word here is mutually exclusiveNo overlap between components. Each responsibility belongs to exactly one component. If Feature X breaks, only Component Y is affected — no other component shares that concern.. Each component should own exactly one responsibility, and no two components should overlap. If two pieces both handle "sending emails," you have a problem. If one piece handles "photo uploads" AND "recommendations," that piece is doing too much.

Think First

Try to split Instagram into 5-6 independent pieces, where each piece owns one clear feature. What would you name each piece, and what would it be responsible for?

Think about what a user does on Instagram: they scroll, they post, they message, they search, they get notified...

There's no single "right" answer — any reasonable breakdown with clear boundaries works. But here's one common way to break Instagram down that demonstrates the key principles:

Notice that each component has a different characteristic noted at the bottom — "read-heavy," "write-heavy," "real-time," "async." These characteristics are crucial because they guide the technical decisions in Step 4:

  • Read-heavy (like Feed) — benefits enormously from caching
  • Write-heavy (like Notifications) — benefits from message queues and async processing
  • Real-time (like Messaging) — needs WebSockets instead of regular HTTP
The "Mutually Exclusive Test": Can you remove any one piece without breaking the others? If the notification service goes down, people can still post photos, browse feeds, and send messages. They just won't get push notifications. Each component is independent — that's the hallmark of a good breakdown.

This independence isn't just a theoretical nicety — it's what allows large engineering organizations to function. At Instagram, the feed team can ship updates without coordinating with the messaging team. The upload team can migrate to a new storage backend without affecting search. Organizational independence is only possible because the technical boundaries are clean.

Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up

There are two ways to approach the breakdown:

Start with the big picture, then zoom in. You name the major features first (Feed, Upload, Messaging, Search, Notifications), and then figure out what each one needs internally. This works best when you're designing a new system from scratch.

Good for: interview questions, greenfield projects, when the system is well-understood (social media, e-commerce, chat).

Start with one concrete feature, build it out, then see what else connects. You pick the most critical feature (e.g., "a user clicks a short URL and gets redirected") and design just that path end-to-end. Then you see what other features need to attach to that core.

Good for: focused problems, systems you're unfamiliar with, when requirements are unclear and you need to start somewhere concrete.

How Many Components Is "Right"?

There's no magic number, but here's a practical guideline:

With fewer than 4 components, each one is probably doing too many things (like our "one giant server" example). With more than 7, you're probably splitting at the wrong level — mixing architecture-level components (like "Feed Service") with module-level details (like "Image Resizer"). The Image Resizer is an internal module of the Upload Service, not a separate component.

Sanity check: Can you describe each component's job in one sentence? "The Feed Service generates and serves personalized user feeds." "The Upload Service handles media upload, storage, and processing." If you need a paragraph to explain what a component does, it's either too broad (split it) or too vaguely defined (clarify it).
The #1 mistake in this step: Drawing too many boxes. If you have 15 components, you probably haven't thought about which ones can be merged. A good breakdown typically has 4-7 components. More than that, and you're either splitting too fine or including implementation details that belong at the module level.

Step 3 — Go Deeper Into Each Piece

You've broken the system into components. Now pick one component and dissect it. What data does it manage? What operations does it support? How does data flow through it?

Let's use a concrete example from Arpit's lectures: the Facebook News Feed. When you open Facebook, you see a personalized feed of posts from your friends. That's one component. But how does it actually work inside?

Think First

Think about what happens when you open Facebook and see your feed. Somewhere, a component needs to collect posts from all your friends, decide which ones to show you, rank them, and deliver them fast. What sub-pieces would you need inside a "Feed Service" to make this work?

Think about the journey of a single feed request: it arrives, something collects posts, something ranks them, something returns the result.

The Feed Service can be split into three internal pieces:

The Web Server is the front door — it receives the user's request, checks that they're logged in (authentication), and passes the request to the right internal service. It doesn't know or care how feeds are generated — it just routes the request and returns the response.

The Feed Generator figures out which posts are candidates for this user's feed — it looks at who the user follows, pulls their recent posts from the database, and collects them into a raw pool. It doesn't decide the order — that's someone else's job.

The Feed Aggregator takes that pool, ranks the posts by relevance (using signals like recency, likes, comments, and relationship strength), removes duplicates from reshared posts, and returns a sorted, paginated page.

Why separate them? If you remove the aggregator, you'd still have posts — just in random order. If you remove the generator, the aggregator has nothing to rank. Each piece is necessary and sufficient. Because they're separate, different teams can work independently — the ranking team can experiment with new algorithms without affecting how posts are fetched.

Let's try another one. A food delivery app (like Uber Eats) needs to handle: browsing restaurants, placing orders, assigning drivers, tracking delivery, and processing payment. What components would you break this into?

Each of those features is a strong candidate for its own component. How would they communicate?

Let's try another example. Here's what a food delivery app might look like when broken into components. Notice how different the components' characteristics are compared to the Instagram example — that's because different products have different dominant workloads.

Each component has fundamentally different technical needs:

  • Restaurant service — mostly reads menus (cache them heavily — menus don't change every minute)
  • Order service — needs strict transaction guaranteesACID properties — Atomicity (all-or-nothing), Consistency (data stays valid), Isolation (concurrent operations don't interfere), Durability (committed data survives crashes). Essential for operations like payments where partial completion is unacceptable. (you can't charge someone without recording the order)
  • Driver matching — needs geographic queriesDatabase queries that work with location data — "find all drivers within 2km of this restaurant." Requires special spatial indexes (like R-trees or geohashes) that regular databases don't optimize for. (finding the nearest available driver)
  • Tracking service — needs real-time bidirectional communicationTwo-way communication where both the client and server can send messages at any time, without waiting for the other to respond first. WebSocket is the most common protocol for this. Regular HTTP is one-way: the client asks, the server answers. (pushing live location updates)

If you jammed all of these into one monolith, you'd be forced to use one technology stack for wildly different problems. Every deployment would risk breaking everything. This is the fundamental reason we split systems into components — not because it looks good on a diagram, but because different problems genuinely need different solutions.

Arpit's advice: "If you're not sure what a component does, try removing it from the diagram. If nothing breaks, that component probably shouldn't exist. Every box must justify its existence."

Step 4 — The Four Technical Decisions

Now comes the part that separates a hand-wavy diagram from a real architecture. For every component you've identified, you need to make four specific technical decisions. Not just "this component exists" — but "this component uses PostgreSQL for storage, scales horizontally with a load balancer, processes image resizing asynchronously, and communicates via REST APIs."

Steps 1-3 = WHAT the system needs. Step 4 = HOW each piece actually works. This is where your knowledge of databases, caching, scaling, and communication protocols becomes essential. Don't worry if you don't know much about these yet — this page teaches the thinking process. The foundation pages that follow will fill in the technical depth.
Think First

You have a "Notification Service" that sends push notifications, emails, and SMS to users. What database would you use? Would you process notifications synchronously (make the user wait) or asynchronously (in the background)? Why?

Think about what happens if you try to send 10 million notifications one at a time, waiting for each to complete...

The four decisions form a checklist you apply to every component:

Let's walk through these four decisions with a real example — the Upload Service for Instagram:

🗄️ Decision 1: Database & Caching

The upload service deals with two kinds of data: metadata (who uploaded it, when, caption, tags) and the actual file (the photo or video bytes).

  • Metadata → PostgreSQL (structured data, queryable by user, date, hashtag)
  • Files → Object storageA storage system optimized for large files (images, videos, documents). Examples: Amazon S3, Google Cloud Storage. Unlike databases, it stores files as "objects" with a unique key, not in tables with rows and columns. like Amazon S3 (storing 500M photos in a traditional database would be insanely expensive)
  • Caching → CDNContent Delivery Network — a network of servers spread around the world that store copies of your content close to users. When someone in Tokyo requests a photo, they get it from a server in Tokyo instead of waiting for it to travel from your server in Virginia. for recently uploaded photos so they load instantly for followers

📈 Decision 2: Scaling & Fault Tolerance

At peak hours, millions of people upload photos simultaneously. One server can't handle that. So we'd scale horizontallyAdding more machines to handle more traffic (as opposed to "vertical scaling" which means getting a bigger, more powerful single machine). Horizontal scaling has no ceiling — you can keep adding machines indefinitely. — run multiple upload servers behind a load balancerA component that distributes incoming requests across multiple servers. Think of it as a receptionist directing visitors to different offices so no single office gets overwhelmed..

For fault toleranceThe ability of a system to continue working correctly even when some parts fail. A fault-tolerant system doesn't prevent failures — it survives them gracefully., if one upload server crashes, the load balancer automatically routes traffic to the remaining healthy servers. The user might see a brief retry, but their upload doesn't fail permanently.

For the database, we'd use read replicasCopies of your main database that only handle read queries. The main (primary) database handles all writes, then automatically copies changes to the replicas. This spreads the read load across multiple machines. — copies of the database that handle read queries, so the main database only needs to handle writes.

⚡ Decision 3: Async Processing

When you upload a photo, does Instagram make you wait while it creates 6 different sizes of your image, generates thumbnails, scans it for prohibited content, and updates the search index? No. That would take 10-30 seconds. Instead, it accepts your upload quickly and does all that heavy work in the background.

This is asynchronous processingInstead of doing all the work while the user waits (synchronous), you accept the request immediately and process it in the background (asynchronous). The user sees "uploaded!" in 1 second, while resizing, compression, and indexing happen behind the scenes over the next 30 seconds.. The upload service puts a "please process this image" message into a message queueA waiting line for tasks. One service puts a task in the queue, another service picks it up and processes it when it's ready. Think of it as a restaurant kitchen's order tickets — the waiter drops off the ticket, and the next available cook grabs it., and a separate image-processing worker picks it up and handles it.

🔗 Decision 4: Communication Protocol

The mobile app talks to the upload service via REST over HTTPS — it's the standard for client-to-server communication, works everywhere, and handles large file uploads well.

Internally, the upload service talks to the image-processing workers via a message queue (like Kafka or SQS) — because the communication is one-way ("here's a job, do it when you can") and doesn't need an immediate response.

The upload service also notifies the Feed Service that there's a new post, potentially using an internal event — "hey, user X just posted, regenerate feeds for their followers."

Why "one database for everything" fails: A notification service writing millions of records per minute has completely different needs than a read-heavy user profile service. Section 9

Step 5 — Know When to Go Deeper (and When to Stop)

You've broken the system into components, explored each one, and made technical decisions. But sometimes a component is still too vague. "Feed Generator" sounds concrete, but what does it actually do internally? Can you imagine writing the code for it? If the answer is "not really," then you need to decompose further.

This step is the most underrated part of the framework. Many people stop at Step 4 and present a diagram with 5 boxes and 4 arrows. That's a starting point, not a design. The depth comes from taking at least one or two critical components and breaking them down further — proving you understand what's happening inside those boxes.

Interview power move: After presenting the high-level design, say: "Let me dive deeper into the most critical component — the [X] service." Then decompose it into sub-components. This signals you can operate at multiple levels of abstraction — you see both the forest and the trees.

Which Component to Deep-Dive?

Choose the one that's most interesting from an engineering perspective — usually the one with the most complex data flow, the hardest scaling challenge, or the most important consistency requirement. For a social media app, that's typically the feed service. For a payment system, it's the transaction service. For a chat app, it's the message delivery pipeline.

Think First

The Feed Generator "pulls posts from all your friends and collects them." But you have 500 friends, each with hundreds of posts. How would you actually implement this? What sub-pieces would the Feed Generator need inside it?

Think about reading posts, merging them, and storing temporary results.

The Feed Generator can be decomposed further into:

  • Post Reader — queries the social graphA data structure that represents relationships between users — who follows whom, who is friends with whom. Think of it as a giant web where each person is a dot and each relationship is a line connecting two dots. ("who does this user follow?"), then fetches recent posts from each friend in parallel. If you follow 500 people, it sends 500 queries simultaneously.
  • Post Merger — takes those hundreds of post lists and merges them into one unified timeline, removing duplicates from reshared content. Like combining 500 newspapers into one personalized edition.
  • Temp Cache — stores the final result in fast memory so refreshing or scrolling doesn't redo all that expensive work. After a few minutes, the cached version expires and the next request generates a fresh feed.

When to Use Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up

Both approaches are valid. In practice, most experienced engineers blend them — they start top-down to get the big picture, then switch to bottom-up when they hit a component they don't fully understand. The key is to keep moving.

Quick heuristic: If you can name at least 4 components within 60 seconds, use top-down — your mental model is strong enough. If you can't, start bottom-up with the most concrete user action (like "user clicks the buy button") and trace the entire flow. The bottom-up approach often reveals components you wouldn't have thought of otherwise.

The "Implementer's Hat" Test

Here's a practical way to know when to stop decomposing: put on the "implementer's hat." For each component, ask yourself: "Could I sit down and write the code for this in one afternoon?"

  • If yes → you've gone deep enough. Stop here.
  • If no → break it down further.
  • If you realize it's basically a well-known tool (like "this is just Redis" or "this is just an S3 bucket") → stop. Don't redesign existing tools. Say "this component is essentially a key-value cache — we'd use Redis" and move on.
  • If it feels like it could be a third-party service (like "this is just sending emails") → mention it briefly and move on. "We'd use a service like SendGrid or SES for email delivery."

The goal is to decompose until each piece is either implementable in an afternoon OR is a well-known tool/service. Once you reach either of those stopping conditions, you've gone deep enough for that component.

Common mistake — going too deep: Some candidates try to design the internal algorithm of the ranking service or the exact data structure of the cache. That's module-level detail (the third zoom level from Section 3). In a system design discussion, stay at the architecture and component levels unless specifically asked to go deeper. Demonstrate breadth of thinking, not implementation details of one small piece.

Real-World Example: How This Applies at Companies

This decomposition pattern isn't just an interview trick — it's how real systems evolve at every successful tech company:

  • Netflix started as a monolith. As they grew, they decomposed the "streaming service" into hundreds of microservicesAn architectural style where a large application is built as a collection of small, independent services. Each service does one thing well, runs in its own process, and communicates with others via APIs. — each one independently deployable, scalable, and ownable by a team.
  • Amazon famously went from a monolithic bookstore to a service-oriented architecture. Each service (product catalog, inventory, recommendations, payments) became an independent team with its own database.
  • Uber decomposed their "ride matching" into sub-components: geospatial indexing, supply/demand prediction, route calculation, and driver dispatch — each optimized for its specific workload.

In every case, the company started simple and decomposed further as complexity grew. They followed the same spiral: understand the problem, break it down, go deeper, make technical decisions, and decompose again. The key lesson? You don't need to get the decomposition perfect on day one — you need a good enough starting point and the willingness to refine as you learn more.

Interview reality: Interviewers don't expect the perfect final-state architecture in 35 minutes. They expect you to start with a reasonable decomposition and iteratively refine it when they push back with "what if the traffic doubles?" or "what happens when this database goes down?" Your ability to evolve a design under pressure matters more than getting it "right" on the first pass.

In fact, interviewers often prefer candidates who start simple and iterate. Starting simple shows you understand progressive refinement — which is exactly how real systems are built. Amazon didn't launch with microservices. Netflix didn't start with a global CDN. They started simple and evolved. Your design should tell the same story.

When to use top-down vs. bottom-up decomposition: Use top-down when you understand the system well — you name the big pieces first and drill down. Use bottom-up when you're uncertain — start with one concrete user flow (like "user clicks a short URL"), build that end-to-end, then see what other pieces need to attach to it.

How to Know You've Built a Good System

You've followed the five steps and you have a diagram with components, data stores, and arrows connecting everything. But how do you know if it's actually good? Lots of people draw boxes and arrows. What separates a strong design from a weak one?

This is where many people stop — they draw a diagram, feel satisfied, and move on. But without evaluation, you have no idea if your design would actually work in production. There's a four-point checklist. If your design passes all four, it's solid. If it fails any one, you know exactly what to fix.

Think First

Look at this architecture: a single server handles user requests, stores data in one database, processes images, sends notifications, and serves the website. Does anything feel wrong? What would happen at 10 million users?

Think about what happens if that one server crashes, or if 10,000 people upload photos at the same time.

The "one giant server" design fails three of the four checks. It's not broken into components — everything is jammed into one process. It has no technical details per component (because there are no components). And if that server crashes, everything goes down — zero fault tolerance.

The strong design test: You should be able to point to your diagram and say: "Here are my components. Here's why each one exists. Here's the database and scaling strategy for each. Here's what happens when any single piece fails." If you can walk through all four checks without stumbling, your design is solid.

This checklist works at every scale — weekend project or global platform. It's also a great tool for evaluating existing systems when you join a new team. Now let's see what a GOOD design looks like:

Compare this to the "one giant server" design. The good design has clear separation, each piece has specific technology choices that match its needs, and every critical component has redundancy. If the upload service crashes, people can still browse their feed and send messages — those features run on different servers. If Redis goes down, the system degrades gracefullyWhen a system continues working at reduced capacity rather than completely failing. Instead of crashing, it falls back to a slower but functional alternative. Like a car running with the AC off to save fuel — not ideal, but still drivable. to hitting the database directly — response times increase from 5ms to 50ms, but nothing actually breaks. This is the hallmark of a well-designed system: individual component failures cause degradation, not catastrophe.

Interview superpower: After presenting your design, proactively run through these four checks yourself. Say: "Let me verify this design — components are separated, no overlap, each has tech details, and each can fail independently." Interviewers love self-evaluation. It shows maturity.
Section 11

Common Mistakes That Derail Your Design

Even with a solid framework, people still make predictable mistakes. The good news? These mistakes are so common and so predictable that you can learn to spot them before they happen. Once you've seen each one, you'll start noticing them everywhere — in your own designs, in technical discussions, and in other people's architecture proposals.

Here are the six most common ones — each explained with why it happens and how to avoid it.

Think First

Think about the last time you tried to design a system (or solve any large problem). Which of these six mistakes did you fall into? Be honest — recognizing your pattern is the first step to breaking it.

Most beginners are either Freezers or Deep Divers. Most intermediate engineers are Over-Engineers.
Mistake 1: Jumping to Solutions Without Understanding the Problem

What happens: The interviewer says "design a chat system" and you immediately say "we'll use WebSockets and Redis Pub/Sub." You skip Step 1 entirely.

Why it's bad: You don't know the constraints yet. Is this a 1-on-1 chat or group chat for 10,000 people? Are messages persistent or ephemeral? Do you need read receipts? Each answer changes the architecture fundamentally.

The fix: Force yourself to spend the first 3-5 minutes asking questions. Write the constraints on the whiteboard before drawing a single box. The interviewer expects this — skipping it is a red flag.

Mistake 2: Over-Engineering for Scale You Don't Have

What happens: The problem says "10 million users" and you design a system with 50 microservices, a data lake, three types of caches, and a custom consensus algorithm.

Why it's bad: Complexity has a cost. More components = more failure points, more latency, more operational overhead. Building for 1 billion users when you need 10 million is like renting a stadium for a dinner party.

The fix: Let the numbers guide you. Do the back-of-envelope math first. If a single beefy server can handle the load (and it often can for surprisingly large numbers), start there and explain how you'd evolve as traffic grows.

Mistake 3: Drawing Boxes for the Sake of It

What happens: You draw "API Gateway → Load Balancer → Web Server → Application Server → Cache → Database → CDN → Message Queue" because that's what architecture diagrams look like online.

Why it's bad: If you can't explain WHY each box exists and what happens if you remove it, you're decorating, not designing. Arpit says it directly: "Drawing boxes for the sake of it is not good."

The fix: For every box you draw, ask: "What is this box's one job?" and "What happens if I remove it?" If you can't answer both clearly, that box shouldn't be there.

Mistake 4: Ignoring What Happens When Things Fail

What happens: Your design is beautiful when everything works. But you never mention what happens when the database goes down, the network partitions, or a server crashes.

Why it's bad: In production, things WILL fail. Networks split. Disks die. Servers restart. A design that only works on a sunny day is not a design — it's a wish.

The fix: For every component, ask: "What happens if this crashes?" and "How does the system recover?" Mention replicas, retries, circuit breakers, graceful degradation. Even a one-sentence answer per component shows you think about reliability.

Mistake 5: Making Everything a Microservice

What happens: You split every single feature into its own service, even when they share a database and always deploy together. You have 20 services that could've been 5.

Why it's bad: Microservices add network calls, serialization overhead, deployment complexity, and distributed debugging nightmares. If two things always change together and always deploy together, they should BE together.

The fix: Start with fewer, larger services. Split only when you have a clear reason — different scaling needs, different teams owning them, or genuinely different deployment cycles. "Because Netflix does it" is not a reason.

Mistake 6: Skipping the Math

What happens: You say "we need sharding" without calculating whether the data even fits on one machine. You say "we need a cache" without knowing the read/write ratio.

Why it's bad: Without numbers, every decision is a guess. Maybe one PostgreSQL server handles your load fine. Maybe you DON'T need Kafka. Numbers tell you what's real and what's imaginary.

The fix: Always do a quick back-of-envelope calculation before making architectural decisions. How many requests per second? How much storage? What's the read/write ratio? A 30-second calculation can save you from building unnecessary complexity.

A Quick Self-Check

Before submitting any system design (in an interview or at work), run through this rapid-fire self-check:

Quick Self-Check (30 seconds) ✓ Did I ask about constraints before drawing? ✓ Can I explain what EACH box does in one sentence? ✓ Is every box necessary? (What breaks if I remove it?) ✓ Did I pick specific databases with reasons? ✓ What happens when my most important component crashes? ✓ Did I do any math to justify my decisions? → If you answered "no" to any of these, go back and fix it. It's worth the 2 extra minutes.

These six checks catch the vast majority of common mistakes. Make them a habit, and your designs will stand out — in interviews and in real architecture reviews.

Junior vs. senior: Everyone makes mistakes — senior engineers have just built the habit of catching them early. The difference isn't the absence of mistakes; it's the speed of detection. The self-check above is your early detection system.

Here's a useful exercise: take any system design article or YouTube video you've watched, and run their final architecture through the four evaluation checks from Section 10 and the six self-check questions above. You'll often find gaps — a great way to practice critical thinking about architectures.

The Anti-Lesson — When NOT to Use This Framework

We've spent this entire page teaching you a structured approach to system design. Now here's the twist: sometimes you shouldn't use it. Knowing when to skip the framework is just as important as knowing when to apply it.

Think First

Your friend says "I want to build a blog this weekend." Should you start by breaking it into 5 components, making 4 technical decisions per component, and evaluating with the 4-point checklist? Why or why not?

Think about the cost of over-planning vs. the cost of under-planning.

No. For a weekend blog, the framework is overkill. Just pick a tool (WordPress, Ghost, or a static site generator), deploy it, and write. The cost of getting the architecture "wrong" is near zero — you can always change it later because the scale is tiny and the stakes are low.

Here are the situations where structured decomposition does more harm than good:

  • Prototypes and MVPs — When you're testing whether anyone even wants the product, speed of iteration matters more than clean architecture. Build it ugly, learn fast, throw it away. Instagram's first version was one Python server. Twitter famously ran on Ruby on Rails. They redesigned later, after they knew the product was worth investing in.
  • Personal projects and hackathons — A weekend project doesn't need fault tolerance analysis. Just build it. If it crashes, restart it. If it can't scale, it doesn't need to — you have 3 users.
  • Single-user tools — If you're building a script for yourself (a file organizer, a scraper, a cron job), don't "design" it. Write the code. The entire system is one component with one responsibility.
  • When requirements are completely unknown — If you don't even know what the product does yet, designing the architecture is premature. Build the smallest possible thing, learn from real users, then design. Architecture follows understanding, not the other way around.

Real-World War Stories: When Over-Engineering Backfired

Premature Microservices at a Fintech Startup

A 4-person startup designed their payment system with 12 microservices from day one — each with its own database, deployment pipeline, and monitoring. They spent 6 months building infrastructure instead of features. By the time they shipped, a competitor with a monolith had captured the market.

Lesson: Microservices are a scaling strategy, not a starting strategy. Start with a monolith, split when you feel the pain.

StackOverflow Runs on 9 Web Servers

As of 2024, StackOverflow serves 1.3 billion monthly page views with just 9 web servers. Not 9,000. Not 900. Nine. No microservices. No Kubernetes. A monolithic .NET application with careful performance tuning and aggressive caching. "Boring" architecture — brilliant results.

Lesson: You almost certainly need less infrastructure than you think.

The $100,000 Kubernetes Cluster for 50 Users

A team deployed their internal tool (used by 50 people) on a full Kubernetes cluster with auto-scaling, service mesh, and distributed tracing. Monthly cloud bill: $8,000+. A single $20/month VPS could have handled the same load. They spent $100K/year on infrastructure for a tool that could run on a Raspberry Pi.

Lesson: Match your architecture to your actual scale, not your imagined future scale.
The golden rule: Use the simplest architecture that meets your current needs plus a reasonable growth margin (typically 10×). Don't design for 1 billion users when you have 1,000. When you actually hit scaling problems, you'll have revenue to fund the redesign — and you'll know exactly which parts need to change because you'll have real data, not guesses.

The Interview Angle

System design interviews typically last 35-45 minutes. That's not a lot of time to design an entire system. You can't cover everything — and that's okay. The secret is knowing how to allocate that time wisely — spending enough on each phase to demonstrate competence, without getting stuck on any single aspect — and knowing what interviewers actually care about.

The biggest surprise for most candidates? Interviewers care far more about your PROCESS than your final diagram. Two candidates can draw the same architecture, but the one who explained their reasoning, asked good questions, and discussed trade-offs will score higher every time.

Think First

You have 35 minutes to design a notification system. How would you split the time? How many minutes on understanding requirements vs. drawing the architecture vs. discussing technical details?

Hint: most candidates spend too little time on requirements and too much time drawing boxes.

The 35-Minute Breakdown

What Interviewers Actually Look For

It might surprise you, but interviewers are NOT looking for the "perfect" architecture. There is no single right answer. Here's what they actually evaluate:

  • Structured thinking — Do you follow a logical process, or do you randomly throw out ideas?
  • Trade-off awareness — Can you explain WHY you chose SQL over NoSQL, not just that you did?
  • Scope management — Can you identify what's important and what to defer? ("We can add read replicas later if needed.")
  • Communication — Can you explain your design clearly, walk through data flows, and respond to follow-up questions?
  • Depth where it matters — They'd rather see deep understanding of 2-3 key components than shallow coverage of 10.
  • Adaptability — When they change the requirements ("what if traffic doubles?"), can you adjust your design without starting over?
Notice what's NOT on this list: Memorizing exact architectures, knowing the internals of every database, or having one "correct" answer. System design is a conversation, not an exam. A candidate who designs a simple but well-reasoned system will almost always beat a candidate who draws a complicated diagram they can't explain.
What to say first: "Before I start designing, let me understand the requirements." This one sentence sets the right tone for the entire interview. It tells the interviewer you're structured, thoughtful, and not impulsive.

Power Phrases That Impress Interviewers

Experienced interviewers can tell within the first 5 minutes whether a candidate has a structured approach. Here are specific phrases you can use at each phase that immediately signal you know what you're doing:

  • During requirements: "What's the read-to-write ratio? That'll determine if I need caching." (Shows you know constraints shape architecture.)
  • During high-level design: "I'm separating these into different services because they have different scaling profilesThe pattern of how a component's resource needs grow. A read-heavy service scales differently (add caches, read replicas) than a write-heavy service (shard the database, use write-optimized storage). — one is read-heavy, the other is write-heavy." (Shows you think about WHY, not just WHAT.)
  • During deep dive: "Let me do a quick back-of-envelope calculation to see if this design holds." (Shows you make data-driven decisions.)
  • During wrap-up: "If I had more time, I'd address [X] — here's what I'd change at 10× scale." (Shows awareness of trade-offs and evolution.)

What to Save for Later

You won't have time to cover everything. Here's what to prioritize and what to mention as "future work":

  • Core components and their responsibilities
  • Data flow for the main use case (e.g., "user posts a photo" end-to-end)
  • Database choices with reasoning
  • How the system handles the most critical requirement (latency? throughput? consistency?)
  • What happens when the most important component fails
  • Caching strategy (where you'd cache and why)
  • Monitoring and alerting approach

  • Load balancing strategy
  • Data partitioning approach if scale demands it

Step 1: Understand the Problem

A URL shortener takes a long URL and gives back a short one. When someone clicks the short URL, they get redirected to the original. Simple in concept — but as we learned in Section 5, the constraints completely shape the architecture.

Following our framework, we resist the urge to draw boxes and instead start by asking questions. We learn the following constraints:

  • 100M monthly active users
  • 500M new URLs created per month
  • Each URL clicked on average 50 times over its lifetime
  • Short URLs should be as short as possible (6-8 characters)
  • URLs never expire (data grows forever)
  • 99.9% availability (about 8.7 hours of downtime per year maximum)

Already, this is vastly more useful than the vague prompt "design a URL shortener." These constraints will drive every subsequent decision. Let's do the math.

Back-of-Envelope Writes: 500M/month ÷ 30 ÷ 86,400 ≈ 200 writes/sec Reads: 500M × 50 / 30 / 86,400 ≈ 10K reads/sec Storage: 500M URLs × 500 bytes avg = 250 GB/year Read:Write ratio = 50:1 → Read-heavy. Moderate write throughput. Storage is manageable on a single machine for years.

Step 2: Break Into Components

Steps 3-4: Technical Decisions

🗄️ Database

PostgreSQLA powerful, open-source relational database known for reliability and ACID compliance. It stores data in tables with rows and columns, supports complex queries, and is the go-to choice when you need structured data with strong consistency guarantees. — the data is structured (short_code, long_url, timestamps), and we need strong consistencyA guarantee that when you write data, the next read will always see that write. For a URL shortener, this means if you create short URL "abc123", any redirect request for "abc123" must find the correct long URL — not a stale or missing result.. For the 50:1 read ratio, we add a RedisAn in-memory key-value store — it keeps all data in RAM for lightning-fast lookups (microseconds, not milliseconds). Commonly used as a cache layer in front of slower databases. cache in front. Popular links get served from cache without hitting the database.

📈 Scaling

The redirect service handles 10K reads/sec — one beefy server with Redis caching handles this easily. As traffic grows, we add read replicasCopies of the main database that handle read-only queries. The main database handles writes, then copies changes to replicas. This lets you spread read load across many machines. and more redirect servers behind a load balancer. The creation service only handles 200 writes/sec — no scaling concern for now.

⚡ Async

URL creation is synchronousThe user sends a request and waits for the response before doing anything else. The opposite of asynchronous, where the system accepts the request immediately and processes it in the background. (the user needs the short URL immediately). But analytics (counting clicks, logging geo data) can be asynchronousThe system accepts the request immediately and processes it in the background later. The user doesn't wait. Good for tasks that don't need instant results — like analytics, email sending, and data aggregation. — fire an event to a queue, process it in the background.

🔗 Communication

REST APIA common way for software to talk over the internet using standard HTTP methods (GET, POST, PUT, DELETE). REST is simple, widely understood, and works with every programming language. It's the default choice for client-to-server communication. for external clients. The redirect service reads from cache/DB — no inter-service communication needed. Analytics events go through a message queue.

Let's run the 4-point checklist:

Notice: This design evolved naturally from the constraints. We chose Redis because the math showed a 50:1 read ratio. We added a message queue because analytics processing doesn't need to block the user's redirect. Every technical choice has a reason rooted in the numbers from Step 1.

How This Design Would Evolve

No design is final. Here's what you'd add as the system grows:

  • At 10× traffic (100K reads/sec) — Add more redirect servers, introduce a CDN for popular short URLs, shard the database by short code prefix
  • At 100× traffic (1M reads/sec) — Multi-region deployment, database per region, eventually consistent global state, dedicated analytics pipeline with Kafka

The beauty of the framework is that the structure doesn't change — you just repeat Steps 3-5 on the pieces that need more attention as scale increases. The answers change as the numbers change, but the questions stay the same.

The creation service is still fine — 2,000 writes/sec is well within a single PostgreSQL's capacity. The architecture doesn't change; we just scale the pieces that need it." See how the framework makes this response smooth and natural? You're not inventing a new design — you're adjusting the numbers.

  • Timeline Service — Generate and serve the home feed. Read-heavy, latency-sensitive.
  • Follow/Social Graph Service — Manage who follows whom. Read-heavy (queried by timeline).
  • Search Service — Full-text search across tweets, users, hashtags. Read-heavy, indexing required.
  • Notification Service — Push, email, in-app alerts. Write-heavy, async.
  • DM Service — Direct messages. Real-time, bidirectional.
Exercise 2 Intermediate

Apply the 4 Technical Decisions to a Chat Service

You have a "Message Service" component for a chat application (like WhatsApp). For this single component, make all four technical decisions: database, scaling, async processing, and communication protocol. Justify each choice.

Hint

Chat messages are write-heavy (every message is a write) but also need fast reads (loading chat history). Messages are append-only — you never update an existing message (usually). Think about what database handles append-heavy workloads well. For communication, users need to see messages instantly — what protocol supports that?

Sample Answer
  • Database: Cassandra or DynamoDB — append-heavy workload, partition by conversation_id for fast retrieval. Recent messages cached in Redis for instant loading.
  • Scaling: Horizontal — partition messages by conversation ID across multiple nodes. Replication factor of 3 for fault tolerance.
  • Async: Message delivery is synchronous (user expects instant delivery). But read receipts, typing indicators, and message indexing can be async via a message queue.
  • Communication: WebSocket for real-time bidirectional messaging. Internal services communicate via gRPC for low-latency typed communication.
Exercise 3 Intermediate

Find the Flaws in This Design

A colleague presents this design for a food delivery app: one monolithic server handles orders, payments, restaurant menus, driver matching, and live tracking. It connects to a single MySQL database. All processing is synchronous. "It works in testing!" they say.

Using the 4-point evaluation checklist, identify every flaw and propose improvements.

Sample Answer
  • Check 1 (Components): FAIL — Everything in one monolith. Payment processing has completely different requirements than live tracking. Split into at least: Order, Payment, Restaurant, Driver Matching, Live Tracking.
  • Check 2 (Clear Roles): PARTIAL FAIL — The monolith mixes responsibilities. If payment code has a bug, it could crash live tracking.
  • Check 3 (Tech Details): FAIL — Single MySQL for everything. Live tracking needs Redis (write-heavy, ephemeral). Orders need ACID. Restaurant menus rarely change (cache-friendly). One database for all is wrong.
  • Check 4 (Scalable/Fault Tolerant): FAIL — Single server + single database = single point of failure. No replicas, no failover, no horizontal scaling.

Proposed fix: Split into 5 services. Give Payment its own PostgreSQL database with strict ACID. Give Live Tracking a Redis instance for ephemeral location data. Put each service behind a load balancer with at least 2 instances. Make driver matching async via a message queue.

Exercise 4 Advanced

Full Design: Pastebin (20-Minute Timer)

Set a 20-minute timer. Design Pastebin (a tool where users paste text and get a shareable link) using all 5 steps. Constraints: 10M daily active users, average paste size 10KB, pastes can be public or private, pastes expire after 6 months by default.

Write down: back-of-envelope math, component breakdown, database choices with justification, scaling strategy, and what happens when your most important component fails. Aim to fill at least one full page.

Key Points to Hit

This is similar to a URL shortener but with larger payloads (10KB vs. 500 bytes) and an expiration requirement.

  • Components: Paste Creation Service, Paste Retrieval Service, Paste Storage (object store for content + metadata DB), Expiration Service (background job that deletes expired pastes)
  • Database: PostgreSQL for metadata, S3 or blob storage for paste content (10KB × 10M = 100GB/day — too much for a DB)
  • Scaling: Read-heavy (like URL shortener). CDN for public pastes. Redis cache for hot pastes.
  • Failure: If metadata DB goes down, use read replicas. If S3 goes down (rare), serve from cache. Expiration service can run behind — better to serve an expired paste than lose it.
Section 16

Quick Reference — The Cheat Card

Here's the entire framework distilled onto one screen. Bookmark this section — it's the one you'll come back to before every practice session, every interview prep, and every real-world architecture discussion. The goal is for these steps, decisions, and checks to become second nature.

The 5 Steps

  1. Understand — Ask questions, get constraints, do the math
  2. Break down — 4-7 components, mutually exclusive roles
  3. Go deeper — Data flow, APIs, storage per component
  4. 4 decisions — DB, scaling, async, communication
  5. Decompose — Split vague pieces further, repeat steps 3-4

The 4 Technical Decisions

  • 🗄️ Database & Caching — SQL vs NoSQL vs cache
  • 📈 Scaling & Fault Tolerance — Horizontal, replicas, failover
  • ⚡ Async Processing — Sync if user waits, async if not
  • 🔗 Communication — REST, gRPC, WebSocket, queues

The 4 Evaluation Checks

  • ✅ Broken into clear components
  • ✅ Mutually exclusive responsibilities
  • ✅ Technical details per component
  • ✅ Scalable + fault tolerant + available

The Framework at Different Experience Levels

The same framework works at every level — what changes is how deep you go at each step:

What you're expected to do:

  • Ask clarifying questions about requirements (Step 1)
  • Break the system into 4-6 logical components (Step 2)
  • Explain what each component does and how data flows (Step 3)
  • Pick a database type (SQL vs NoSQL) with a basic reason (Step 4)
  • Mention that you'd add caching and load balancing as the system grows

What you're NOT expected to do: Detailed capacity planning, specific technology comparisons, multi-region architectures, or handling every edge case.

What you're expected to do:

  • Everything from Junior, plus:
  • Back-of-envelope calculations to justify decisions
  • Specific database choices with trade-off reasoning ("PostgreSQL for ACID; Cassandra for write-heavy")
  • Identify which operations should be async vs. sync
  • Discuss failure scenarios and how the system recovers
  • Mention monitoring and alerting at a high level

What you're expected to do:

  • Everything from Mid-Level, plus:
  • Deep-dive into the most critical component's internals
  • Discuss data consistency models (strong, eventual, causal)
  • Design for multi-region deployment and disaster recovery
  • Cost analysis: "This architecture costs ~$X/month on AWS"
  • Proactively identify bottlenecks before being asked
  • Discuss evolution: "Here's what changes at 10× and 100× current scale"

No matter your level, the 5-step framework is the same backbone. You just fill in more detail as you learn more foundation topics. This is why System Guide teaches foundations before case studies — each foundation topic adds a new tool to your belt.

Where to focus based on your level:
  • Just starting? Focus on being a confident "Junior" first. Nail the basics: ask good questions, draw clear components, explain data flow. The framework scales with your knowledge.
  • Already mid-level? Focus on the areas where mid and senior diverge: back-of-envelope math, specific technology comparisons, failure scenario analysis, and multi-region considerations.
Remember: System design is not about memorizing architectures. It's about having a repeatable process that works for ANY problem. The framework never changes — only the details do. Master the process, and you'll never freeze at the whiteboard again.
