Networking Foundations

TCP & UDP

Two ways to send data across the internet — one obsesses over reliability, the other obsesses over speed. Every system you'll ever design picks one (or both).

Section 1

TL;DR — The One-Minute Version

Mental Model: TCP is registered mail — you get a tracking number, delivery confirmation, and they'll re-send it if it gets lost. UDP is shouting across a room — fast, no paperwork, but if someone doesn't hear you, too bad.

Every single thing you do on the internet — loading a webpage, sending a message, streaming a video, playing an online game — involves chopping your data into small pieces called packetsA small chunk of data (usually 1,000–1,500 bytes) that travels independently across the network. Like an individual envelope in a stack of mail. and shipping them across the network. The question is: how carefully do you want those packets delivered?

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) says: "I will make absolutely sure every single packet arrives, in the right order, with nothing missing or corrupted. If something gets lost, I'll re-send it. This takes a bit longer, but you'll get perfect data." UDP (User Datagram Protocol) says: "I'll fire packets as fast as I can. If some get lost along the way, that's the cost of speed. You'll get data fast, even if it's not perfect."

One-line takeaway: Use TCP when every byte matters (downloading files, loading webpages, sending money). Use UDP when speed matters more than perfection (video calls, gaming, live streams).
Section 2

Why You Need This — The Scenario

Imagine two situations happening at the same time in the same house:

Situation A: You're downloading your bank statement as a PDF. The file is 2 MB. If even one byte is wrong, the PDF won't open. If the bytes arrive out of order, the file is garbage. You need every single piece, in exactly the right order, and you're willing to wait an extra second or two for that guarantee.

Situation B: Your partner is on a video call with a friend. They're laughing, chatting, showing off the dog. Every now and then, the video freezes for a split second or a word gets clipped. Do they care? Not really — the conversation keeps flowing. But imagine if the video paused completely every time a single packet went missing, waited for the network to re-send it, then resumed. The call would be unwatchable. Constant stuttering. Five-second freezes. Your partner would hang up.

These two situations perfectly capture why both TCP and UDP exist. They solve fundamentally different problems. And every system you'll ever design — whether it's a chat app, a payment service, a game server, or a video platform — has to make this exact choice: do I care more about getting perfect data, or fast data?

Section 3

The First Attempt — Just Send the Data

Let's pretend we're designing the internet from scratch. Computer A wants to send a message to Computer B. The simplest possible approach? Just fire the data across the wire. No setup, no confirmation, no tracking. Like tossing a paper airplane across the room and hoping it lands on the right desk.

This is basically how the very earliest network experiments worked, and it's essentially what UDP still does today (with a tiny bit more structure). And honestly? For some things, this "fire and forget" approach works great. DNS lookups, for example — you send a quick question ("what's the IP address for google.com?"), get a quick answer, and move on. If the answer doesn't come back, you just ask again.

But the moment you try to use this for anything serious — downloading a file, loading a webpage, sending a payment — things fall apart spectacularly. Here's why.

Section 5

The Breakthrough — Two Tools for Two Jobs

The designers of the internet had a brilliant insight: don't force one protocol to do everything. Instead, build two protocols that sit on top of the raw network (called IPInternet Protocol — the base layer of the internet. IP handles addressing (where to send) and routing (how to get there), but makes zero promises about reliability. Packets can be lost, duplicated, or reordered.) and let the application choose which one fits:

TCP adds a whole reliability system on top of IP. Before sending any data, TCP makes both computers agree to a connection (like a phone call — you dial, the other person picks up, then you talk). During the transfer, TCP numbers every byte, checks for errors, re-sends anything lost, and puts everything back in order. It's more work, but the application above gets a perfect stream of data with zero effort.

UDP adds almost nothing on top of IP. It slaps on a port number (so your computer knows which application the data is for) and a basic checksumA small value calculated from the data, used to verify nothing was corrupted during transfer. Like adding up all the digits in a credit card number — if the total doesn't match, something's wrong. (to catch obvious corruption), and sends the data immediately. No connection setup, no waiting for confirmation, no re-sending lost packets. If the application needs any of that stuff, it has to build it itself.

The key insight: TCP and UDP aren't competing protocols — they're different tools for different jobs. HTTP chose TCP because web pages need every byte. DNS chose UDP because a quick question-and-answer doesn't need a full connection. Video calling apps use UDP because a missing frame is better than a frozen screen.

Now let's dig into exactly how each protocol works, starting with TCP's clever bag of tricks.

Section 6

How It Works — The Mechanics

TCP has six major mechanisms that turn an unreliable network into a reliable data stream. Think of them as a team — each one solves a specific problem we identified in Section 4. Then we'll look at UDP's intentional simplicity.

🤝 The 3-Way Handshake — "Let's Agree to Talk"

Before sending a single byte of real data, TCP makes both computers confirm they're ready. It's like calling someone on the phone: you dial (ring ring), they pick up ("hello?"), and you say "hey, it's me" — now you both know the connection is live.

The handshake has three steps, each using a special flag in the TCP header:

Those numbers (sequence numbersEach side picks a random starting number. Every byte of data sent gets a sequence number so the receiver knows the exact order and can detect gaps. Think of it like numbering pages in a book.) are crucial. Each side picks a random starting number, and from then on, every byte of data gets numbered. This is how TCP detects missing data and puts things back in order.

Why random starting numbers? If TCP always started at 0, an old packet from a previous connection could arrive late and be mistaken for new data. Random starting numbers make that almost impossible. This also provides a basic defense against attackers guessing sequence numbers to inject fake data.

🔢 Sequence Numbers — "What's the Order?"

Remember the reordering problem from Section 4? Packets can arrive out of order because they take different routes through the network. TCP solves this by stamping every byte with a sequence number. The receiver uses these numbers like page numbers in a book — even if page 5 arrives before page 3, you know exactly where each one goes.

The receiver holds a buffer where it slots incoming data by sequence number. If packet 200 arrives before packet 100, no panic — it just waits, slots each one into the right position, and delivers a clean, ordered stream to the application above.

✅ ACKs & Retransmission — "Did You Get That?"

This is the core of TCP's reliability. Every time the receiver gets data, it sends back a small message called an ACKShort for "acknowledgment." The receiver's way of saying "I got everything up to byte X." If the sender doesn't hear an ACK within a timeout, it assumes the data was lost and re-sends it. (acknowledgment) — essentially saying "I got everything up to byte number X, send me the next part."

If the sender doesn't receive an ACK within a certain time (the retransmission timeoutA timer the sender sets after sending data. If no ACK arrives before the timer expires, TCP assumes the data was lost and sends it again. The timeout adapts — it gets longer on lossy networks and shorter on fast ones.), it assumes something went wrong and re-sends the data. This is how TCP solves packet loss — by detecting silence and trying again.

🚰 Flow Control — "Slow Down, I Can't Keep Up!"

Imagine pouring water into a glass. If you pour faster than someone can drink, the glass overflows. The same thing happens with data: if the sender blasts data faster than the receiver can process it, the receiver's buffer fills up and data gets dropped.

TCP solves this with a receive windowA number the receiver includes in every ACK, telling the sender "I have this many bytes of free buffer space." The sender must never have more unacknowledged data in flight than the receiver's window allows.. In every ACK, the receiver tells the sender: "I have X bytes of free buffer space." The sender never sends more unacknowledged data than that window allows. If the receiver gets busy (maybe the application is slow to read the data), the window shrinks. If the receiver catches up, the window grows.

This prevents a fast server from overwhelming a slow client (like a phone on a weak Wi-Fi connection). The beauty is that it's completely automatic — neither the sender nor receiver application has to do anything.

🚦 Congestion Control — "The Network Is Crowded"

Flow control prevents overwhelming the receiver. Congestion control prevents overwhelming the network itself. Think of it like traffic management on a highway. Even if the destination parking lot has plenty of space (big receive window), the highway between you and the destination might be jammed.

TCP starts by sending data slowly — just a few packets — and gradually speeds up. This phase is called slow startTCP's algorithm for probing network capacity. It starts with a small congestion window (usually 10 packets) and doubles it every round trip until it detects congestion. Despite the name, it actually ramps up exponentially — which is quite fast.. Every time the sender gets an ACK, it doubles the amount of data it sends (1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 16). This ramps up quickly. But the moment TCP detects a packet loss (which it interprets as "the network is full"), it dramatically cuts back and then increases more cautiously.

This back-and-forth — ramp up, detect congestion, cut back, ramp up more slowly — is what keeps the internet from collapsing. Every TCP connection independently adjusts its speed, and together they share the available bandwidth fairly (mostly).

👋 Connection Teardown + UDP's Simplicity

When both sides are done exchanging data, TCP cleanly closes the connection with a 4-step process (FIN → ACK → FIN → ACK). This makes sure both sides know the conversation is over and no data is left in transit.

Now compare all of this machinery to UDP. UDP has... none of it. No handshake. No sequence numbers. No ACKs. No retransmission. No flow control. No congestion control. No teardown. The entire UDP "protocol" adds just 8 bytes of header to your data: source port, destination port, length, and checksum. That's it.

UDP is intentionally simple. It gets your data out the door with the absolute minimum overhead. If you need reliability on top of UDP, you build it yourself in your application. And some applications do exactly that — QUIC (which powers HTTP/3) is built on top of UDP but adds its own reliability layer. We'll explore that in Section 8.

Think First

TCP needs a 3-way handshake before sending data and a 4-way teardown to close. That's 7 round trips of overhead just for setup and cleanup. If you only need to send one tiny message (like a DNS query), is TCP's overhead worth it?

This is exactly why DNS uses UDP by default. One question, one answer — no connection needed.
Section 7

Going Deeper — The Clever Details

Now that you understand the big picture, let's explore four advanced TCP mechanisms that come up in system design interviews and real-world debugging. Each one is a clever optimization that solves a subtle problem.

🪟 Sliding Window — Send Multiple Packets at Once

Imagine you're sending a letter and you can only send one at a time, waiting for a reply before sending the next. That would be painfully slow — especially if the recipient is far away. TCP's sliding windowA mechanism that lets the sender have multiple packets "in flight" simultaneously without waiting for individual ACKs. The window size determines how many bytes can be sent ahead before pausing for acknowledgments. solves this by allowing multiple packets to be "in flight" at the same time.

The window "slides" forward as ACKs arrive. The bandwidth-delay productThe amount of data that can be "in transit" on a network link at any moment. Calculated as: bandwidth × round-trip time. A 1 Gbps link with 50ms RTT can hold ~6.25 MB of data in flight. The window needs to be at least this big to fully utilize the link. determines the ideal window size: bandwidth × round-trip time. A high-bandwidth, high-latency link (like a satellite connection) needs a very large window to stay busy.

📦 Nagle's Algorithm — Batch Small Messages

Here's a fun problem: you're typing in a terminal connected to a remote server. Each keystroke is just 1 byte. But TCP has a 20-byte header (plus IP's 20-byte header). So you're sending 41 bytes to deliver 1 byte of actual data. That's 97.5% overhead. Multiply that by thousands of users and you're wasting enormous bandwidth on headers.

Nagle's algorithmA TCP optimization that batches small outgoing messages. Instead of sending each tiny piece immediately, it waits until it has enough data to fill a reasonable packet, or until it receives an ACK for the previous data. Reduces header overhead dramatically. fixes this by batching: if there's already unacknowledged data in flight, hold back small new messages and combine them into one bigger packet. Once the ACK comes back, send the accumulated data as a single packet.

The catch: Nagle's algorithm adds latency to small messages (it's waiting to batch). For real-time applications like games or trading platforms, this delay is unacceptable. That's why many performance-sensitive applications disable it with the TCP_NODELAY socket option — sending every piece immediately, even if it's tiny.

⚡ TCP Fast Open — Skip the Wait

The 3-way handshake adds one full round-trip timeThe time for a packet to travel from sender to receiver and back. Often abbreviated RTT. A typical RTT is 1-5ms on a local network, 20-50ms within a region, and 100-300ms across continents. (RTT) of latency before any data can be sent. On a mobile connection with 100ms RTT, that's 100ms of staring at a blank screen before any content starts flowing. For a page that makes 10 separate TCP connections, that's a full second of just handshakes.

TCP Fast Open (TFO) lets the client embed data in the very first SYN packet — skipping the wait. On the first connection, the server gives the client a special cookie. On subsequent connections, the client presents the cookie in its SYN and includes the HTTP request right there. The server can start processing immediately.

🚧 Head-of-Line Blocking — TCP's Achilles Heel

This is TCP's biggest weakness, and it's the main reason HTTP/3 moved to UDP. Here's the problem: TCP guarantees that data arrives in order. That sounds great — until one packet gets lost.

Imagine you're loading a webpage. The browser is downloading images, CSS, and JavaScript over one TCP connection. Packet 5 contains part of an image. Packet 6 contains CSS. Packet 7 contains JavaScript. If packet 5 gets lost, TCP blocks everything — packets 6 and 7 sit in the receiver's buffer, waiting for packet 5 to be retransmitted. The CSS and JavaScript are right there, ready to go, but TCP won't deliver them out of order.

This single problem — head-of-line blocking — motivated the entire development of QUIC and HTTP/3. It's a great example of how a protocol's strengths (ordering guarantee) can become its weakness in certain use cases.

TCP and UDP are the classic duo, but the networking world hasn't stood still. Let's see how they compare and what modern alternatives have emerged.

QUIC is a modern transport protocol developed by Google and now standardized as the foundation for HTTP/3The third major version of HTTP. Unlike HTTP/1.1 and HTTP/2 which use TCP, HTTP/3 runs on QUIC (which uses UDP underneath). This eliminates head-of-line blocking and reduces connection setup time.. It's built on top of UDP but adds reliability features that are smarter than TCP's.

Why QUIC over TCP?

  • 0-RTT connection setup — QUIC combines the transport handshake and TLS handshake into one step. On repeat connections, data can flow on the very first packet.
  • No head-of-line blocking — Independent streams mean a lost packet on one stream doesn't stall the others.
  • Connection migration — Switch from Wi-Fi to 4G mid-connection without dropping it (TCP connections die when your IP changes).
  • Built-in encryption — TLS 1.3 is mandatory, not optional. Even the headers are encrypted.

SCTP (Stream Control Transmission Protocol) is a lesser-known protocol that was designed for telecom signaling. It has some unique features:

  • Multi-homing — A single connection can span multiple IP addresses. If one network path fails, traffic seamlessly moves to another.
  • Multi-streaming — Like QUIC, SCTP supports independent streams within one connection, avoiding head-of-line blocking.
  • Message-oriented — Unlike TCP's byte stream, SCTP preserves message boundaries (like UDP) while providing reliability (like TCP).

In practice, SCTP is rarely used on the public internet because many NATNetwork Address Translation — what your home router does to share one public IP address among multiple devices. NAT devices understand TCP and UDP but often don't know how to handle SCTP, blocking it entirely. devices and firewalls don't support it. But it's important in telecom (4G/5G core networks use SCTP extensively).

Think First

If QUIC is built on top of UDP, and QUIC adds its own reliability... why not just use TCP? What does building on UDP give QUIC that TCP couldn't?

Think about who controls the protocol implementation. TCP is in the operating system kernel. UDP is too, but QUIC runs in userspace (the application). What's easier to update — the OS kernel or an app?

The answer is deployability. TCP is baked into every operating system kernel. Changing TCP behavior requires OS updates across billions of devices — a process that takes years or decades. QUIC runs in userspace (inside Chrome, inside nginx) and can be updated with a browser update or server deploy. Google can ship QUIC improvements to 3 billion Chrome users in weeks.

Section 9

At Scale — How the Giants Use TCP & UDP

Theory is great, but let's see how real companies make these decisions at massive scale. Each of these stories reinforces the core trade-off: reliability vs speed.

🎮 Multiplayer Gaming — Why Every Millisecond Counts

In a fast-paced shooter like Fortnite or Valorant, the game server sends position updates 30-128 times per second. Each update contains: where every player is, what they're doing, and what projectiles are in flight. If one of these updates gets lost, what should happen?

If you used TCP, the game would freeze while waiting for the lost packet to be retransmitted. By the time it arrives, the data is stale anyway — the player has already moved. So gaming uses UDP. If update #47 is lost, the game just uses update #48 when it arrives. The player might see a tiny teleport, but the game keeps running smoothly.

🎬 Netflix & YouTube — Streaming Over TCP (Surprising!)

Here's a surprise: Netflix and YouTube actually use TCP for video streaming, not UDP. Why? Because they don't stream video in real-time — they buffer ahead. When you watch a Netflix show, the player downloads the next 30-60 seconds of video ahead of time. This buffer absorbs any TCP retransmission delays.

The math works out: if your buffer has 30 seconds of video and a TCP retransmission takes 200ms, you'll never notice. The player keeps playing from the buffer while TCP sorts out the lost packet in the background. And since the video file needs to arrive perfectly (a corrupted video frame shows ugly artifacts), TCP's reliability guarantee is actually valuable here.

The distinction: Netflix/YouTube use TCP because they're delivering pre-recorded content with buffering. Zoom/FaceTime use UDP because they're doing real-time communication where a 200ms delay is painful. Same "video" — completely different requirements.

🎙️ Discord — A Hybrid Approach

Discord is a great example of using both protocols in the same app. Text messages, friend lists, and server data all go over TCP (via WebSocket) because every message must arrive perfectly and in order — you can't have chat messages disappearing or arriving scrambled.

But voice chat runs over UDP. When you're in a Discord voice channel with friends, the audio packets fly via UDP. If a packet gets lost, you might hear a tiny click or millisecond gap — but the conversation keeps flowing without freezing.

Section 10

The Anti-Lesson — When People Choose Wrong

Knowing when to use TCP vs UDP is important. Knowing when people use them wrong is even more valuable. Here are real mistakes that cost companies real money.

Mistake #1: Using TCP for real-time gaming and wondering why it's laggy. A team built a multiplayer mobile game using TCP because "reliability is always better." Every time a player on a flaky mobile connection lost a packet, the game froze for 200-500ms waiting for retransmission. Players on the subway (where packet loss hits 5-10%) reported the game was "unplayable." The fix: switch to UDP for game state, keep TCP for chat and leaderboards.
Mistake #2: Using UDP for financial transactions. A fintech startup built their order-matching engine with UDP for "maximum speed." It worked great on their local test network (0% packet loss). In production, across data centers with occasional congestion, orders were silently lost. A customer's $50,000 sell order vanished into the void. They rebuilt on TCP within a week.
Mistake #3: "UDP is always faster than TCP." A team migrated their REST API from TCP to a custom UDP protocol to "reduce latency." They then spent 6 months rebuilding reliability (retransmission, ordering, flow control) on top of UDP — badly reinventing what TCP already does well. Their custom protocol had more bugs and was slower than TCP because TCP's implementation in the OS kernel is highly optimized after 40 years of tuning.

The default choice for almost everything is TCP. HTTP, gRPC, database connections, message queues, email, file transfer — all TCP. UDP is the exception, not the rule. If you find yourself reaching for UDP, make sure you have a clear reason why TCP's overhead is genuinely unacceptable for your use case.

Section 11

Common Mistakes — What People Get Wrong

These are the misconceptions that trip up engineers in interviews and production alike. Each one sounds reasonable until you look closer.

Myth: "UDP is faster than TCP"

The truth: UDP has less overhead than TCP, which is not the same as "faster." On a reliable network (like a data center with 0.001% packet loss), TCP's throughput can match or exceed a naive UDP implementation. TCP has 40+ years of kernel-level optimizations — things like scatter-gather I/OA technique where the network card can assemble packets from multiple memory locations without copying data, significantly reducing CPU overhead for large transfers., hardware offloading, and segmentation offload that hand-rolled UDP rarely matches.

UDP's latency advantage only materializes when packet loss occurs and you'd rather skip a lost packet than wait for retransmission. For raw throughput on a clean network, TCP often wins.

Myth: "TCP guarantees delivery"

The truth: TCP guarantees that data arrives in order and without corruption, or the connection is dropped entirely. If the network is completely down, TCP doesn't magically deliver your data — it retries for a while and then gives up with a connection reset. "Reliable" means "it will tell you if it can't deliver," not "delivery is guaranteed under all conditions."

Myth: "HTTP uses TCP, so all web traffic is TCP"

The truth: HTTP/1.1 and HTTP/2 use TCP. But HTTP/3 uses QUIC, which runs on UDP. As of 2025, roughly 30% of web traffic runs on HTTP/3/QUIC. So a significant chunk of "web traffic" is actually UDP at the transport layer. Google, Cloudflare, and Meta are driving this adoption hard.

Myth: "You can't build reliable protocols on UDP"

The truth: You absolutely can, and major protocols do. QUIC (HTTP/3), WebRTC (video calling), and custom game protocols all build reliability on top of UDP. The difference is that you control the reliability logic instead of the OS kernel. This lets you make smarter choices — like only retransmitting the specific stream that lost data, instead of blocking everything like TCP does.

Myth: "TCP connections are expensive"

The truth: The 3-way handshake adds one RTT of latency, but modern optimizations reduce this cost. Connection pooling reuses established connections across requests. TCP Fast Open eliminates the handshake on repeat connections. HTTP/2 multiplexing sends multiple requests over one connection. And keep-alive prevents connections from closing between requests. In practice, a well-configured system rarely pays the handshake cost.

Myth: "Video streaming should always use UDP"

The truth: As we saw with Netflix and YouTube, pre-recorded video streaming works perfectly well over TCP because the buffer absorbs retransmission delays. Only real-time video (video calls, live auctions, surgery telepresence) needs UDP's low latency. Many live streaming platforms use HLSHTTP Live Streaming — Apple's protocol for adaptive video streaming. It breaks video into small chunks (2-10 seconds each) served over plain HTTP/TCP. The player can switch quality levels based on available bandwidth. over TCP for their live broadcasts too, accepting 5-15 seconds of delay in exchange for simpler infrastructure.

Section 12

Interview Playbook — How to Talk About TCP & UDP

TCP and UDP come up in virtually every system design interview — not as the main topic, but as a decision you need to justify when designing any distributed system. Here's how to handle it at each level.

What they'll ask: "What's the difference between TCP and UDP?"

What to say:

"TCP is a reliable, connection-based protocol — it sets up a connection with a 3-way handshake, numbers every byte, acknowledges received data, and retransmits anything that gets lost. This guarantees data arrives in order and complete, which is why HTTP, database connections, and file transfers use it.

UDP is minimal — no connection setup, no reliability, no ordering. It just fires packets and moves on. This makes it faster for real-time use cases like gaming, voice calls, and DNS lookups where speed matters more than perfect delivery."

Bonus points: Mention that it's not binary — many modern systems use both (e.g., "Discord uses TCP for chat and UDP for voice").

What they'll ask: "You're designing a chat system. Should messages go over TCP or UDP?"

What to say:

"Text messages absolutely need TCP — or more specifically, WebSocket over TCP. Messages must arrive in order, completely, and exactly once. Losing a chat message is unacceptable. The slight overhead of TCP is negligible for text data.

However, if we add voice/video calling to the chat app, those media streams should go over UDP. A dropped audio frame causes a tiny click; waiting 200ms for a retransmission causes an awkward pause. We'd use WebRTC, which runs on UDP with its own selective reliability."

Then go deeper: Mention connection pooling for efficiency, how WebSocket maintains a persistent TCP connection to avoid repeated handshakes, and how HTTP/2 multiplexes multiple logical streams over one TCP connection.

What they'll ask: "How does your choice of transport protocol affect the architecture of a live streaming platform?"

What to say:

"It depends on the latency requirements. For a platform like Twitch where 3-5 seconds of delay is acceptable, I'd use HLS or DASH over HTTP/TCP. The buffer absorbs retransmission delays, and TCP-based delivery works with CDNs, proxies, and standard HTTP infrastructure without modification.

For ultra-low-latency use cases — live auctions, sports betting, interactive streaming — I'd use WebRTC (UDP-based) or QUIC. Sub-second latency requires accepting that some frames may be lost rather than waiting for retransmission.

At the architecture level, the choice cascades: TCP-based streaming works with existing CDN infrastructure. UDP-based streaming requires specialized relay servers (SFUs), makes it harder to cache/proxy, and means you need to handle packet loss gracefully in the player.

I'd also consider HTTP/3 (QUIC) as a middle ground — it gives us TCP-like reliability without head-of-line blocking, and connection migration handles users switching networks."

Senior signal: Show that the transport protocol choice affects the entire architecture, not just a single connection. Talk about how it impacts CDN strategy, caching, load balancing, failover, and observability.

This is essentially how Valve's Steamworks networking library works.

Exercise 4: Diagnose the Bottleneck Intermediate

A file transfer between two data centers (100 Gbps link, 20ms RTT) is only achieving 50 Mbps throughput. The network shows 0% packet loss. What's the most likely cause? How would you fix it?

Show Answer

The TCP window is too small. The bandwidth-delay product is 100 Gbps × 20ms = 250 MB. The default TCP window on many systems is 64 KB (without window scaling) or a few MB. At 64 KB window: max throughput = 64 KB / 20ms = ~26 Mbps. Fix: enable TCP window scaling and increase the send/receive buffer sizes (net.core.rmem_max and net.core.wmem_max on Linux) to at least match the bandwidth-delay product.

Exercise 5: HTTP/3 Migration Advanced

Your company's web application currently runs on HTTP/2 over TCP. Your CTO wants to migrate to HTTP/3 (QUIC). List three benefits you'd gain, two potential challenges, and one scenario where staying on HTTP/2 might actually be better.

Show Answer

Benefits: (1) No head-of-line blocking between streams, (2) faster connection setup (0-RTT on repeat visits), (3) connection migration for mobile users switching networks.

Challenges: (1) Some corporate firewalls and networks block UDP traffic — you need TCP fallback, (2) observability tooling (packet captures, monitoring) is less mature for QUIC since headers are encrypted.

Stay on HTTP/2: If your users are primarily on corporate networks with strict firewalls that block UDP, or if your infrastructure relies heavily on TCP-level inspection/load balancing that doesn't support QUIC yet.
