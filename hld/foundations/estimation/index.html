Section 1

TL;DR — The One-Minute Version

Mental Model: Estimation is a bullshit detector. In 30 seconds of multiplication, you can tell whether an idea is brilliant or broken — before writing a single line of code.

The entire skill boils down to one sentence: figure out how many users you have, what each one does, and how big each action is. Multiply those three numbers together. That's it. That answer tells you how many servers you need, how much storage to buy, and whether your architecture will survive its first real traffic spike.

You don't need to be exact. Nobody expects you to nail the number down to the byte. What matters is getting within the right order of magnitudeA factor of 10. Being 'off by an order of magnitude' means your guess was 10× too high or too low. — knowing whether you need gigabytes or petabytes, 100 servers or 10,000. Being off by 2x is fine. Being off by 1000x is a career-ending architecture mistake.

One-line takeaway: Estimation isn't about precision — it's about preventing disasters. Thirty seconds of multiplication can save you from building a system that crashes on day one.
What top companies actually expect: At Google, interviewers expect you to start estimating within the first 5 minutes — before you draw a single box. At Amazon, they literally call it "napkin math" and treat it as a leadership principle (Insist on the Highest Standards — you can't set standards without numbers). At Netflix, they want you to identify the bottleneck from the numbers — storage? bandwidth? CPU? The estimate should drive every design decision that follows.
Section 2

Why You Need This

Picture this. You're in a system design interview. The interviewer says: "Design a chat app like WhatsApp." You start drawing boxes — a client, a server, a database. You're feeling good. Then the interviewer leans forward and asks:

"How much storage do you need per day?"

Silence. You have no idea. You stammer, "Uh... probably a lot?" The interviewer nods politely, but you can see them writing something on their notepad. It's not a good something.

Here's the thing: that question wasn't a trick. The interviewer doesn't have an exact number in mind either. They wanted to see you think with numbers. They wanted you to say something like: "WhatsApp has about 2 billion users. Let's say 1 billion are active daily. If each sends 40 messages a day, and each message is about 100 bytes of text... that's 4 trillion bytes. About 4 terabytes per day, just for message content." That takes 20 seconds, and it completely changes the conversation.

Section 3

The First Attempt — Guessing

Here's what most people do when asked "how much storage?" or "how many requests per second?" — they guess. "Probably a terabyte?" "Maybe a thousand requests per second?" It sounds reasonable in the moment. The problem is, guesses are almost always wildly wrong — and not just a little wrong, but orders-of-magnitude wrong.

An order of magnitude just means "a factor of 10." If the real answer is 10 TB and you guess 10 GB, you're off by three orders of magnitude — you're off by 1000x. That's the difference between needing one server and needing a thousand servers. That's the difference between a $500/month cloud bill and a $500,000/month cloud bill.

The interviewer doesn't expect an exact number. Nobody can tell you the precise storage requirement down to the byte. What they DO expect is that you can reason your way to the right ballpark. "About 10 TB per day" is a perfect answer. "Probably a lot" is not.

Here's why guessing is dangerous beyond interviews: in real engineering, a wrong estimate doesn't just lose you interview points — it loses money. A team that guesses "probably 1,000 requests per second" and builds for that, when the actual traffic is 50,000 requests per second, will watch their system crash on launch day. Every recovery is expensive, rushed, and stressful.

The good news: You don't need to memorize a million numbers. You just need a simple method — a recipe — that turns any "how much?" question into a chain of multiplications you can do on the back of an envelope. That's exactly what the next sections teach you.

Sanity-Check Your Guesses with Real Cloud Pricing

Here's a trick that separates good estimators from great ones: verify your estimate against real-world costs. If you estimated that WhatsApp needs 10 TB/day of message storage, you can check if that "smells right" using actual AWS pricing:

WhatsApp Storage Cost Sanity Check 10 TB/day × 365 days = 3.65 PB/year AWS S3 Standard: $0.023 per GB/month 3.65 PB = 3,650 TB = 3,650,000 GB 3,650,000 GB × $0.023 = ~$83,950/month just for storage Does that sound reasonable? Yes — Meta (WhatsApp's parent) spent $32 billion on infrastructure in 2023. $84K/month for message storage is a rounding error for them. → If your estimate implied $500M/month for text storage alone, you'd know something was off.

You can also cross-reference estimates against public company filings. Twitter's (now X's) Q4 2022 10-K filing showed ~$1.2 billion in annual infrastructure costs. If your estimate for Twitter's storage + compute costs came to $50 billion/year, that's a red flag — you're off by 40x. If it came to $800 million, you're in the right ballpark. Public filings are your estimation answer key.

Quick reference — real cloud prices for estimation (2024 rates):
• AWS S3 storage: $0.023/GB/month (~$23/TB/month)
• AWS EC2 (c5.xlarge, 4 vCPU): ~$0.17/hour (~$124/month)
• AWS data transfer out: $0.09/GB (first 10 TB), drops at volume
• AWS RDS (db.r5.xlarge, 4 vCPU, 32 GB RAM): ~$0.48/hour (~$350/month)
Memorize these four numbers. They let you convert any estimate into a dollar figure — which is what your boss and the interviewer really care about.
Section 4

What Happens Without Math

Let's look at what actually happens when teams skip estimation and just... build things based on vibes.

The Launch Day Disaster

A team builds a new social feature. They assume maybe 1,000 users will use it at launch. They don't do any math. They provision two small servers and a single database instance.

Launch day arrives. The feature goes viral. 50,000 users hit it in the first hour. The database connection pool fills up in 30 seconds. Response times spike from 200ms to 15 seconds. Users see error pages. The team scrambles to add servers — but scaling up takes time, and by then the damage is done. Users leave. The press writes "Company X's new feature crashes on launch." All because nobody spent 30 seconds multiplying.

The Real Cost of Guessing

Guessing wrong doesn't just cause crashes. It causes bad architecture decisions that are expensive to undo:

Guess too low: You build for 1,000 users/sec, actual is 50,000. System crashes on launch day. Emergency rewrite under pressure. Weeks of rework. Lost users. Lost revenue.
Guess too high: You build a massively distributed system for 10 million users/sec when you only get 100. You've spent 6 months building infrastructure you didn't need. Your startup runs out of money before finding product-market fit.

Both directions are painful. Estimation keeps you in the sweet spot — building just enough infrastructure for your actual needs, with some headroom for growth.

Real-World Estimation Failures

These aren't hypotheticals. These are real incidents where skipping the math cost real companies real money:

Healthcare.gov launch (October 2013): The team expected ~50,000 concurrent users on day one. They got 250,000. The site crashed within two hours and stayed broken for weeks. The emergency fix cost taxpayers an additional $40 million beyond the original $93.7 million contract. A 30-second estimate — "320 million Americans, heavily marketed, launch day curiosity, maybe 1% try it on day 1 = 3.2 million, spread over 8 hours = ~400K concurrent at peak" — would have shown 50K was absurdly low.
Parler relaunch (2021): After being dropped by AWS, Parler relaunched on a single-provider setup without estimating their returning traffic. They provisioned for "moderate" load. Their 20 million users tried to log in simultaneously. The platform crashed repeatedly for days. Proper estimation: 20M users, even 10% returning on day one = 2M users in ~4 hours = ~140 concurrent requests per second just for login. That's before any browsing traffic.

And over-estimation is equally destructive:

The over-provisioning trap: A startup builds a distributed microservices architecture with Kubernetes, Kafka, Elasticsearch, and Redis — designed for 10 million users. They launch with 500 users. Monthly AWS bill: $45,000. Revenue: $0. They run out of funding in 6 months. A quick estimate would have shown: 500 users × 20 requests/day ÷ 86,400 = 0.1 QPS. A single $20/month VPS handles that. They could have survived two more years on the saved infrastructure costs.
Think about it: Every architecture decision without numbers is a coin flip. How many servers? Coin flip. Which database? Coin flip. Cache or no cache? Coin flip. Would you bet your company on a coin flip? Because that's what building without estimation is.
Section 5

The Breakthrough — It's Just 4th-Grade Math

Here's the revelation that changes everything: back-of-envelope estimation is multiplication and division. That's it. No calculus. No statistics. No algorithms. Just the same math you learned when you were ten years old.

The name says it all — back-of-envelope estimationA rough calculation done quickly, named because you could literally do it on the back of an envelope with a pen. — because you can literally do it on the back of an envelope at a coffee shop. No laptop required. No spreadsheet. Just a pen and some napkin math.

You only need three pieces of information to estimate almost anything:

  1. How many users? — Not total registered users. How many actually show up each day.
  2. What does each user do? — How many messages, clicks, uploads, searches per day.
  3. How big is each action? — Each message is 100 bytes. Each photo is 2 MB. Each video is 50 MB.

Multiply those three things together and you have your answer. Want storage? Users × actions × size per action. Want requests per second? Users × actions per day ÷ seconds in a day. Want bandwidth? Same thing, but measured in bits per second instead of bytes stored.

Think First

YouTube has about 2 billion monthly users, and around 800 million daily active users. Each user watches an average of 40 minutes per day. The average video streams at about 5 Mbps (megabits per second). How much total bandwidth does YouTube need per second?

Start with: 800M users × 40 min. But not all users watch at the same instant — spread over 24 hours. Think about how to convert daily watching time to concurrent viewers.

Let's work through that Think First. 800 million users each watch 40 minutes per day. That's 32 billion minutes of video per day. Spread across 24 hours (1,440 minutes), that means on average about 22 million people are watching at any given moment. At 5 Mbps per stream, that's 22 million × 5 Mbps = 110 million Mbps = 110 Tbps (terabits per second). That's why YouTube spends billions of dollars on infrastructure and CDN. And you just figured that out with a napkin and a pen.

Key insight: The name "back-of-envelope estimation" exists because the whole skill literally fits on the back of an envelope. You just need to know which numbers to start with — and that's what the next section teaches you step by step.

Real Tools for Real Estimation

You don't always need a pen and napkin. Here are the tools professional engineers actually use for quick estimation — from a terminal, a REPL, or a spreadsheet:

The bc Calculator (Linux/macOS Terminal)

Already installed on every Linux box. Perfect for quick multiplication chains:

bash — estimation math
# WhatsApp daily storage: 100 billion messages × 100 bytes
echo "100000000000 * 100" | bc
# Output: 10000000000000 (10 trillion bytes = 10 TB)

# YouTube bandwidth: 22M concurrent × 5 Mbps
echo "22000000 * 5" | bc
# Output: 110000000 Mbps = 110 Tbps

# Quick powers of 2
echo "2^40" | bc
# Output: 1099511627776 (≈1 TB — handy to remember: 2^40 ≈ 1 TB)

Python REPL — The Engineer's Calculator

Most engineers just open python3 and type. Python handles big numbers natively — no overflow, no scientific notation confusion:

python3 — estimation REPL
>>> # WhatsApp: messages per second
>>> 100_000_000_000 / 86_400
1157407.4074074074   # ~1.16 million messages/sec

>>> # Instagram: storage for 100M photos/day at 2MB each
>>> 100_000_000 * 2 * 1024**3 / 1024**4  # convert to TB
195.3125   # ~200 TB/day

>>> # Annual cost on S3
>>> 200 * 365 * 0.023 * 1000   # TB/year × $/GB/mo × GB/TB
1_679_000.0   # ~$1.68M/month... just for photos

Measure Real Sizes with curl

Don't guess how big a web page or API response is — measure it. This one command tells you the actual download size:

bash — measure real sizes
# How big is Google's homepage response?
curl -w "Download size: %{size_download} bytes\n" -o /dev/null -s https://google.com
# Output: Download size: 20123 bytes  (~20 KB)

# How big is a Twitter/X API response (single tweet)?
# Typical JSON tweet object: 5-10 KB (way more than the 280 chars of text)

# How big is your own database?
du -sh /var/lib/mysql/        # MySQL data directory
du -sh /var/lib/postgresql/   # PostgreSQL data directory
# Real answer beats any estimate

Google Sheets Estimation Template

For longer estimates (multi-system, multi-year projections), set up a spreadsheet. Column A: assumption name. Column B: value. Column C: formula. This way you can tweak one number (say DAU goes from 100M to 200M) and every downstream calculation updates instantly. Senior engineers at Google and Amazon keep estimation spreadsheets like this for capacity planning — it's not just an interview thing.

Pro tip: In an actual interview, you won't have a laptop. But practicing with these tools at home builds number intuition — you'll start to "feel" when something is wrong. "Wait, 500 PB for a chat app? That can't be right..." That gut feeling comes from having done the real math dozens of times with real tools.
Section 6

How It Works — The 5-Step Method

Here's the complete method, step by step. Once you learn this, you can estimate the storage, throughputThe total number of operations a system can handle per unit of time. Higher throughput = more work done., bandwidth, and server count for any system in under a minute. Let's walk through each step, then do two full worked examples end to end.

Step 1: Start with Users

Every estimation begins with the same question: how many people actually use this thing every day?

There are three numbers that matter, and they form a funnel. At the top you have the total number of people who have ever signed up — total users. A lot of those people signed up and forgot about it, so the next number is monthly active users (MAU)Monthly Active Users — unique people who use a service at least once in a month. DAU is usually 20-50% of MAU. — people who use the app at least once a month. And finally, daily active users (DAU)Daily Active Users — the number of unique people who use a service on a given day. This is the starting point for most estimations. — people who use it every single day. This is the number that matters most for estimation, because your servers don't care about people who signed up three years ago and never came back.

A handy rule of thumb: for social apps, DAU is usually 20-50% of MAU. WhatsApp is on the high end (~70% — people check messages daily). A shopping app might be on the low end (~10% — people don't shop every day). In an interview, if you're not given specific numbers, pick a reasonable ratio and state your assumption clearly.

Step 2: Estimate Actions per User

Now that you know how many people show up each day, the next question is: what does each person actually DO? How many messages do they send? How many tweets do they read? How many photos do they upload?

This is where you need to think about the read/write ratioThe proportion of read operations to write operations. Most systems are heavily read-biased (10:1 to 1000:1).. Most systems are heavily read-biased — users read much more content than they create. This ratio matters enormously because reads and writes stress your system in completely different ways.

SystemReads/user/dayWrites/user/dayRead:Write Ratio
Twitter/X200 tweets viewed2 tweets posted100:1
WhatsApp40 messages read40 messages sent1:1
Instagram100 posts viewed1 post uploaded100:1
Google Search5 searches done0 (no user writes)Read-only
URL Shortener50 redirects1 URL created50:1

Notice the pattern: most systems are massively read-heavy. Twitter users read 100 tweets for every 1 they write. Instagram users scroll past 100 photos for every 1 they upload. This is why caching exists — if 99% of your traffic is reads, putting a cache in front of your database eliminates most of the load.

Step 3: Calculate Per-Second Rates (QPS)

Now you know the total number of actions per day. But servers don't think in "per day" — they think in "per second." So you need to convert. The magic number is 86,400 — that's how many seconds are in a day (24 × 60 × 60). To make life easy, just round it to 100,000 (105). That makes the division trivial and only off by ~15%, which is perfectly acceptable for estimation.

QPS Formula Average QPS = daily actions ÷ 86,400 Quick version: daily actions ÷ 100,000 (rounds down ~15%, but close enough) → Example: 500M tweets read/day ÷ 100K ≈ 5,000 reads/sec

But that's the average. Traffic doesn't arrive evenly throughout the day. There are peaks — the morning commute, lunch breaks, evenings. At peak trafficThe busiest moment of the day, typically 2-5× the average load. Your system must handle this without crashing. times, traffic is typically 2-5× the average for social media apps, and can spike to 10-100× for flash sales or live events.

Twitter Write QPS 500 million DAU × 2 tweets/day = 1 billion tweets/day Average write QPS = 1B ÷ 86,400 ≈ 12,000 writes/sec Peak write QPS = 12,000 × 3 ≈ 36,000 writes/sec → You need infrastructure that can handle 36K writes/sec at peak.

The term QPSQueries Per Second — the number of requests a server handles every second. The key metric for sizing infrastructure. stands for "queries per second" — it's just a count of how many requests hit your server every second. In an interview, always calculate both average AND peak QPS. The average tells you what your system needs to handle most of the time. The peak tells you what it needs to survive without crashing.

Step 4: Calculate Storage

Storage estimation is the easiest of all — it's literally just how much data you create per day, times how long you keep it.

Storage Formula Daily storage = actions/day × data size per action Total storage = daily storage × retention periodHow long you keep data before deleting or archiving it. Affects total storage needs dramatically. (days) Don't forget: + Metadata (20-30% overhead typically) + Indexes (depends on DB, but ~10-20%) + Replicas (usually 3× for reliability) → Multiply your raw data by ~5× for a realistic total.
WhatsApp Storage Example 100 billion messages/day × 100 bytes/message = 10 TB/day Per year: 10 TB × 365 = 3.65 PB/year (just messages) With 3× replication: ~11 PB/year With metadata + indexes: ~15 PB/year → WhatsApp needs to add ~15 petabytes of storage every year for messages alone. That's serious infrastructure.

A common trap: forgetting about replicationStoring multiple copies of data across different servers. Typically 3 copies (one primary, two backups) for reliability.. Almost every production database keeps 3 copies of every piece of data (one primary, two replicas). This triples your storage requirement instantly. And if you have indexes on multiple columns, add another 10-20% on top of that. When you give your final number in an interview, mentioning replicas and indexes shows the interviewer you understand real-world systems, not just textbook math.

Step 5: Calculate Bandwidth

Bandwidth is how much data flows through your network per second. There are two directions: data coming IN (ingressIncoming data — traffic flowing INTO your system. Users uploading, posting, or sending data.) and data going OUT (egressOutgoing data — traffic flowing OUT of your system. Users downloading, streaming, or receiving responses.). For most systems, egress is much larger than ingress because many users are reading/downloading, while only a few are writing/uploading.

Bandwidth Formula Ingress = write QPS × data size per write Egress = read QPS × data size per read Convert bytes to bits: multiply by 8 (Network speeds are measured in bits/sec, not bytes/sec) → Egress is usually 10-100× larger than ingress. That's why CDNs exist.
YouTube Egress Example 800M DAU × 40 min/day = 32B minutes of video/day Spread over 1,440 min/day ≈ 22M concurrent viewers At 5 Mbps per stream: 22M × 5 Mbps = 110 Tbps That's 110 terabits per second. → No single data center can handle this. This is why YouTube uses CDN servers in thousands of locations worldwide.
Quick tip: When bandwidth numbers get absurdly large (like YouTube's), that's your signal that the system MUST use a CDNContent Delivery Network — a network of servers spread around the world that cache content close to users, so they don't all have to fetch data from one central location (Content Delivery Network). It's not a nice-to-have — it's physically impossible to serve that much bandwidth from one location.

Full Worked Example: URL Shortener

Let's put the whole method together and estimate everything for a URL shortener (like bit.ly). We'll walk through all 5 steps end to end.

How many users?

Let's assume our URL shortener has 100 million monthly active users. Not everyone creates short URLs — most people just click on them. Maybe 10% of MAU create URLs (10M creators), and 90% just click (90M clickers). For DAU, let's say 20% of MAU = 20 million daily active users.

Step 1: Users MAU = 100 million DAU = 20 million (20% of MAU) Creators: 10% of DAU = 2 million/day create URLs Clickers: 90% of DAU = 18 million/day click URLs

What does each user do?

A URL creator shortens maybe 1 URL per day on average. Each short URL gets clicked about 50 times over its lifetime. Since we have 2 million new URLs per day, and each gets 50 clicks spread over time, let's estimate about 100 million clicks per day total (combining new and historical URLs).

Step 2: Actions Writes: 2M new URLs created per day Reads: ~100M redirects per day (clicks on short URLs) Read:Write ratio = 100M : 2M = 50:1 → This is a heavily read-dominant system.

How many requests per second?

Step 3: QPS Write QPS = 2M / 86,400 ≈ 23 writes/sec Read QPS = 100M / 86,400 ≈ 1,160 reads/sec Peak (3× average): Peak write QPS ≈ 70/sec Peak read QPS ≈ 3,500/sec → Very manageable. A single modern server can handle this read load with caching.

How much storage?

Each URL record stores: the short code (~7 bytes), the original URL (~200 bytes average), creation timestamp (8 bytes), and user ID (8 bytes). Let's round to ~500 bytes per record.

Step 4: Storage Daily: 2M URLs × 500 bytes = 1 GB/day Yearly: 1 GB × 365 = 365 GB/year 5 years: ~1.8 TB With 3× replication: ~5.5 TB With indexes: ~7 TB total after 5 years → This easily fits on a single modern server. No sharding needed for storage — the bottleneck is reads, not space.

How much bandwidth?

Step 5: Bandwidth Ingress: 23 writes/sec × 500 bytes = 11.5 KB/sec ≈ 92 Kbps Egress: 1,160 reads/sec × 500 bytes = 580 KB/sec ≈ 4.6 Mbps Peak egress: ~14 Mbps → Tiny bandwidth. A basic internet connection could handle this. Bandwidth is NOT the bottleneck here — latency is (you want redirects under 50ms).
Summary: A URL shortener is a surprisingly small system. 7 TB of storage over 5 years, ~3,500 reads/sec at peak. The real challenge isn't scale — it's generating unique short codes fast enough and making redirects lightning-quick. The estimation tells you where to focus your design effort.

Full Worked Example: Instagram

Now let's try something bigger. Instagram is a photo and video sharing platform with massive read traffic, heavy storage requirements, and a global user base. The numbers get much scarier.

How many users?

Instagram has about 2 billion MAU. It's a highly engaging app — people scroll through it daily — so let's say DAU is about 50% of MAU.

Step 1: Users MAU = 2 billion DAU = 1 billion (50% of MAU) Posters: ~10% of DAU = 100M upload daily Scrollers: ~90% of DAU = 900M just browse

What does each user do?

The average user scrolls through about 100 posts per day (a mix of photos and Reels). Posters upload about 1 photo per day on average. Let's focus on photo uploads for storage (Reels/video would be a separate, much larger calculation).

Step 2: Actions Reads: 1B DAU × 100 posts viewed = 100B post views/day Writes: 100M uploaders × 1 photo = 100M uploads/day Read:Write ratio = 100B : 100M = 1000:1 → Extremely read-heavy. Caching and CDN are absolutely essential.

How many requests per second?

Step 3: QPS Write QPS = 100M / 86,400 ≈ 1,160 uploads/sec Read QPS = 100B / 86,400 ≈ 1,160,000 reads/sec Peak (3×): Peak writes ≈ 3,500 uploads/sec Peak reads ≈ 3.5 MILLION reads/sec → 3.5 million reads per second at peak. No single server handles this — you need thousands of cache servers and CDN nodes.

How much storage?

An Instagram photo is typically stored in multiple sizes (thumbnail, medium, original). Let's say the average total per photo is about 2 MB across all sizes.

Step 4: Storage Daily: 100M photos × 2 MB = 200 TB/day Yearly: 200 TB × 365 = 73 PB/year With 3× replication: ~220 PB/year → 220 petabytes per year just for photos. This is why Instagram uses massive object storage systems (like S3) — no traditional database can hold this.

How much bandwidth?

Step 5: Bandwidth Ingress: 1,160 uploads/sec × 2 MB = 2.3 GB/sec ≈ 18.5 Gbps Egress: Each post view serves a ~200KB image (cached CDN size) → 1.16M reads/sec × 200 KB = 232 GB/sec ≈ 1.85 Tbps Peak egress: ~5.5 Tbps → Nearly 6 terabits per second at peak. This MUST be served from CDN edge nodes worldwide — no central data center can push this much data.
Summary: Instagram is a storage monster (73+ PB/year) and a bandwidth beast (Tbps-level egress). The estimation makes it obvious that you need object storage (not a database) for photos, a global CDN for serving, and heavy caching at every layer. Without these numbers, you might naively propose "store photos in the database" — and the interviewer would know you haven't thought about scale.

Going Deeper — The Numbers You Need to Memorize

So far you've learned the method. Now you need ammunition — the reference numbers that make the method work. There are three sets of numbers worth committing to memory: powers of 2 (data sizes), latency numbers (how fast things are), and quick conversion tricks (shortcuts that speed up your math).

Computers think in powers of 2. Every time you go up by 10 powers, the data size jumps by about 1,000×. Here's the ladder that maps those powers to units you actually use:

Cross-continent round-trip150 ms150,000,000×New York to London and back

The big takeaway: RAM is 1,000× faster than SSDSolid State Drive — storage that uses flash memory chips. 100-1000× faster than traditional spinning hard drives (HDD)., and SSD is 100× faster than HDD. This is why caching matters so much. If your database reads from disk at 10ms per query, but you cache the result in RAM and serve it in 100ns, you've just made your system 100,000× faster for cached items. That's not a small optimization — that's the difference between "works" and "doesn't work at scale."

Think about it: If a RAM access takes 1 second in human time (instead of 100 ns), then an SSD read would take 15 minutes, an HDD read would take 1.5 days, and a cross-continent network round-trip would take 4.5 years. That's how dramatic these differences are.

The Art of Rounding

The secret weapon of fast estimation is aggressive rounding. The goal isn't exact math — it's "right order of magnitude" math. Here are the rules:

  • Round to the nearest power of 10. 86,400 → 100,000. 2,592,000 → 2,500,000. Close enough.
  • Round intermediate numbers, not just the final one. If you're multiplying 47 × 823, just do 50 × 800 = 40,000. The real answer is 38,681. You're off by 3%. Nobody cares.
  • Always round in the same direction for safety. If you're estimating infrastructure, round UP so you don't under-provision. If you're estimating cost to a budget committee, round UP too so there are no surprises.
  • State your assumptions. In an interview, saying "I'll round 86,400 to 100,000 for simplicity" shows the interviewer you know it's approximate AND you're doing it intentionally. That's a signal of maturity.
The golden rule of estimation: Being within 2-5× of the real number is a success. Being within 10× is acceptable. Being off by 100× or more means you made a fundamental mistake (like forgetting to account for replicas, or confusing bytes and bits). The method in Section 6 keeps you in the safe zone.
Section 8

The Variations — 5 Types of Estimation

So far we've treated estimation as one thing. But in practice, interviewers (and real architects) care about five different kinds of numbers. Each one answers a different question about your system. Let's walk through them one at a time — each introduced through a real problem that needs it.

You might wonder: why bother splitting estimation into 5 types? Can't I just calculate "how much stuff" and be done? The answer is that each type drives a different architectural decision. Traffic estimation tells you how many servers to provision. Storage estimation tells you which database technology to use and when to shard. Bandwidth estimation tells you whether you need a CDN. Cache estimation tells you how much RAM to buy. Server estimation ties it all together into a bill of materials. If you lump them all into one number, you'll miss the nuance — and that nuance is exactly what separates a good design from a broken one.

1. Traffic Estimation (QPS) — "How many servers do I need?"

Traffic estimation is about counting requests per second. It's the most common type because it directly tells you how many servers to run. Think of it like a restaurant — you need to know how many customers walk in per hour to decide how many chefs and waiters you need.

There are three flavors of QPS you should always compute:

  • Average QPS — total requests ÷ seconds in a day. This is your baseline.
  • Peak QPS — the busiest moment. Usually 2–10× the average. This is what your system must handle without crashing.
  • Read vs Write QPS — most systems have 10–1000× more reads than writes. This tells you where to focus optimization.

Notice the huge difference between the night-time valley and the afternoon peak — that's easily a 5–8× gap. If you only design for the average, your system will crash during peak hours. Always design for peak.

Quick formula: Servers needed = Peak QPS ÷ QPS per server. If your peak is 8,000 QPS and each server handles 500 QPS, you need at least 16 servers. Add 30% headroom → 21 servers.

2. Storage Estimation — "How big is my database in 5 years?"

Storage estimation tells you how much disk space your system will consume over time. It's the simplest multiplication: figure out how big one record is, figure out how many records you'll create, then multiply across the time horizon.

The formula looks like this:

Storage = Per-record size × Records/day × Days of retention × Replication factor

Replication factor is the one most people forget. In production, data is stored at least 3 times (one primary + two backups). So if you calculate 10 TB of raw data, the real storage is 30 TB.

Think First

Twitter stores 500 million tweets per day. Each tweet is about 300 bytes of text + 200 bytes of metadata = 500 bytes total. How much storage does Twitter need per year (before replication)?

500M tweets/day × 500 bytes = 250 GB/day. 250 GB × 365 = ~91 TB/year. With 3× replication: ~273 TB/year. That's just text — media is far more.

3. Bandwidth Estimation — "How fat do my pipes need to be?"

Bandwidth is about the speed of data flow, not the total amount. It's measured in bits per second (bps). There are two directions to think about:

  • Ingress (incoming) — data flowing INTO your system. Users uploading photos, posting messages, sending requests.
  • Egress (outgoing) — data flowing OUT of your system. Users downloading feeds, streaming videos, receiving API responses.

Here's the key insight most people miss: egress is almost always 10–100× bigger than ingress. One person uploads a photo, but a thousand people view it. One person writes a tweet, but millions read it.

Quick formula: Egress bandwidth = QPS × average response size. If you serve 5,000 QPS and each response is 200 KB, that's 5,000 × 200 KB = 1 GB/s = 8 Gbps.

4. Memory / Cache Estimation — "How much RAM for my cache?"

Caching is one of the most powerful tools in system design — it keeps your most popular data in fast RAM instead of slow disk. But RAM is expensive, so you can't cache everything (and if you're not careful, a cache stampedeWhen a cached item expires and hundreds of requests simultaneously hit the database to regenerate it, causing overload. can overwhelm your database). That's where the 80/20 rule comes in.

The Pareto principleAlso called the 80/20 rule — roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. In caching: 20% of data serves 80% of requests. says that roughly 20% of your data accounts for 80% of your traffic. So if you cache the top 20% of "hot" data, you'll serve 80% of requests from memory — a massive speedup.

Cache size = Daily read requests × 0.2 × Average response size

Example: If your system handles 10M read requests per day, and each response is 500 bytes, then: 10M × 0.2 × 500 bytes = 1 GB of cache. That's a single Redis instance — cheap and effective.

5. Server Count Estimation — "How many machines?"

This is where everything comes together. You've estimated your QPS, storage, bandwidth, and cache — now you need to figure out how many physical (or virtual) servers to run. The trick is understanding that different workloads hit different bottlenecks:

  • CPU-bound — heavy computation (image processing, encryption). Bottleneck is processor speed. One server might only handle 100–500 QPS.
  • Memory-bound — large working sets (caching, in-memory databases). Bottleneck is RAM capacity. You might need more servers just for memory, even if CPUs are idle.
  • I/O-bound — lots of disk or network activity (video streaming, file uploads). Bottleneck is disk throughput or network bandwidth.
Rule of thumb: A standard API server handles ~500 QPS. A static content server (images, CSS) handles ~10,000 QPS. A Redis cache server handles ~50,000 QPS. These are rough — the exact number depends on hardware and workload — but they get you in the right ballpark.

Section 10

The Anti-Lesson — When Estimation Goes Wrong

Estimation is a superpower, but it can backfire in subtle ways. In 2013, healthcare.gov launched expecting 50,000-60,000 concurrent users based on government estimates. The actual load on day one? 250,000 concurrent users — 5x the estimate. The site crashed, stayed broken for weeks, and cost over $600 million in emergency fixes. The original team had done "estimation" — but they used historical government website traffic, not the pent-up demand of 40 million uninsured Americans. Wrong starting assumption, right math, catastrophic result. Here are the three most common traps that cause exactly this kind of failure.

Trap 1: The Over-Precision Trap — spending 15 minutes on math that doesn't matter

This is the most common mistake in interviews. Someone spends 15 minutes calculating to 3 decimal places... but their initial assumption (like "each user sends 10 messages/day") could easily be off by 2×. All that precision is wasted theater.

The fix: Round aggressively. If your estimate says 47 servers, just say "about 50." Nobody needs the exact number — they need to know it's not 5 and not 500.
Trap 2: The Wrong-Assumption Trap — using the wrong starting numbers

Using U.S.-centric numbers for a global system (or vice versa). For example: assuming all users are active during the same 8-hour window. In reality, a global system has traffic from every timezone, which actually flattens your peak. Or assuming broadband speeds when half your users are on 3G in Southeast Asia.

The fix: State your assumptions out loud. "I'll assume the user base is primarily in North America, so I'll use a peak factor of 3×." This shows the interviewer you're aware of the limitation.
Trap 3: The "Just Add More Servers" Trap — hardware can't fix bad design

Not all problems can be solved by throwing more hardware at them. A poorly-designed database query that takes 5 seconds won't get faster by adding 100 more app servers — you'll just have 100 servers waiting on the same slow database. This is the classic vertical vs horizontal scalingAdding more machines to handle increased load, as opposed to making one machine bigger (vertical scaling). confusion.

The fix: Identify the bottleneck first (CPU? Memory? Disk? Network? Database?), then estimate whether the fix is more machines or a better design.

The meta-lesson here is that estimation is a tool for THINKING, not a tool for precision. The moment you start optimizing your estimate instead of your architecture, you've lost the plot. Spend 2 minutes estimating, then spend the remaining 40 minutes designing. The estimate's job is to tell you the shape of the problem — are we dealing with gigabytes or petabytes, hundreds of QPS or millions? Once you know that, every design decision becomes clearer.

The bottom line: Your estimate is guaranteed wrong. The question is whether it's wrong by 2× (totally fine) or 1,000× (disaster). The method in Section 6 keeps you in the safe zone. The traps above are what push you into the danger zone.
Section 11

Common Mistakes — What Engineers Get Wrong

These mistakes are dangerous because they feel invisible. Your math looks clean, your logic feels right, but one missed factor silently multiplies every number by 3× or 10×. You end up confident in an answer that's quietly off by an order of magnitude. The good news: once you learn to spot them, you'll never make them again. Each mistake below is something that trips up even experienced engineers — but after reading this section, you'll have a mental checklist that catches them every time.

These are the six mistakes that come up again and again in interviews and real engineering. Each one can throw your estimate off by 10× or more. Learn them once and you'll never make them.

Mistake 1: Forgetting peak vs average

If you only plan for the average, your system crashes during peak hours. Peak traffic is typically 2–10× the average, depending on the application. Social media spikes during evenings and events. E-commerce spikes during sales.

The fix: Multiply your average by 3× for a "normal" peak factor. For viral/event-driven systems, use 10× or higher.
Mistake 2: Confusing bytes and bits

This is embarrassingly common. 8 bits = 1 byte. Storage is measured in bytes (KB, MB, GB). Bandwidth is measured in bits (Kbps, Mbps, Gbps). So 100 Mbps of bandwidth can transfer about 12.5 MB per second, not 100 MB.

The fix: Always clarify. Write "MBps" for megabytes and "Mbps" for megabits. And when converting bandwidth to throughput, divide by 8.
Mistake 3: Ignoring the read/write ratio

Most systems have vastly more reads than writes. Twitter has a 50:1 read-to-write ratio. YouTube is closer to 1000:1 (one upload, millions of views). If you estimate read and write QPS as equal, your storage, caching, and server allocation will all be wrong.

The fix: Always ask: "Is this a read-heavy or write-heavy system?" Then estimate them separately.
Mistake 4: Not accounting for replication

You calculate 10 TB of data. You buy 10 TB of disk. Then your server crashes and all your data is gone. In production, data is replicated at least 3 times (usually 3 replicas across different availability zonesIsolated data center locations within a cloud region. If one zone goes down, others keep running. Spreading replicas across zones protects against hardware failures.). So that 10 TB is actually 30 TB.

The fix: Some databases (like HDFS) use 3× replication by default. Cloud databases often have it hidden in the pricing. Always multiply raw storage by your replication factor.
Mistake 5: Using daily numbers when you need per-second

"We handle 10 million requests per day" sounds impressive, but your servers don't care about daily totals — they care about requests per second. 10M/day ÷ 86,400 ≈ 116 QPS. That's... not very much. A single server could handle that.

The fix: Always convert to per-second (divide by ~100K for easy math). Then multiply by your peak factor.
Mistake 6: Forgetting metadata and indexes

You estimate the raw data size — say, 1 KB per user record. But the database also stores indexes (for fast lookup), internal metadata, and write-ahead logs. The total is typically 2–3× the raw data size.

The fix: For systems with many indexes (e.g., a search engine), the overhead can be 5× or more. Always mention this in your estimate: "Plus 2–3× for indexes and metadata."
Section 12

Interview Playbook — How to Present Estimation

Knowing how to estimate is only half the battle. The other half is how you present it in an interview. The format, the timing, and the confidence all matter. Here's the playbook.

The Format: What to Say Out Loud

Interviewers want to hear your thought process, not just the final number. Follow this script:

  1. "Let me start with some assumptions..." — State your user counts and activity levels. This gives the interviewer a chance to correct you early.
  2. "So the QPS would be..." — Show the multiplication. Write it on the whiteboard.
  3. "For storage, that means..." — Connect QPS to storage, bandwidth, and servers.
  4. "Based on these numbers, we'll need..." — State the conclusion (e.g., "about 20 servers, 50 TB of storage, and a CDN for egress").
  5. "I should note that..." — Mention one caveat (peak vs average, replication, etc.).

Sample Interview Dialogue

Interviewer: "Design a URL shortener."

You: "Let me start with the scale. I'll assume we handle 100 million new URLs per month, which is about 40 QPS for writes. If the read-to-write ratio is 100:1, that's 4,000 QPS for reads. Each URL record is maybe 500 bytes — original URL, short code, timestamp, user ID. So monthly storage is 100M × 500 bytes = 50 GB/month, or about 600 GB/year. With 3× replication, call it 2 TB/year. For reads at 4,000 QPS, I'll need maybe 8 servers plus a cache layer for the hot URLs. Does that scale sound right?"

Interviewer: "Yes, that's a good starting point. Let's move to the high-level design."

Notice what happened: the estimation took about 90 seconds, used round numbers, stated assumptions clearly, and ended with a check-in question. That's exactly what impresses interviewers.

Level-Specific Approaches

Focus: Get the basics right.

  • State users and QPS. Show the division from daily to per-second.
  • Estimate storage for one year (record size × records/day × 365).
  • Mention replication (3×) and peak factor (3×).
  • Don't worry about being precise — just show you can think in numbers.

Even getting half of this right puts you above most junior candidates who skip estimation entirely.

Focus: Separate read and write paths. At this level, you're showing the interviewer you understand that reads and writes are fundamentally different beasts — they stress different parts of the system and need different solutions.

  • Split QPS into reads and writes. Estimate the ratio explicitly — "Our system is 100:1 read-heavy."
  • Calculate cache size using the 80/20 rule — "Top 20% of URLs = 1.4M × 500 bytes = 700 MB cache."
  • Estimate bandwidth — break out ingress and egress separately. Mention that egress is typically 10-100× larger.
  • Consider storage tiers: hot data on SSD, warm on HDD, cold on S3/Glacier. This shows cost awareness.
  • Mention sharding triggers — "If storage exceeds ~2 TB for a relational DB, or writes exceed ~10K QPS, sharding becomes necessary."

At this level, your estimation should directly inform your architecture diagram. "The 100:1 ratio tells me I need read replicas and a cache layer" is exactly what interviewers want to hear.

Focus: Drive the architecture from the numbers.

  • Use estimation to justify every architectural decision: "Since our read QPS is 100× writes, I'll use a CQRSCommand Query Responsibility Segregation — separating read and write operations into different models or databases. Useful when read and write patterns differ dramatically. pattern with read replicas."
  • Estimate cost: "20 c5.xlarge instances at ~$0.17/hr = ~$2,500/month for compute alone."
  • Consider geographic distribution: "With 40% of users in Asia, I'd place a CDN edge in Singapore."
  • Identify the bottleneck: "The limiting factor is database write throughput at 6K QPS — that drives us toward sharding by user_id."
What impresses interviewers: Not the exact number — the structured thinking. Can you identify the important dimensions? Can you make reasonable assumptions? Can you do quick math without a calculator? Can you use the numbers to drive design decisions? That's the real test.
Section 13

Hands-On Challenges — Practice Estimation

Estimation is a skill that only gets better with practice. Here are five challenges at increasing difficulty. Set a timer — real interviews give you 3–5 minutes for this. Try each one before peeking at the hint or solution.

Challenge 1: Photo Sharing App Storage Beginner

Estimate the storage needed for a photo-sharing app with 50 million daily active users. Assume 10% of users upload 3 photos per day. Each photo is 2 MB on average. How much new storage per day? Per year?

Time target: 2 minutes

Hint

Start with uploaders: 50M × 10% = 5M uploaders. Then photos per day: 5M × 3 = 15M photos. Then total size: 15M × 2 MB = ?

Full Solution
  • Uploaders per day: 50M × 0.1 = 5M
  • Photos per day: 5M × 3 = 15M
  • Storage per day: 15M × 2 MB = 30 TB/day
  • Storage per year: 30 TB × 365 ≈ 11 PB/year
  • With 3× replication: ~33 PB/year
  • With thumbnails (3 sizes, ~100 KB each): add ~4.5 TB/day = ~1.6 PB/year

Conclusion: You need about 35 PB of storage per year. That's a significant infrastructure investment — probably distributed across multiple data centers using object storage (like S3).

Challenge 2: URL Shortener QPS & Servers Intermediate

A URL shortener processes 100 million new URLs per month. The read-to-write ratio is 100:1. Each server handles 500 QPS for writes and 2,000 QPS for reads. How many servers do you need (accounting for peak)?

Time target: 3 minutes

Hint

Convert monthly to per-second first: 100M/month ÷ 30 days ÷ 86,400 seconds. Then apply the read:write ratio. Then peak factor of 3×.

Full Solution
  • Write QPS: 100M ÷ 30 ÷ 86,400 ≈ 100M ÷ 2.5M ≈ 40 QPS (average)
  • Read QPS: 40 × 100 = 4,000 QPS (average)
  • Peak writes: 40 × 3 = 120 QPS
  • Peak reads: 4,000 × 3 = 12,000 QPS
  • Write servers: 120 ÷ 500 = 1 server (writes are easy here)
  • Read servers: 12,000 ÷ 2,000 = 6 servers
  • Total: ~7 servers + add 30% headroom ≈ 9-10 servers

Insight: The read side dominates — that's why URL shorteners are great candidates for caching (most URLs are accessed frequently right after creation).

Challenge 3: News Feed Cache Size Intermediate

A social media app has 500 million users. Each user's feed is generated from the latest 200 posts in their network. Each post is about 1 KB (text + metadata, no images). Use the 80/20 rule to estimate cache size.

Time target: 3 minutes

Hint

Not all 500M users are active daily. Assume ~20% DAU. Each active user's feed = 200 posts × 1 KB = 200 KB. Apply the 80/20 rule to decide what to cache.

Full Solution
  • DAU: 500M × 0.2 = 100M active users
  • Feed per user: 200 posts × 1 KB = 200 KB
  • Total if caching all active feeds: 100M × 200 KB = 20 TB
  • 80/20 rule: cache feeds for top 20% most active: 20M users
  • Cache size: 20M × 200 KB = 4 TB
  • With overhead (pointers, hash maps): ~5-6 TB

Reality check: A single Redis cluster can hold ~1 TB. So you'd need about 5-6 Redis nodes. That's totally manageable — Facebook-scale companies actually do this.

Challenge 4: Video Streaming Infrastructure Cost Advanced

A video streaming service has 10 million concurrent viewers. Average bitrate is 5 Mbps. CDN bandwidth costs $0.02 per GB. Estimate monthly bandwidth cost alone.

Time target: 4 minutes

Hint

Total bandwidth = 10M × 5 Mbps = 50 Tbps. Convert to GB/month: 50 Tbps × seconds in a month ÷ 8 (bits to bytes). Then multiply by the CDN rate.

Full Solution
  • Bandwidth: 10M streams × 5 Mbps = 50 Tbps = 50,000 Gbps
  • Bytes per second: 50,000 Gbps ÷ 8 = 6,250 GBps
  • Assume average 10M concurrent (not 24/7 — weight to ~6M average over the full day)
  • GB per month: 6,250 × 0.6 × 86,400 × 30 ≈ 6,250 × 0.6 × 2.6M ≈ 9.7 billion GB ≈ ~10 EB/month
  • Cost: 10B GB × $0.02 = ~$200 million/month on CDN alone

Reality check: Netflix reportedly spends $500M+/year on AWS. Bandwidth is the single biggest cost for video streaming — that's why Netflix built their own CDN (Open Connect).

Challenge 5: Multiplayer Game Infrastructure Expert

Estimate everything for a real-time multiplayer game with 1 million concurrent players. The game sends a state update every 50ms (20 updates/sec). Each update is 200 bytes. Players are in rooms of 100. Estimate QPS, bandwidth, server count, and what bottleneck you'd hit first.

Time target: 5 minutes

Hint

Each player sends 20 updates/sec. But each update must be broadcast to 99 other players in the room. So the fan-outWhen one message is delivered to many recipients. A fan-out of 100 means each incoming message generates 100 outgoing messages. is the key cost. Think about both inbound (player → server) and outbound (server → all players).

Full Solution
  • Inbound QPS: 1M players × 20 updates/sec = 20M QPS inbound
  • Rooms: 1M ÷ 100 = 10,000 rooms
  • Outbound per room: Each update → 99 other players. 100 players × 20 updates/sec × 99 recipients = 198,000 messages/sec per room
  • Total outbound: 10,000 rooms × 198,000 = ~2 billion messages/sec
  • Inbound bandwidth: 20M × 200 bytes = 4 GB/sec = 32 Gbps
  • Outbound bandwidth: 2B × 200 bytes = 400 GB/sec = 3.2 Tbps
  • Servers (at 50K messages/sec each): 2B ÷ 50K = 40,000 servers
  • Bottleneck: Network bandwidth. The 3.2 Tbps outbound is the killer. Each 10 Gbps server can push ~1.25 GB/s, so you need at least 320 servers just for bandwidth.

Real-world fix: Nobody actually broadcasts every update to every player. Games use interest management (only send updates for nearby entities), delta compression (only send what changed), and client-side prediction. This typically reduces bandwidth by 10–100×.
