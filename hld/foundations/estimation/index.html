Section 1

TL;DR — The One-Minute Version

Mental Model: Estimation is a bullshit detector. In 30 seconds of multiplication, you can tell whether an idea is brilliant or broken — before writing a single line of code.

The entire skill boils down to one sentence: figure out how many users you have, what each one does, and how big each action is. Multiply those three numbers together. That's it. That answer tells you how many servers you need, how much storage to buy, and whether your architecture will survive its first real traffic spike.

You don't need to be exact. Nobody expects you to nail the number down to the byte. What matters is getting within the right order of magnitudeA factor of 10. Being 'off by an order of magnitude' means your guess was 10× too high or too low. — knowing whether you need gigabytes or petabytes, 100 servers or 10,000. Being off by 2x is fine. Being off by 1000x is a career-ending architecture mistake.

One-line takeaway: Estimation isn't about precision — it's about preventing disasters. Thirty seconds of multiplication can save you from building a system that crashes on day one.
What top companies actually expect: At Google, interviewers expect you to start estimating within the first 5 minutes — before you draw a single box. At Amazon, they literally call it "napkin math" and treat it as a leadership principle (Insist on the Highest Standards — you can't set standards without numbers). At Netflix, they want you to identify the bottleneck from the numbers — storage? bandwidth? CPU? The estimate should drive every design decision that follows.
Section 2

Why You Need This

Picture this. You're in a system design interview. The interviewer says: "Design a chat app like WhatsApp." You start drawing boxes — a client, a server, a database. You're feeling good. Then the interviewer leans forward and asks:

"How much storage do you need per day?"

Silence. You have no idea. You stammer, "Uh... probably a lot?" The interviewer nods politely, but you can see them writing something on their notepad. It's not a good something.

Here's the thing: that question wasn't a trick. The interviewer doesn't have an exact number in mind either. They wanted to see you think with numbers. They wanted you to say something like: "WhatsApp has about 2 billion users. Let's say 1 billion are active daily. If each sends 40 messages a day, and each message is about 100 bytes of text... that's 4 trillion bytes. About 4 terabytes per day, just for message content." That takes 20 seconds, and it completely changes the conversation.

Without estimation, you can't answer the most basic architecture questions. Every single one of these decisions depends on numbers:

Section 3

The First Attempt — Guessing

Here's what most people do when asked "how much storage?" or "how many requests per second?" — they guess. "Probably a terabyte?" "Maybe a thousand requests per second?" It sounds reasonable in the moment. The problem is, guesses are almost always wildly wrong — and not just a little wrong, but orders-of-magnitude wrong.

An order of magnitude just means "a factor of 10." If the real answer is 10 TB and you guess 10 GB, you're off by three orders of magnitude — you're off by 1000x. That's the difference between needing one server and needing a thousand servers. That's the difference between a $500/month cloud bill and a $500,000/month cloud bill.

The interviewer doesn't expect an exact number. Nobody can tell you the precise storage requirement down to the byte. What they DO expect is that you can reason your way to the right ballpark. "About 10 TB per day" is a perfect answer. "Probably a lot" is not.

Here's why guessing is dangerous beyond interviews: in real engineering, a wrong estimate doesn't just lose you interview points — it loses money. A team that guesses "probably 1,000 requests per second" and builds for that, when the actual traffic is 50,000 requests per second, will watch their system crash on launch day. Every recovery is expensive, rushed, and stressful.

The good news: You don't need to memorize a million numbers. You just need a simple method — a recipe — that turns any "how much?" question into a chain of multiplications you can do on the back of an envelope. That's exactly what the next sections teach you.

Sanity-Check Your Guesses with Real Cloud Pricing

Here's a trick that separates good estimators from great ones: verify your estimate against real-world costs. If you estimated that WhatsApp needs 10 TB/day of message storage, you can check if that "smells right" using actual AWS pricing:

WhatsApp Storage Cost Sanity Check 10 TB/day × 365 days = 3.65 PB/year AWS S3 Standard: $0.023 per GB/month 3.65 PB = 3,650 TB = 3,650,000 GB 3,650,000 GB × $0.023 = ~$83,950/month just for storage Does that sound reasonable? Yes — Meta (WhatsApp's parent) spent $32 billion on infrastructure in 2023. $84K/month for message storage is a rounding error for them. → If your estimate implied $500M/month for text storage alone, you'd know something was off.

You can also cross-reference estimates against public company filings. Twitter's (now X's) Q4 2022 10-K filing showed ~$1.2 billion in annual infrastructure costs. If your estimate for Twitter's storage + compute costs came to $50 billion/year, that's a red flag — you're off by 40x. If it came to $800 million, you're in the right ballpark. Public filings are your estimation answer key.

Quick reference — real cloud prices for estimation (2024 rates):
• AWS S3 storage: $0.023/GB/month (~$23/TB/month)
• AWS EC2 (c5.xlarge, 4 vCPU): ~$0.17/hour (~$124/month)

Section 4

What Happens Without Math

Let's look at what actually happens when teams skip estimation and just... build things based on vibes.

The Launch Day Disaster

A team builds a new social feature. They assume maybe 1,000 users will use it at launch. They don't do any math. They provision two small servers and a single database instance.

Launch day arrives. The feature goes viral. 50,000 users hit it in the first hour. The database connection pool fills up in 30 seconds. Response times spike from 200ms to 15 seconds. Users see error pages. The team scrambles to add servers — but scaling up takes time, and by then the damage is done. Users leave. The press writes "Company X's new feature crashes on launch." All because nobody spent 30 seconds multiplying.

The Real Cost of Guessing

Guessing wrong doesn't just cause crashes. It causes bad architecture decisions that are expensive to undo:

Guess too low: You build for 1,000 users/sec, actual is 50,000. System crashes on launch day. Emergency rewrite under pressure. Weeks of rework. Lost users. Lost revenue.
Guess too high: You build a massively distributed system for 10 million users/sec when you only get 100. You've spent 6 months building infrastructure you didn't need. Your startup runs out of money before finding product-market fit.

Both directions are painful. Estimation keeps you in the sweet spot — building just enough infrastructure for your actual needs, with some headroom for growth.

Real-World Estimation Failures

These aren't hypotheticals. These are real incidents where skipping the math cost real companies real money:

Healthcare.gov launch (October 2013): The team expected ~50,000 concurrent users on day one. They got 250,000. The site crashed within two hours and stayed broken for weeks. The emergency fix cost taxpayers an additional $40 million beyond the original $93.7 million contract. A 30-second estimate — "320 million Americans, heavily marketed, launch day curiosity, maybe 1% try it on day 1 = 3.2 million, spread over 8 hours = ~400K concurrent at peak" — would have shown 50K was absurdly low.
Parler relaunch (2021): After being dropped by AWS, Parler relaunched on a single-provider setup without estimating their returning traffic. They provisioned for "moderate" load. Their 20 million users tried to log in simultaneously. The platform crashed repeatedly for days. Proper estimation: 20M users, even 10% returning on day one = 2M users in ~4 hours = ~140 concurrent requests per second just for login. That's before any browsing traffic.

And over-estimation is equally destructive:

The over-provisioning trap: A startup builds a distributed microservices architecture with Kubernetes, Kafka, Elasticsearch, and Redis — designed for 10 million users. They launch with 500 users. Monthly AWS bill: $45,000. Revenue: $0. They run out of funding in 6 months. A quick estimate would have shown: 500 users × 20 requests/day ÷ 86,400 = 0.1 QPS. A single $20/month VPS handles that. They could have survived two more years on the saved infrastructure costs.
Think about it: Every architecture decision without numbers is a coin flip. How many servers? Coin flip. Which database? Coin flip. Cache or no cache? Coin flip. Would you bet your company on a coin flip? Because that's what building without estimation is.
Section 5

The Breakthrough — It's Just 4th-Grade Math

Here's the revelation that changes everything: back-of-envelope estimation is multiplication and division. That's it. No calculus. No statistics. No algorithms. Just the same math you learned when you were ten years old.

The name says it all — back-of-envelope estimationA rough calculation done quickly, named because you could literally do it on the back of an envelope with a pen. — because you can literally do it on the back of an envelope at a coffee shop. No laptop required. No spreadsheet. Just a pen and some napkin math.

You only need three pieces of information to estimate almost anything:

  1. How many users? — Not total registered users. How many actually show up each day.
  2. What does each user do? — How many messages, clicks, uploads, searches per day.
  3. How big is each action? — Each message is 100 bytes. Each photo is 2 MB. Each video is 50 MB.

Multiply those three things together and you have your answer. Want storage? Users × actions × size per action. Want requests per second? Users × actions per day ÷ seconds in a day. Want bandwidth? Same thing, but measured in bits per second instead of bytes stored.

Think First

YouTube has about 2 billion monthly users, and around 800 million daily active users. Each user watches an average of 40 minutes per day. The average video streams at about 5 Mbps (megabits per second). How much total bandwidth does YouTube need per second?

Start with: 800M users × 40 min. But not all users watch at the same instant — spread over 24 hours. Think about how to convert daily watching time to concurrent viewers.

Let's work through that Think First. 800 million users each watch 40 minutes per day. That's 32 billion minutes of video per day. Spread across 24 hours (1,440 minutes), that means on average about 22 million people are watching at any given moment. At 5 Mbps per stream, that's 22 million × 5 Mbps = 110 million Mbps = 110 Tbps (terabits per second). That's why YouTube spends billions of dollars on infrastructure and CDN. And you just figured that out with a napkin and a pen.

Key insight: The name "back-of-envelope estimation" exists because the whole skill literally fits on the back of an envelope. You just need to know which numbers to start with — and that's what the next section teaches you step by step.

Real Tools for Real Estimation

You don't always need a pen and napkin. Here are the tools professional engineers actually use for quick estimation — from a terminal, a REPL, or a spreadsheet:

The bc Calculator (Linux/macOS Terminal)

Already installed on every Linux box. Perfect for quick multiplication chains:

bash — estimation math
# WhatsApp daily storage: 100 billion messages × 100 bytes
echo "100000000000 * 100" | bc
# Output: 10000000000000 (10 trillion bytes = 10 TB)

# YouTube bandwidth: 22M concurrent × 5 Mbps
echo "22000000 * 5" | bc
# Output: 110000000 Mbps = 110 Tbps

# Quick powers of 2
echo "2^40" | bc
# Output: 1099511627776 (≈1 TB — handy to remember: 2^40 ≈ 1 TB)

Python REPL — The Engineer's Calculator

Most engineers just open python3 and type. Python handles big numbers natively — no overflow, no scientific notation confusion:

python3 — estimation REPL
>>> # WhatsApp: messages per second
>>> 100_000_000_000 / 86_400
1157407.4074074074   # ~1.16 million messages/sec

>>> # Instagram: storage for 100M photos/day at 2MB each
>>> 100_000_000 * 2 * 1024**3 / 1024**4  # convert to TB
195.3125   # ~200 TB/day

>>> # Annual cost on S3
>>> 200 * 365 * 0.023 * 1000   # TB/year × $/GB/mo × GB/TB
1_679_000.0   # ~$1.68M/month... just for photos

For longer estimates (multi-system, multi-year projections), set up a spreadsheet. Column A: assumption name. Column B: value. Column C: formula. This way you can tweak one number (say DAU goes from 100M to 200M) and every downstream calculation updates instantly. Senior engineers at Google and Amazon keep estimation spreadsheets like this for capacity planning — it's not just an interview thing.

"Wait, 500 PB for a chat app? That can't be right..." That gut feeling comes from having done the real math dozens of times with real tools.
Section 6

How It Works — The 5-Step Method

Here's the complete method, step by step. Once you learn this, you can estimate the storage, throughputThe total number of operations a system can handle per unit of time. Higher throughput = more work done., bandwidth, and server count for any system in under a minute. We'll walk through each step with a full worked example — a ride-sharing app like Uber — so you can see the real math at every stage.

Let's estimate everything for an Uber-like ride-sharing platform. Every step uses the output of the previous step, so by the end you'll have storage, QPS, bandwidth, and server count — all from one starting number.

Step 1: Start with Users

Every estimation begins with the same question: how many people actually use this thing every day?

There are three numbers that form a funnel. At the top: total users (everyone who ever signed up). In the middle: monthly active users (MAU)Monthly Active Users — unique people who use a service at least once in a month. DAU is usually 20-50% of MAU. — people who use the app at least once a month. At the bottom: daily active users (DAU)Daily Active Users — the number of unique people who use a service on a given day. This is the starting point for most estimations. — people who use it every single day. DAU is the number you actually care about, because servers don't care about people who signed up three years ago and never came back.

For ride-sharing, DAU/MAU is lower than social apps (~30% vs ~50%). People don't take a ride every day — maybe a few times a week. In an interview, state your assumption: "Uber has about 100 million MAU. Not everyone rides daily, so let's say 30% DAU = 30 million daily active riders."

Step 1: Uber Users MAU = 100 million DAU = 30 million (30% of MAU) Active drivers at any moment ≈ 5 million → 30 million riders + 5 million drivers = 35M active participants per day

Step 2: Estimate Actions per User

Now that you know how many people show up each day, the next question is: what does each person actually DO? For ride-sharing, the key actions are: requesting a ride, sending GPS location updates, viewing the map, and completing a payment.

This is where you think about the read/write ratioThe proportion of read operations to write operations. Most systems are heavily read-biased (10:1 to 1000:1).. For Uber, it's unusual — both reads AND writes are high, because every driver sends GPS pings every few seconds (writes), and every rider's app constantly fetches nearby drivers (reads).

ActionWhoFrequencyData Size
GPS location ping5M driversEvery 4 seconds~100 bytes (lat, lng, timestamp, ID)
Request a ride30M riders~2 rides/day per active rider~1 KB (pickup, destination, preferences)
Fetch nearby drivers30M riders~10 map refreshes per ride~5 KB response (list of nearby drivers)
Trip record savedSystemOnce per completed ride~2 KB (route, fare, timestamps, IDs)
Payment processedSystemOnce per completed ride~500 bytes (charge, method, receipt)
Step 2: Actions per Day GPS pings: 5M drivers × (86,400 sec ÷ 4 sec) = 5M × 21,600 = 108 BILLION pings/day Ride requests: 30M riders × 2 rides = 60 million requests/day Map fetches: 60M rides × 10 refreshes = 600 million reads/day Trip records: 60M trips saved/day Payments: 60M payments/day → GPS pings dominate EVERYTHING. 108 billion writes/day is the killer number.

Notice something interesting: the ride requests (60 million/day) are dwarfed by the GPS pings (108 billion/day). That's a 1,800:1 ratio. In an interview, identifying this kind of dominant factor is exactly what impresses interviewers — it shows you know where the real engineering challenge is.

Step 3: Calculate Per-Second Rates (QPS)

Servers don't think in "per day" — they think in "per second." The magic number is 86,400 (seconds in a day). Round it to 100,000 (105) for quick math — only off by ~15%, which is fine for estimation.

Step 3: Uber QPS GPS pings: 108 billion/day ÷ 86,400 ≈ 1,250,000 writes/sec Ride requests: 60M/day ÷ 86,400 ≈ 700 requests/sec Map fetches: 600M/day ÷ 86,400 ≈ 7,000 reads/sec Peak (3× average during evening rush): Peak GPS: ~3.75 million writes/sec Peak ride requests: ~2,100/sec Peak map fetches: ~21,000 reads/sec → 1.25 MILLION GPS writes per second at average. 3.75M at peak. That's the bottleneck.

The term QPSQueries Per Second — the number of requests a server handles every second. The key metric for sizing infrastructure. stands for "queries per second." Always calculate both average AND peak. The average tells you what you handle most of the time. The peak tells you what you must survive without crashing. Uber's two-peak pattern (morning commute + evening) means the peak multiplier is about 3-5x the daily average.

Step 4: Calculate Storage

Storage estimation is the easiest of all — it's literally just how much data you create per day, times how long you keep it.

Storage Formula Daily storage = actions/day × data size per action Total storage = daily storage × retention periodHow long you keep data before deleting or archiving it. Affects total storage needs dramatically. (days) Don't forget: + Metadata (20-30% overhead typically) + Indexes (depends on DB, but ~10-20%) + Replicas (usually 3× for reliability) → Multiply your raw data by ~5× for a realistic total.
Uber Storage Breakdown GPS pings: 108B/day × 100 bytes = 10.8 TB/day Trip records: 60M/day × 2 KB = 120 GB/day Payments: 60M/day × 500 bytes = 30 GB/day ───────────────────────────────── Raw total: ~11 TB/day (GPS dominates at 98%) Per year: 11 TB × 365 = ~4 PB/year With 3× replication: ~12 PB/year With metadata + indexes: ~15 PB/year → 15 petabytes per year. But GPS data can be aged out — you only need real-time pings for matching. Historical routes can move to cheap cold storage.

A common trap: forgetting about replicationStoring multiple copies of data across different servers. Typically 3 copies (one primary, two backups) for reliability.. Almost every production database keeps 3 copies of data. This triples your storage instantly. When you mention replicas and indexes in an interview, the interviewer knows you understand real-world systems, not just textbook math.

Smart insight for Uber: Not all data is equal. GPS pings are "hot" for ~10 minutes (matching riders to drivers), then become "warm" (trip history), then "cold" (analytics). A tiered storage strategy — Redis for real-time, PostgreSQL for recent trips, S3 for historical data — is the right answer. The estimation tells you which tier matters most.

Step 5: Calculate Bandwidth

Bandwidth is how much data flows through your network per second. There are two directions: data coming IN (ingressIncoming data — traffic flowing INTO your system. Users uploading, posting, or sending data.) and data going OUT (egressOutgoing data — traffic flowing OUT of your system. Users downloading, streaming, or receiving responses.).

Uber Bandwidth Ingress (GPS pings coming IN): 1.25M writes/sec × 100 bytes = 125 MB/sec = 1 Gbps Egress (map data going OUT to riders): 7,000 reads/sec × 5 KB = 35 MB/sec = 280 Mbps Peak ingress: 1 Gbps × 3 = 3 Gbps Peak egress: 280 Mbps × 3 = 840 Mbps → Ingress dominates because of GPS pings. 3 Gbps peak ingress is serious but manageable — a few 10 Gbps NICs handle it.

Notice something unusual? For most apps (Instagram, YouTube), egress is much bigger because users download/stream content. But for Uber, ingress is bigger because millions of drivers are constantly sending location data IN. This kind of insight — knowing that Uber is ingress-heavy while YouTube is egress-heavy — is exactly what interviewers love to hear.

Putting It All Together

Summary: From one starting number (30M DAU), we derived QPS (1.25M GPS writes/sec), storage (11 TB/day), and bandwidth (3 Gbps). The estimation revealed that GPS writes are the bottleneck — not ride requests, not storage, not bandwidth. That single insight drives the entire architecture: you need an in-memory store like Redis for real-time GPS, a message queue to buffer writes, and tiered storage for aging data out. Without the math, you'd never know where to focus.

Try estimating a food delivery app like DoorDash. Assume 10 million DAU, each orders 1.5 times per day, each order involves ~20 GPS pings from the driver, and each order record is about 3 KB. What's the daily storage? What's the write QPS for GPS pings?

Orders: 10M × 1.5 = 15M/day. GPS pings: 15M orders × 20 pings × 100 bytes. Storage: sum it up. QPS: total pings per day ÷ 86,400.

Section 8

Variations — 5 Types of Estimation

Every system design estimation falls into one of five categories. Each type answers a different question, and each one uses the same 5-step method with slightly different inputs. Let's walk through all five, each with a different real company example so you build range.

Type 1: Traffic Estimation (Spotify)

Traffic estimation answers: how many requests hit your servers per second? This is the most common type in interviews because it directly determines how many servers you need.

Spotify Traffic Estimation MAU: 600 million DAU: 200 million (33% — not everyone listens daily) Actions per user per day: - Song plays: ~30 songs/day (avg session ~60 min, ~2 min/song) - Search queries: ~5/day - Playlist fetches: ~3/day Total daily actions: 200M × (30 + 5 + 3) = 7.6 billion/day QPS = 7.6B / 86,400 ≈ 88,000 req/sec Peak QPS (3×) ≈ 264,000 req/sec → ~88K average QPS, ~264K peak. Each song play is actually a stream — so the real challenge is concurrent streams, not just request count.

Type 2: Storage Estimation (Twitter/X)

Storage estimation answers: how much disk space do you need? The key is knowing the data size per action and the retention period.

Twitter/X Storage Estimation DAU: 250 million Tweets posted: 250M × 2 tweets/day = 500M tweets/day Tweet sizes: - Text tweet: ~1 KB (text + metadata + user object) - Photo tweet: ~1 KB text + 2 MB photo = ~2 MB - Video tweet: ~1 KB text + 50 MB video = ~50 MB Breakdown (of 500M tweets): - 80% text only: 400M × 1 KB = 400 GB/day - 15% with photos: 75M × 2 MB = 150 TB/day - 5% with video: 25M × 50 MB = 1.25 PB/day ──────────────── Total: ~1.4 PB/day (media dominates at 99.97%) Per year: ~511 PB. With 3× replication: ~1.5 EB (exabytes) → Text is a rounding error. Media (photos + video) is 99.97% of storage. This is why Twitter uses object storage (S3-like) for media and a separate DB for text/metadata.

Type 3: Bandwidth Estimation (TikTok)

Bandwidth estimation answers: how much data flows through your network per second? For video-heavy platforms, this number gets enormous fast.

TikTok Bandwidth Estimation DAU: 1 billion Average session: 52 minutes/day (TikTok's reported average) Average video length: 30 seconds Videos watched per session: 52 min × 60 sec / 30 sec = ~104 videos/day Average video bitrate: 3 Mbps (720p mobile) Average video size: 3 Mbps × 30 sec / 8 = ~11 MB per video Egress per day: 1B × 104 videos × 11 MB = 1.14 exabytes/day Egress per second: 1.14 EB / 86,400 sec ≈ 13.2 TB/sec = 105 Tbps Peak (2× — TikTok is evening-heavy): ~210 Tbps → 210 terabits per second at peak. This is physically impossible from one location. TikTok uses thousands of CDN edge nodes worldwide to serve this.

Type 4: Cache Sizing (Reddit)

Cache estimation answers: how much memory do you need for your cache layer? The key insight is the 80/20 ruleAlso called the Pareto principle — roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. For caching: 20% of content gets 80% of views. — typically 20% of your content gets 80% of the traffic. So you only need to cache the "hot" 20%.

Reddit Cache Sizing DAU: 50 million Page views per user per day: ~40 Total page views: 50M × 40 = 2 billion/day Unique posts accessed: ~50 million (many users view the same hot posts) Average rendered page size: ~200 KB (HTML + metadata, not images) Total unique content per day: 50M × 200 KB = 10 TB Cache the hot 20%: 10 TB × 20% = 2 TB of cache Redis/Memcached memory needed: 2 TB With 2× for overhead (hash tables, pointers): ~4 TB At 256 GB per cache server: 4 TB / 256 GB = 16 cache servers → 16 Redis/Memcached servers to cache the hot 20% of Reddit's daily content. That's surprisingly manageable for a top-20 website.

Type 5: Server Count Estimation (LinkedIn)

Server count estimation answers: how many machines do you need? This depends on whether you're CPU-bound, memory-bound, or I/O-bound — the bottleneck determines the answer.

LinkedIn Server Count DAU: 100 million Actions per user per day: ~20 (feed loads, profile views, searches) Total daily requests: 100M × 20 = 2 billion/day QPS = 2B / 86,400 ≈ 23,000 req/sec Peak QPS (3×) ≈ 70,000 req/sec Assume each app server handles ~1,000 req/sec (typical for Java/Spring apps): Servers needed: 70,000 / 1,000 = 70 app servers With 30% headroom for spikes: 70 × 1.3 = 91 servers Round up to ~100 app servers → ~100 application servers for LinkedIn's peak traffic. This doesn't count database servers, cache servers, search servers, etc. — each is sized separately.
Important nuance: "Servers" is fuzzy. A modern app server (8 cores, 32 GB RAM) running optimized Go or Rust might handle 10,000 req/sec. A Python Django app might handle 200 req/sec. Always state your assumption: "Assuming a Java app server handles ~1,000 req/sec..."
Section 9

At Scale — Real Company Estimation Stories

Theory is good, but seeing how actual companies estimated and planned their infrastructure makes it real. These are public numbers from engineering blogs, conference talks, and filings — the kind of data points that make your interview estimates credible.

Netflix: Estimating CDN Needs

Netflix serves about 250 million subscribers worldwide. At peak (Sunday evening in the US), Netflix accounts for roughly 15% of all downstream internet traffic on the planet. How did they plan for this?

Their engineers at Open Connect (Netflix's custom CDN) started with the same estimation method we learned:

Netflix CDN Estimation Subscribers: 250 million Average viewing: ~3.2 hours/day per active account Concurrent viewers at peak: ~10% of subscribers = 25 million Average bitrate: ~5 Mbps (adjusts by device and connection) Peak bandwidth: 25M × 5 Mbps = 125 Tbps One CDN server (10 Gbps NIC, serving from cache): ~8 Gbps usable Servers needed: 125 Tbps / 8 Gbps = ~15,600 servers → Netflix deployed 17,000+ Open Connect Appliances (OCAs) in ISP data centers worldwide. The estimation was within 10% of the real deployment. That's a success.

The critical insight from Netflix's estimation: it's physically impossible to serve 125 Tbps from AWS data centers. The solution was to build custom CDN hardware (Open Connect Appliances) and embed them inside ISP networks, so the video data travels the shortest possible path to users. The estimation didn't just size the system — it proved that a CDN was mandatory, not optional.

Slack: 12 Billion Messages per Week

Slack's engineering team shared in a 2023 blog post that their platform handles about 12 billion messages per week. Let's verify that number with estimation and see what it implies for infrastructure:

Slack Message Estimation Paid workspaces: ~750,000 Average users per workspace: ~50 Total active users: ~37.5 million DAU: ~20 million (enterprise users are active most workdays) Messages per user per day: ~85 (Slack's own reported number) Daily messages: 20M × 85 = 1.7 billion/day Weekly: 1.7B × 7 = 11.9 billion/week ✓ (matches the 12B reported) Write QPS: 1.7B / 86,400 ≈ 20,000 messages/sec Average message size: ~200 bytes (text + metadata) Daily storage: 1.7B × 200 bytes = 340 GB/day (text only) But messages with files/images: ~5% have attachments averaging 500 KB File storage: 1.7B × 5% × 500 KB = 42.5 TB/day → Text messages are tiny (340 GB/day). File attachments are 125x larger (42.5 TB/day). This is why Slack stores messages in a database but files in object storage (S3).

Stripe: Payment Processing Capacity

Stripe processes payments for millions of businesses. Their engineering team must estimate capacity not for average load, but for extreme spikes — Black Friday, Singles' Day, flash sales.

Stripe Capacity Estimation Annual payment volume: ~$1 trillion (2023 reported) Average transaction size: ~$50 Total transactions/year: $1T / $50 = 20 billion Daily transactions: 20B / 365 ≈ 55 million/day Average TPS: 55M / 86,400 ≈ 635 transactions/sec Black Friday spike: ~10× normal Peak TPS: ~6,350 transactions/sec But each transaction involves: - Auth request to card network: 1 API call - Fraud check: 1-3 ML inference calls - Database writes: 2-4 (charge, event log, ledger) - Webhook delivery: 1-5 outbound calls Total internal QPS per transaction: ~10× Real peak QPS: 6,350 × 10 = ~63,500 internal req/sec → Each payment triggers ~10 internal operations. The 635 TPS becomes 63,500 internal QPS at peak. This is why Stripe needs a distributed architecture even though 635 TPS sounds "small."

The key lesson from Stripe: external QPS and internal QPS are very different numbers. One user-facing request often fans out into 5-20 internal requests (database queries, cache lookups, service-to-service calls, queue operations). Always ask yourself: "What happens behind each request?"

Pattern: Every one of these companies' numbers can be derived using the 5-step method from Section 6. Netflix: 250M subscribers x 10% concurrent x 5 Mbps = 125 Tbps. Slack: 20M DAU x 85 messages x 200 bytes = 340 GB/day for text. The method works at any scale — startup or FAANG.
Section 10

The Anti-Lesson — When Estimation Goes Wrong

Estimation is powerful, but it can also lead you astray if you fall into common traps. Here are the three deadliest mistakes that turn a useful tool into a dangerous one.

Trap 1: The Over-Precision Trap

You're in an interview. The interviewer asks "How much storage?" You start calculating: "Well, each user has a username averaging 8.3 characters at 1 byte each, plus an email averaging 22.7 characters, plus a password hash of exactly 60 bytes for bcrypt, plus a created_at timestamp of 8 bytes, plus..."

Stop. You've just spent 5 minutes calculating that a user record is 347 bytes instead of rounding to "about 500 bytes." That precision gained you nothing — the final answer will be the same order of magnitude either way — but it cost you 5 precious interview minutes.

The rule: If rounding changes your answer by less than 2x, the rounding doesn't matter. 347 bytes vs 500 bytes? Doesn't matter. 100 MB vs 10 GB? That matters — that's a 100x difference. Save your precision for the assumptions that actually move the needle.

Trap 2: Precise Math on Wrong Inputs

This is the most insidious trap. You do beautiful, careful math — but your starting assumption is wrong. "Let's assume every user sends 500 messages per day." If the real number is 40, your entire cascade of calculations is off by 12.5x. All that careful arithmetic was wasted because the input was bad.

How to avoid it: Always sanity-check your inputs before doing the math. "500 messages per day — is that right? I send maybe 50 on a busy day. Heavy users might send 200. Let's use 40-100 as the range." If you're unsure, use a range instead of a single number. "Between 40 and 100 messages → between 4 TB and 10 TB." That range is more honest and more useful than a single precise-but-wrong number.

Trap 3: "Just Add More Servers"

An interviewer says: "Your system needs to handle 100,000 QPS." You respond: "Easy — just add 100 servers at 1,000 QPS each." The interviewer's face drops. Why? Because not every problem is solved by adding servers.

Some bottlenecks are fundamentally unscalable by just adding machines:

BottleneckWhy More Servers Don't HelpWhat Actually Helps
Database writes (single leader)All writes go to one machineSharding, write-ahead log, async writes
Cross-region latencySpeed of light is constantEdge computing, multi-region deployment
Hot key in cacheOne key = one machine = one bottleneckKey splitting, local caches, read replicas
Sequential processingSteps that must happen in orderPipeline parallelism, batch processing
The bottom line: Your estimate is guaranteed wrong. The question is whether it's wrong by 2x (totally fine) or 1,000x (disaster). The method in Section 6 keeps you in the safe zone. The traps above are what push you into the danger zone. Be approximately right, not precisely wrong.
Section 11

Common Mistakes

These are the mistakes that show up in almost every beginner's first estimation attempt. Each one can throw your answer off by 10x or more. Learn to spot them before they wreck your numbers.

Confusing bytes and bits

The mistake: Network speeds are in bits per second (Mbps, Gbps). Storage is in bytes (MB, GB). Mixing them up gives you an 8x error.

Example: "We need 100 MB/sec bandwidth" — if you provision a 100 Mbps link, you're 8x short. 100 MB/sec = 800 Mbps.

Fix: Always convert to the same unit. For bandwidth, use bits. For storage, use bytes. When switching between them, multiply or divide by 8.

Forgetting replication

The mistake: You estimate 10 TB of storage and stop there. In reality, every production database keeps 3 copies (one primary, two replicas). That's 30 TB, not 10 TB.

Fix: Always multiply raw storage by 3x for replication, then add 10-20% for indexes and metadata. A good rule: raw × 5 = realistic total.

Using average instead of peak

The mistake: "Average QPS is 5,000, so I'll provision for 5,000." Your system crashes every evening when traffic hits 15,000.

Fix: Always calculate both average AND peak (typically 2-5x average for social apps, 10-100x for flash sales/live events). Provision for peak, not average. Add 30% headroom on top of peak.

Ignoring media in storage calculations

The mistake: "A tweet is 280 characters = 280 bytes. So 500M tweets = 140 GB/day." You forgot that 20% of tweets have photos (2 MB each) and 5% have videos (50 MB each). The real number is 1.4 PB/day — you were off by 10,000x.

Fix: Always ask: "Does this system have media (photos, video, audio)?" If yes, media will dominate storage by 100-10,000x over text. Calculate text and media separately.

Forgetting read amplification

The mistake: "We have 60M rides per day, so 60M database reads." But each ride request actually triggers: 1 read for user profile, 1 read for payment method, 5-10 reads for nearby driver locations, 1 read for surge pricing, 1 read for route calculation. That's 8-13 reads per user-facing action.

Fix: Multiply user-facing actions by a fan-out factor (typically 5-20x) to get real internal QPS. State your assumption: "Each ride request triggers ~10 internal reads."

Assuming uniform global traffic

The mistake: Dividing daily traffic evenly across 24 hours. But users are concentrated in time zones, and most platforms have 60-70% of traffic in a 6-hour window.

Fix: For single-region apps, divide daily traffic by ~6 hours (21,600 seconds) instead of 24 hours for a realistic peak estimate. For global apps, consider that traffic follows the sun — there's always some peak somewhere.

Section 12

Interview Playbook

Knowing how to estimate is one thing. Knowing how to perform the estimation in an interview — out loud, under pressure, while sounding confident — is a different skill entirely. Here's the playbook.

The 60-Second Estimation Routine

In an interview, you get about 60 seconds for estimation. Not 5 minutes. Here's the exact sequence:

Sample Interview Dialogue

Here's what a strong estimation sounds like in a real interview. The candidate is asked to design a notification system:

Interviewer: "Design a notification system for a social media platform."

Candidate: "Before I start drawing boxes, let me get a sense of the scale. I'll assume we're talking about a platform with about 500 million DAU — something in the Instagram/Twitter range. Sound reasonable?"

Interviewer: "Sure, go ahead."

Candidate: "OK. Each user might receive about 20 notifications per day — likes, comments, follows, system alerts. That's 500 million times 20, so 10 billion notifications per day.

Divided by 86,400 seconds... that's roughly 115,000 notifications per second. At peak — say 3x for evening hours — about 350,000 per second.

Each notification is small — maybe 500 bytes for the payload (user ID, action type, message, timestamp, target link). So storage is 10B times 500 bytes = 5 TB per day, or about 1.8 PB per year before replication.

Now the interesting part: 350K writes per second is significant. A single PostgreSQL instance handles maybe 10-20K writes per second. So we'd need sharding or a write-optimized store — maybe Cassandra or a message queue like Kafka for the write path. The read side is even harder: if each user opens the app and loads their notification feed 5 times a day, that's 2.5 billion reads per day, or about 30,000 reads per second. Easily cacheable with Redis.

So the architecture should be: Kafka for ingesting notifications at 350K/sec, Cassandra for persistent storage, Redis for the per-user notification feed cache. That gives us the write throughput we need, the storage durability, and fast reads."

Interviewer: (nods, impressed) "Great. Let's dig into the Kafka partition strategy..."

Notice the pattern: assumptions first ("500M DAU, 20 notifications each"), then math ("10B/day, 115K/sec"), then architectural conclusion ("need Kafka + Cassandra + Redis"). The estimation drove every design decision. That's exactly what interviewers want to see.

What Strong vs Weak Looks Like

  • States assumptions upfront: "Let's assume 100M DAU..."
  • Shows the multiplication on the whiteboard
  • Rounds aggressively: 86,400 → "about 100K"
  • Calculates both average AND peak QPS
  • Draws an architectural conclusion from the numbers
  • Mentions replication, caching, sharding when numbers justify it
  • Finishes estimation in under 60 seconds
  • Skips estimation entirely: "Let me just draw the components..."
  • Guesses without showing work: "Probably a few terabytes"
  • Gets lost in precision: "Actually, let me recalculate that..."
  • Forgets peak traffic: "Average is 5K QPS, let's provision for that"
  • No connection between numbers and design: estimates, then ignores them
  • Spends 5+ minutes on calculation instead of 60 seconds
Think First

Practice this out loud (seriously, out loud): "Design a file storage system like Google Drive. 500 million users, 10% active daily. Each active user uploads 2 files per day averaging 5 MB each." Do the 60-second estimation. What's the daily storage? QPS? Bandwidth? What does it imply for your architecture?

50M DAU × 2 files × 5 MB = 500 TB/day. Write QPS: 100M files/86,400 ≈ 1,157/sec. Ingress bandwidth: 1,157 × 5 MB = 5.8 GB/sec ≈ 46 Gbps. That's a LOT of ingress — you need distributed upload endpoints.
Section 13

Hands-On Challenges

Reading about estimation isn't enough — you need to practice doing it yourself. Set a timer for each exercise. The goal isn't to get the "right" answer — it's to practice the 5-step method until it becomes second nature.

Exercise 1: Estimate a Ride-Sharing App Easy

Assume a ride-sharing app with 20 million DAU. Each rider takes 1.5 rides per day. Each ride involves 15 GPS pings (100 bytes each), a trip record (2 KB), and a payment (500 bytes). Estimate daily storage, write QPS, and peak bandwidth.

Hints
  • Total rides per day: 20M × 1.5 = 30M rides
  • GPS pings: 30M rides × 15 pings = 450M pings/day
  • Don't forget to add trip records + payments to the GPS storage
  • Peak multiplier for ride-sharing: ~3-5x (morning + evening commute)
Solution

Storage: GPS: 450M × 100B = 45 GB/day. Trips: 30M × 2 KB = 60 GB/day. Payments: 30M × 500B = 15 GB/day. Total: ~120 GB/day raw, ~600 GB/day with replication + indexes.

Write QPS: 450M GPS pings / 86,400 ≈ 5,200 writes/sec. Peak (4×) ≈ 20,800/sec.

Bandwidth: Ingress: 5,200 × 100B = 520 KB/sec ≈ 4 Mbps. Very light — this is a small app compared to Uber's scale.

Exercise 2: Estimate a Video Platform Medium

A video platform has 500 million DAU. Each user watches 30 minutes of video per day (average bitrate: 4 Mbps). 0.1% of DAU upload a video daily (average 5 minutes at 10 Mbps). Estimate storage, egress bandwidth, and the number of CDN servers needed (each serves 8 Gbps).

Hints
  • Uploaded videos: 500M × 0.1% = 500,000 videos/day
  • Each uploaded video: 5 min × 10 Mbps / 8 = ~37.5 MB
  • But videos are transcoded into multiple resolutions (360p, 720p, 1080p, 4K) — multiply storage by ~5x
  • For concurrent viewers: 500M × 30 min spread over 1,440 min/day
Solution

Upload storage: 500K videos × 37.5 MB = 18.75 TB/day raw. With 5 resolutions: ~94 TB/day. With 3× replication: ~280 TB/day.

Egress bandwidth: 500M users × 30 min = 15B minutes/day. Concurrent viewers: 15B / 1,440 ≈ 10.4M. Egress: 10.4M × 4 Mbps = 41.6 Tbps. Peak (2×) ≈ 83 Tbps.

CDN servers: 83 Tbps / 8 Gbps = ~10,375 CDN edge servers. Netflix has ~17,000 — our estimate is in the right ballpark for a smaller platform.

Exercise 3: Estimate a Gaming Platform Hard

An online multiplayer gaming platform (like Steam) has 30 million concurrent players at peak. Each player's game client sends a position update every 50ms (64 bytes). The platform also stores game replays (average 20 minutes at 1 KB/sec). Estimate the write QPS, daily storage for position updates, and monthly storage for replays.

Hints
  • Position updates: 30M players × (1000ms / 50ms) = 30M × 20 = 600M updates/sec
  • Position data is ephemeral — you only need it for the current game session, not forever
  • Replays: estimate how many games are played per day, then multiply by replay size
  • A 20-minute replay at 1 KB/sec = 1,200 KB ≈ 1.2 MB per replay
Solution

Write QPS: 600 MILLION writes per second. This is astronomically high — no database handles this. This data must go to an in-memory system (Redis/custom UDP server) and be discarded after each game session.

Position storage (daily, ephemeral): 600M/sec × 64 bytes × 86,400 sec = 3.3 PB/day. But this is throwaway data — you don't persist it. Real-time only.

Replays: Assume 50M unique games/day (not all concurrent players play at once, sessions overlap). 50M × 1.2 MB = 60 TB/day. Monthly: 60 × 30 = 1.8 PB/month. With 2× replication (replays are less critical): 3.6 PB/month in object storage.

Key insight: The 600M writes/sec number proves that game position data CANNOT use traditional databases. It must be in-memory, UDP-based, and ephemeral. The estimation drives the architecture more powerfully here than in any other example.
