Networking Foundations

Proxies

The invisible middlemen that shield your servers, speed up your site, and keep attackers at bay. Every large-scale system has one — here's why.

Section 1

TL;DR — The One-Minute Version

Mental Model: A proxy is a middleman that sits between two parties and handles communication on their behalf. Like a receptionist at an office — visitors don't walk straight to your desk; they go through the front desk first.

Whenever your browser loads a webpage, your request travels across the internet and hits a server. That's the simple version. In reality, most requests don't go directly to the server. They pass through a proxy — an intermediary that receives the request, decides what to do with it, and forwards it along. Sometimes the proxy works on behalf of the client (that's a forward proxyA proxy that sits in front of clients. It forwards requests on their behalf, often for privacy, filtering, or caching. Think of it as a personal assistant making calls for you.). Sometimes it works on behalf of the server (that's a reverse proxyA proxy that sits in front of servers. Clients don't know it exists — they think they're talking to the real server. It handles load balancing, caching, SSL, and security.).

Why bother? Because proxies give you superpowers: caching (serve responses without hitting the server), load balancing (spread traffic across multiple servers), SSL termination (handle encryption so servers don't have to), security (hide your servers from the public internet), and compression (shrink responses before sending them). Every company at scale — Netflix, Google, Amazon — runs reverse proxies in front of their servers. It's not optional; it's standard infrastructure.

One-line takeaway: A proxy is a middleman between client and server. Forward proxies protect clients; reverse proxies protect servers. Every production system uses at least one.
Section 2

Why You Need This

You've built an API. It's running on a single server, exposed directly to the internet. Everything works. Then your app takes off. Thousands of users start hitting your server every second. And suddenly, problems pile up fast.

Your server is doing everything: serving your app, handling encryption, fighting off attackers, and trying to stay alive under the load. That's too many jobs for one machine. You need someone standing in front of it — filtering the bad stuff, caching the repetitive stuff, and spreading the load when it gets heavy. You need a proxy.

And the problems compound. When your server is busy handling 5,000 legitimate requests, it has no CPU left to deal with the 50,000 bot requests hammering it at the same time. Your response times go from 200ms to 12 seconds. Users start leaving. Your monitoring page is loading so slowly you can't even see what's wrong. You're trying to fix a fire while standing inside the burning building.

The core problem: Your application server is doing four jobs that aren't its job: SSL encryption, traffic filtering, request caching, and load distribution. That's like asking a chef to also be the bouncer, the cashier, and the delivery driver. The food quality (your app performance) suffers because the chef is spread too thin.
Think First: Imagine you're a celebrity. Would you give your home address to every fan, or would you hire a manager to handle all messages on your behalf? A reverse proxy is that manager for your server.
Section 3

First Attempt — Direct Client-to-Server

The simplest possible architecture: your client talks directly to your server. No middleman. The browser sends a request to your server's IP address, the server processes it, and sends back a response. Simple, clean, and perfectly fine for a hobby project.

This works when you have a few dozen users. But here's the problem: your server is doing everything — parsing requests, running your application logic, encrypting responses with SSL, serving static files, and defending against bad traffic. It's like a one-person restaurant where the same person cooks, serves, cleans, and handles the front door. It works until you get busy.

Real-world example: In 2016, a major DDoS attack targeted Dyn (a DNS provider). Their servers were directly exposed, and the attack took down Twitter, Netflix, Reddit, and GitHub for hours. A proxy layer with DDoS absorption (like Cloudflare) could have filtered the malicious traffic before it ever reached the DNS servers.

Some developers argue: "My cloud provider gives me a firewall, isn't that enough?" Not even close. A firewallA network device or software that filters traffic based on IP addresses, ports, and protocols. It operates at L3/L4 and can block or allow connections, but it can't inspect HTTP content, cache responses, or do load balancing. blocks bad IP ranges and closes unused ports. It doesn't understand HTTP, can't cache your responses, can't distribute traffic across servers, and can't terminate SSL. A proxy works at the application layer — it reads your requests, makes smart decisions, and does the heavy lifting your servers shouldn't have to do. A firewall + proxy is the proper defense-in-depth approach.

Think First: Your server's IP address is now publicly known to every client. That means anyone can directly attack it. No bouncer at the door, no caller ID — just an open line to your machine.
Section 4

Where It Breaks

The direct setup breaks in four critical ways. Each one is a ticking time bomb, and they all go off around the same time — when your app starts getting real traffic.

🔓 No Security Shield

Your server's real IP address is visible to the world. Attackers can flood it with fake requests (DDoS attacksDistributed Denial of Service — thousands of compromised machines send junk traffic to your server simultaneously, overwhelming it so real users can't get through.), probe for vulnerabilities, or try to break in directly. Without a proxy, you have no first line of defense.

🐌 No Caching Layer

Every single request — even identical ones — goes all the way to your server. A thousand users asking for the same homepage? That's a thousand times your server runs the exact same work. A caching proxy would serve the first response to all of them without breaking a sweat.

🔐 SSL Is Eating Your CPU

Encrypting and decrypting HTTPS traffic is expensive. Your server is doing this for every single connection, on top of running your actual application. That's like asking your chef to also manage the restaurant's accounting during dinner rush. SSL terminationEnding the encrypted (HTTPS) connection at the proxy level, then forwarding plain HTTP to your backend servers. The proxy handles the expensive crypto work so your app servers don't have to. at a proxy would free your server to focus on what it does best — running your app.

📈 No Way to Scale Horizontally

When one server isn't enough, you add more. But now clients are connecting directly — they only know about one server. How do you spread traffic across multiple servers without changing every client? You can't. You need something in front that knows about all your servers and distributes requests. That something is a reverse proxy.

Section 5

The Breakthrough — Put a Proxy in Front

The fix is elegant and universal: stop exposing your server directly. Instead, put a machine in front of it — a reverse proxy — that handles all the messy, dangerous, repetitive work. Clients talk to the proxy; the proxy talks to your server. Your server's IP stays hidden, its CPU stays focused, and you can add more servers behind the proxy whenever you need.

This single architectural change solves all four problems from the previous section. Security? The proxy hides your servers and can block malicious traffic. Caching? The proxy stores popular responses and serves them without touching your servers. SSL? The proxy handles encryption, forwarding plain HTTP internally. Scaling? Just add more servers behind the proxy — clients don't notice a thing.

Before vs After:
  • Security: Server IP exposed → Server IP hidden behind proxy
  • Caching: Every request hits backend → Repeated requests served from cache
  • SSL: Backend CPU wasted on crypto → Proxy handles all TLS
  • Scaling: Single server ceiling → Add servers behind proxy anytime
  • Observability: Blind to traffic patterns → Proxy logs every request
Think First: Notice that clients now see HTTPS (encrypted) going to the proxy, but the proxy forwards plain HTTP to backend servers. This is SSL termination — the proxy "terminates" the encrypted connection and talks to your internal servers in the clear. This is safe because the backend servers are on a private network that the outside world can't reach.
Section 6

How It Works — Four Types of Proxies

Not all proxies are the same. They differ in who they protect, where they sit, and whether you know they're there. Let's break down the four main types.

1. Forward Proxy — Protects the Client

A forward proxy sits in front of clients. It takes a client's request and sends it to the internet on the client's behalf. The server on the other end doesn't know who the real client is — it only sees the proxy's IP address. Think of it like a VPN or a corporate network gateway.

Common uses: hiding your identity online (privacy), bypassing geo-restrictions ("I want to watch UK Netflix from India"), corporate internet filtering ("block social media at work"), and caching frequently visited sites to save bandwidth.

2. Reverse Proxy — Protects the Server

A reverse proxy sits in front of servers. The client has no idea the proxy exists — it thinks it's talking directly to the server. Meanwhile, the proxy handles SSL, caching, load balancing, and security. This is the one you'll see in almost every production system. Nginx, HAProxy, and Envoy are all reverse proxies.

3. Transparent Proxy — Nobody Knows It's There

A transparent proxyA proxy that intercepts traffic without any configuration on the client or server side. Neither party knows the proxy exists. ISPs and corporate networks use these for filtering and caching. intercepts traffic without anyone configuring it. The client doesn't set up a proxy. The server doesn't know one exists. It just sits in the network path and silently processes traffic. ISPs use these to cache popular websites and save bandwidth. Hotels use them to redirect you to their Wi-Fi login page. Some governments use them to block certain websites.

4. Sidecar Proxy — Lives Next to Your App

A sidecar proxy runs alongside your application — in the same machine or the same container pod. Instead of one big central proxy for everything, every service gets its own tiny proxy. All traffic in and out of your service goes through this sidecar first. This is the foundation of a service meshA network of sidecar proxies attached to every service in your system. They handle encryption, retries, load balancing, and observability — so your application code doesn't have to. Istio and Linkerd are popular service meshes. — a pattern used by companies like Lyft (Envoy), Google, and Netflix.

The beauty of the sidecar model is that your application code stays clean. Your service just talks to "localhost" and the sidecar handles everything else — encryption, retries, circuit breaking, metrics collection. The networking complexity is completely separated from your business logic.

Think First: Quick summary — forward proxy = protects clients, reverse proxy = protects servers, transparent proxy = invisible to both, sidecar proxy = one per service. In system design interviews, "proxy" almost always means reverse proxy.
Section 7

Going Deeper — What Proxies Actually Do

We said proxies handle "SSL, caching, load balancing, security." But what does that actually mean under the hood? Let's unpack each superpower one by one.

SSL/TLS Termination

When a browser connects to your site over HTTPS, there's a TLS handshakeA multi-step negotiation at the start of every HTTPS connection where client and server agree on encryption keys. It involves certificate verification, key exchange, and cipher negotiation — and it's CPU-intensive. — a negotiation where the client and server agree on encryption keys. This is computationally expensive. If your backend servers handle this for every connection, they're wasting CPU cycles on crypto instead of running your app.

With SSL termination, the proxy handles all the encryption/decryption. It presents the SSL certificate to clients, does the handshake, decrypts incoming requests, and forwards plain HTTP to your backend servers. Your backend servers never touch a certificate.

Connection Pooling

Every time a client connects to a server, there's overhead: a TCP handshake (3 round trips), possibly a TLS handshake (2 more round trips), and memory allocation. If you have 10,000 clients, that's 10,000 open connections your backend must maintain — each eating memory and file descriptors.

A proxy solves this with connection poolingKeeping a small set of persistent connections between the proxy and backend servers, then reusing them for many client requests. 10,000 client connections might map to just 50 backend connections.. It accepts thousands of client connections, but uses only a small pool of persistent connections to your backend. 10,000 client connections become 50 backend connections. Your servers breathe easy.

Request Routing & Load Balancing

When you have multiple backend servers, the proxy decides which one gets each request. This is load balancingDistributing incoming traffic across multiple servers so no single server is overwhelmed. Common algorithms: round-robin (take turns), least-connections (pick the least busy), weighted (stronger servers get more), and IP-hash (same user always goes to same server).. Common strategies:

  • Round Robin — take turns: Server 1, Server 2, Server 3, Server 1...
  • Least Connections — send to whichever server is currently handling the fewest requests
  • Weighted — powerful servers get more traffic than weaker ones
  • IP Hash — same client always goes to the same server (useful for session stickiness)

The proxy can also do path-based routing: send /api/* requests to your API servers, /images/* to a CDN, and /admin/* to an internal admin server.

Header Manipulation

Proxies can add, remove, or modify HTTP headers as requests pass through. This is useful for:

  • X-Forwarded-For — tells the backend server the real client IP (since the proxy's IP is what actually connects)
  • X-Request-ID — adds a unique ID to every request for tracing through distributed systems
  • Security headers — adds HSTS, Content-Security-Policy, X-Frame-Options automatically so your app code doesn't need to
  • CORS headers — handles cross-origin requestsWhen a webpage at domain A tries to make an API call to domain B, the browser blocks it by default (same-origin policy). CORS headers from domain B tell the browser "it's okay, let domain A talk to me." at the proxy level instead of in your application

The beauty of handling headers at the proxy is consistency. Instead of every backend service implementing security headers independently (and some teams forgetting), the proxy enforces them uniformly across all responses.

Caching at the Proxy Layer

Proxy caching is one of the most effective performance optimizations you can make. The proxy stores a copy of the response the first time it's requested. For subsequent identical requests, the proxy serves the cached copy without even contacting your backend server. Your server does zero work, and the response time drops from 200ms to 2ms.

How does the proxy know what to cache and for how long? It reads the Cache-Control headers from your backend's response:

  • Cache-Control: public, max-age=3600 — cache for 1 hour, any proxy can cache it
  • Cache-Control: private, max-age=300 — only the user's browser should cache it, not the proxy (contains user-specific data)
  • Cache-Control: no-store — never cache this (sensitive data like bank balances)
  • Cache-Control: no-cache — cache it, but validate with the server before serving (check if it's still fresh)

A well-configured caching proxy can reduce your backend load by 60-90%. That's not a typo — if most of your traffic is reading the same product pages, blog posts, or API responses, the proxy handles it all without your servers lifting a finger.

Nginx vs HAProxy vs Envoy — The Big Three

Three tools dominate the proxy world. Each started with a different philosophy, but they've been converging over the years. Here's the honest breakdown:

Nginx (pronounced "engine-X") started as a web server and evolved into a reverse proxy. It's the most widely used — about 34% of all websites run on Nginx. Brilliant for serving static files, SSL termination, and basic load balancing. Configuration is file-based (nginx.conf). Downsides: reloading config requires restarting worker processes, no built-in health checking in the open-source version, and the advanced features are locked behind Nginx Plus (paid).

Best for: General-purpose reverse proxy, static file serving, small to medium deployments.

HAProxy (High Availability Proxy) was born as a pure load balancerA device or software that distributes incoming network traffic across multiple backend servers. HAProxy is one of the oldest and most battle-tested load balancers in existence.. It's legendary for its performance — it can handle millions of concurrent connections with minimal latency. Built-in health checks, detailed stats dashboard, graceful config reloads (no dropped connections), and rock-solid stability. Downsides: can't serve static files (it's not a web server), less flexible with complex routing rules, and the config format is unique.

Best for: High-performance load balancing, TCP/UDP proxying, environments where every microsecond matters.

Envoy was created by Lyft specifically for cloud-native, microservice architectures. It's designed to be a sidecar proxy — one instance per service. Key differentiators: hot config reloading (change routing without restarting), first-class gRPC support, built-in distributed tracing, automatic retries and circuit breaking, and an API-driven control plane (no config files). Downsides: higher memory usage, steeper learning curve, and overkill for simple setups.

Best for: Kubernetes/microservices, service meshes (Istio uses Envoy), modern cloud-native architectures.

Path-Based Routing

One of the most powerful proxy features is routing requests to different backend services based on the URL path. Instead of having one monolithic server handle everything, you can split your system into specialized services and let the proxy direct traffic to the right one.

This is incredibly powerful because each backend can be scaled independently. Your API might need 10 servers during a sale, but your admin panel only needs 1. The proxy handles the routing; each team manages their own service.

Section 8

Variations — Proxies in Disguise

Several things you've heard of are actually proxies wearing different hats. Understanding this helps you see the bigger picture — and avoid confusion in interviews.

API Gateway vs Reverse Proxy

An API gatewayA specialized reverse proxy designed for APIs. It adds features like authentication, rate limiting, request transformation, API versioning, and developer portal management on top of basic proxying. is a reverse proxy with extra features bolted on. A plain reverse proxy forwards requests and maybe caches them. An API gateway also handles authentication, rate limiting, request/response transformation, API key management, and analytics. Think of it this way: every API gateway is a reverse proxy, but not every reverse proxy is an API gateway.

Examples: Kong, AWS API Gateway, Azure API Management, Apigee — all API gateways built on top of reverse proxy fundamentals.

When to use which? If your system is a simple web app with a React frontend and one API, a reverse proxy (Nginx) is all you need. If you have multiple APIs that different clients (mobile, web, partners) consume differently, an API gateway adds the auth, rate limiting, and transformation features you'll need. Don't over-engineer: start with a reverse proxy, graduate to a gateway when the complexity justifies it.

CDN as a Proxy

A CDNContent Delivery Network — a global network of proxy servers that cache your content at locations close to users. When someone in Tokyo requests your image, they get it from a Tokyo server instead of your origin server in New York. (Content Delivery Network) is essentially a distributed network of reverse proxy servers spread across the globe. When a user in Tokyo requests your website, instead of that request traveling all the way to your server in New York, it hits the nearest CDN edge server in Tokyo. If the CDN has a cached copy, it serves it instantly. If not, it fetches from your origin server, caches it, and serves it. Cloudflare, Akamai, Fastly, and AWS CloudFront are all CDNs — and they're all reverse proxies at their core.

Service Mesh — Proxies Everywhere

A service mesh takes the sidecar proxy concept and applies it to your entire system. Every microservice gets its own sidecar proxy (usually Envoy). A central control planeThe brain of a service mesh. It tells all the sidecar proxies how to route traffic, what policies to enforce, and collects metrics from them. Istio's control plane is called Istiod. (like Istio or Linkerd) manages all the sidecars, pushing routing rules, security policies, and observability config to them. The result: your application code only handles business logic, while the mesh handles networking, security, retries, tracing, and load balancing.

Trade-off: A service mesh adds significant operational complexity. You need Kubernetes (usually), you need to learn the mesh's configuration language, and debugging becomes harder because every request goes through two extra proxy hops. It's powerful but not free.

Think First: Here's a handy way to remember these variations. Ask two questions: (1) "Is the proxy between the internet and my servers?" If yes, it's a reverse proxy or API gateway. (2) "Does every service get its own proxy?" If yes, it's a service mesh/sidecar. (3) "Is it a global network of caching servers?" If yes, it's a CDN.
Section 9

At Scale — How the Giants Use Proxies

Every major tech company runs proxies at a massive scale. Here's how three different companies use proxies in three very different ways — each one is a masterclass in proxy design.

Cloudflare — Proxy as a Service

Cloudflare is essentially a global reverse proxy. When you put your site behind Cloudflare, your DNS points to Cloudflare's servers instead of yours. Every request goes through Cloudflare first, where it gets DDoS protection, WAF (Web Application Firewall) filtering, SSL termination, caching, and image optimization — before being forwarded to your actual server. As of 2024, Cloudflare proxies roughly 20% of all internet traffic through 300+ data centers worldwide.

Key insight: Cloudflare turned the reverse proxy into a product. Instead of running Nginx yourself, you outsource the proxy layer to Cloudflare. They handle the complexity, you get the benefits.

Lyft (Envoy) — Proxy for Microservices

Lyft had hundreds of microservices, and each team was implementing their own networking logic — retries, timeouts, circuit breaking, logging. It was chaos. In 2016, they created Envoy — a sidecar proxy that standardized all networking across every service. Instead of each service team writing retry logic, Envoy handled it uniformly. The result: consistent behavior, better observability (every request is traced), and teams could focus on business logic instead of networking plumbing.

Envoy was so successful that it became an open-source CNCF project and is now the default proxy in Istio (Google's service mesh), AWS App Mesh, and many other platforms. The lesson: when networking complexity becomes a problem across hundreds of services, the answer isn't "fix each service" — it's "move networking into a uniform proxy layer."

Why sidecar instead of centralized? A centralized proxy means one team owns the proxy and every other team files tickets to change routing rules. A sidecar per service means each team controls their own networking behavior through the mesh configuration — much faster iteration when you have 200 teams.

Netflix — Multi-Layer Proxy Architecture

Netflix uses proxies at multiple layers. At the edge, they built Zuul — a custom gateway proxy that handles authentication, routing, canary testing, and load shedding. Behind Zuul, their microservices communicate through Ribbon (client-side load balancing — essentially a proxy embedded in the client) and Eureka (service discovery that proxies don't need to know about server locations). They also use their own CDN called Open Connect — a network of proxy/cache servers placed directly inside ISPs' data centers, so Netflix streams never even traverse the public internet.

Key insight: Netflix doesn't have "one proxy." They have proxies at every layer — edge, mid-tier, client-side, and CDN. Each proxy solves a different problem at a different scale.

Think First: Notice the pattern: small companies start with one reverse proxy (Nginx). As they grow, they add specialized proxies at each layer — edge, internal, per-service. There's no "one proxy to rule them all" at scale. You mix and match based on what each layer needs.

Section 10

Common Mistakes & Gotchas

Proxies seem simple — "just put Nginx in front" — but the details trip up even experienced engineers. Here are the mistakes that come up over and over in real deployments and interviews.

Mistake #1: Forgetting X-Forwarded-For

What happens: Your app logs the client's IP address. But after adding a reverse proxy, every request shows the proxy's IP instead of the real client's IP. Your analytics, rate limiting, and geo-blocking all break overnight.

Why: The proxy is the one connecting to your backend. Your backend sees the proxy's IP as the "client." Unless the proxy explicitly passes along the original client IP in a header (usually X-Forwarded-For), that information is lost.

Fix: Configure your proxy to add the X-Forwarded-For header, and configure your backend to read client IPs from that header instead of the connection IP. But be careful — if you blindly trust this header, an attacker can spoof any IP. Always validate that the header comes from a trusted proxy.

Mistake #2: The Proxy Becomes a Single Point of Failure

What happens: You put everything behind one proxy. The proxy crashes. Your entire system goes down — every single service is unreachable.

Why: A proxy is only helpful if it's always up. If it's running on one machine with no failover, it's a single point of failureA component whose failure takes down the entire system. If you have 10 backend servers but one proxy, the proxy is the single point of failure — not the backend servers. — the very thing you were trying to avoid.

Fix: Run at least two proxy instances behind a floating IP or DNS-based failover. Use keepalived (for on-prem) or a cloud load balancer (for cloud) in front of your proxies. Yes, it's proxies in front of proxies — that's normal at scale.

Mistake #3: Not Tuning Timeouts

What happens: Your proxy has a 60-second timeout but your backend API sometimes takes 90 seconds for complex queries. The proxy cuts the connection, returns a 504 Gateway Timeout, and your users see an error — even though the backend was working, just slowly.

Why: Proxies have default timeouts for reading from upstream servers (often 60s). If your backend is slower than this default, the proxy kills the connection prematurely.

Fix: Set proxy_read_timeout (Nginx) or timeout server (HAProxy) to match your actual backend response times. But don't just set it to 5 minutes — that wastes resources on hung connections. Tune it per route: fast APIs get short timeouts, slow report generators get longer ones.

Mistake #4: Caching Without Invalidation Strategy

What happens: Your proxy caches API responses. You update a product's price in the database. Users keep seeing the old price because the proxy is serving stale cached data.

Why: Caching is easy to enable, but cache invalidationThe process of removing or updating stale data from a cache. It's famously one of the "two hard things in computer science" (the other being naming things). Getting it wrong means serving outdated data. is notoriously hard. If you don't have a strategy for when and how cached entries get refreshed, you'll serve stale data.

Fix: Use proper Cache-Control headers from your backend. Set appropriate max-age values. For dynamic data, use no-cache or short TTLs. For data that changes on demand, implement cache purge APIs.

Mistake #5: Enabling Caching on POST/PUT/DELETE Requests

What happens: You turn on proxy caching, and it accidentally caches responses to write operations. A user submits an order, gets a "success" page cached, and the next user sees that same "success" page without actually submitting their own order.

Why: Some proxy configurations cache all 200 responses by default, regardless of the HTTP method.

Fix: Only cache GET and HEAD requests. Never cache POST, PUT, PATCH, or DELETE. In Nginx: proxy_cache_methods GET HEAD; — this is the default, but always verify. Also ensure your backend sends appropriate Cache-Control: no-store on write responses.

Mistake #6: Not Setting Up Health Checks

What happens: One of your backend servers crashes. The proxy keeps sending traffic to it. Users randomly get 502 Bad Gateway errors because every few requests, the proxy picks the dead server.

Why: Without health checksPeriodic probes sent by the proxy to each backend server. If a server fails the check (times out, returns an error), the proxy removes it from the rotation until it recovers. Like a hospital checking patients' vital signs., the proxy assumes all configured backends are alive. It has no way to know a server has crashed.

Fix: Configure active health checks. HAProxy has this built-in (free). Nginx requires the Plus version for active checks, but the open-source version does passive health checks (marks a server as down after N consecutive failures). Envoy has both active and passive built-in.

Mistake #7: Mixing Up Forward and Reverse in Interviews

What happens: The interviewer asks "how does a proxy help with security?" and you describe a VPN (forward proxy) when they're asking about protecting your API (reverse proxy). You lose credibility.

Fix: Forward = sits in front of clients, protects/hides the client. Reverse = sits in front of servers, protects/hides the server. In 99% of system design interviews, they mean reverse proxy. If unsure, ask: "Are you asking about a client-side proxy or a server-side proxy?"

Section 11

Interview Playbook

Proxies come up in almost every system design interview — sometimes directly ("What is a reverse proxy?") and sometimes indirectly ("How would you handle 100K requests per second?"). Here's how to use proxy knowledge at each interview level.

The good news: proxy questions are predictable. There are about 5 questions that cover 90% of what interviewers ask. Master those, and you'll handle proxy topics confidently at any level.

Think First: In a system design interview, you should mention a reverse proxy in your architecture within the first 5 minutes. It's one of the "everyone uses this" components — like a database or a cache. Not mentioning it is a red flag.

Junior Level — Show You Know the Basics

What they expect: You know what a proxy is, the difference between forward and reverse, and you can draw it in your architecture diagram.

Talking points:

  • "I'll put an Nginx reverse proxy in front of my servers for SSL termination and load balancing."
  • "The proxy hides my backend servers from the public internet."
  • "Forward proxy protects clients (like a VPN), reverse proxy protects servers (like Nginx)."

Mid Level — Show Depth

What they expect: You can explain WHY you chose a specific proxy, configure it properly, and handle edge cases.

Talking points:

  • "I'd use Nginx for the reverse proxy because we need to serve static assets too. If we were purely API, HAProxy would give us better performance."
  • "The proxy does path-based routing: /api goes to the API cluster, /static goes to the CDN origin."
  • "We need to make sure X-Forwarded-For headers are passed through for rate limiting and logging."
  • "I'll configure connection pooling on the proxy to reduce backend connection overhead."

Senior Level — Show Systems Thinking

What they expect: You think about proxy high availability, multi-layer proxy architectures, and trade-offs between approaches.

Talking points:

  • "We'll run two proxy instances with keepalived for failover — the proxy can't be a SPOF."
  • "At the edge, I'd use Cloudflare for DDoS protection and global caching. Behind that, our own Nginx cluster handles application-level routing."
  • "For internal service-to-service communication, I'd deploy Envoy sidecars in a service mesh to get mutual TLS, retries, and observability without changing application code."
  • "The trade-off with a service mesh is operational complexity — we'd need a team to manage it. For 5 services, it's overkill. For 50, it's essential."
Interview Red Flag: If you design a system with multiple backend servers but no proxy or load balancer in front, the interviewer will immediately ask: "How does the client know which server to talk to?" If you can't answer, it signals a gap in production architecture knowledge. Always draw a proxy/LB between clients and servers in your diagram.

Common Interview Questions About Proxies

Here are the exact questions you'll hear and how to answer them concisely:

  • "What is a reverse proxy?" — "A server that sits between clients and backend servers, forwarding requests on behalf of the client. It handles SSL termination, load balancing, caching, and hides the backend topology. Nginx, HAProxy, and Envoy are common implementations."
  • "Why not just expose your servers directly?" — "Direct exposure means no security shield, no caching layer, no way to distribute load, and SSL overhead on every app server. A proxy solves all four problems in one layer."
  • "Forward vs reverse proxy?" — "Forward protects clients (hides their identity from servers — like a VPN). Reverse protects servers (hides their identity from clients — like Nginx). In system design, we almost always mean reverse."
  • "How do you handle proxy HA?" — "Run multiple proxy instances with active-passive failover (keepalived) or active-active behind DNS round-robin or a cloud L4 LB."

HTTPS goes from client to proxy. HTTP goes from proxy to backend. The proxy sits in a DMZ or public subnet; backends and database sit in a private subnet.

Exercise 2: Forward vs Reverse Scenarios Beginner

Classify each scenario as forward proxy or reverse proxy: (a) A company blocks employees from accessing social media. (b) A website uses Nginx to distribute traffic across 5 servers. (c) A user connects through a VPN to appear as if they're in another country. (d) An e-commerce site caches product pages to reduce server load. (e) A school filters which websites students can visit.

Solution

(a) Forward — company controls client access. (b) Reverse — server-side load balancing. (c) Forward — hides client identity. (d) Reverse — server-side caching. (e) Forward — controls client access at the network level.

Exercise 3: Choose the Right Tool Intermediate

For each scenario, pick Nginx, HAProxy, or Envoy and explain why: (a) A small startup needs a reverse proxy that also serves their React frontend's static files. (b) A fintech company needs a TCP-level proxy with sub-millisecond latency. (c) A company with 200 microservices on Kubernetes needs consistent networking behavior across all services.

Solution

(a) Nginx — it can serve static files AND act as a reverse proxy. HAProxy can't serve files, and Envoy is overkill. (b) HAProxy — legendary TCP-level performance, built for low-latency load balancing. (c) Envoy — designed for sidecar deployment in Kubernetes, with hot config reloading and built-in observability.

Exercise 4: Debug the Proxy Problem Intermediate

Your team deployed Nginx as a reverse proxy. Everything works, but your rate-limiting middleware (which limits by client IP) now treats all clients as the same IP. Users are getting rate-limited after just a few requests because all traffic appears to come from one source. What's wrong and how do you fix it?

Solution

The backend sees the proxy's IP for all requests. You need to: (1) Configure Nginx to pass X-Forwarded-For header with the real client IP. (2) Update your rate-limiting middleware to read the client IP from X-Forwarded-For instead of the connection source IP. (3) Validate that the header is from a trusted proxy to prevent spoofing.

Exercise 5: Explain the Request Journey Intermediate

A user types https://shop.example.com/api/products in their browser. The system uses Cloudflare as a CDN, Nginx as a reverse proxy, and has 3 backend API servers. Trace the complete journey of this request — every hop, every transformation, every decision — from the browser to the backend and back.

Solution

1. DNS: Browser resolves shop.example.com → gets Cloudflare's IP (not the origin server's IP). 2. Cloudflare edge: Request hits the nearest Cloudflare PoP. Cloudflare checks its cache — miss (dynamic API). Checks WAF rules — passes. Terminates TLS. Forwards to origin via Cloudflare tunnel. 3. Nginx: Receives HTTP request. Reads path /api/products → matches location /api block. Picks backend using round-robin (e.g., Server 2). Adds X-Forwarded-For and X-Request-ID headers. Forwards. 4. Backend: Server 2 processes the request, queries DB, builds JSON. Returns 200 with Cache-Control: public, max-age=60. 5. Return: Nginx receives response, could cache it for 60s. Forwards to Cloudflare. Cloudflare caches it at the edge for 60s. Re-encrypts with TLS. Sends to browser. Total hops: Browser → Cloudflare edge → Nginx → Server 2 → Nginx → Cloudflare → Browser (6 hops, ~150ms total).

Exercise 6: Design a Multi-Layer Proxy Architecture Advanced

Design the proxy architecture for an e-commerce platform that: serves customers globally (low latency), handles 50,000 requests/second during flash sales, has 40 microservices, needs DDoS protection, and requires mutual TLS between services. Describe each proxy layer, which tool you'd use, and why.

Solution

Layer 1 — CDN/DDoS: Cloudflare at the edge for DDoS protection, global caching of static assets, and SSL termination. Layer 2 — API Gateway: Kong or Nginx Plus for authentication, rate limiting, and path-based routing to different service clusters. Layer 3 — Service Mesh: Envoy sidecars (Istio) on each microservice for mutual TLS, retries, circuit breaking, and distributed tracing. This gives you global reach (Cloudflare), flash sale resilience (Kong rate limiting), security (mTLS via Istio), and observability (Envoy tracing).

Section 13

Cheat Sheet

Forward Proxy

Sits in front of clients. Hides client identity. Used for VPNs, corporate filtering, geo-bypass. Client configures it.

Reverse Proxy

Sits in front of servers. Hides server identity. Used for SSL, caching, load balancing, security. Client doesn't know it exists.

Transparent Proxy

Invisible interception. No config needed on client or server. Used by ISPs for caching, by hotels for captive portals.

SSL Termination

Proxy handles HTTPS decryption. Forwards plain HTTP to backends. Frees server CPU. Certificate lives on the proxy.

Nginx vs HAProxy vs Envoy

Nginx: general-purpose + static files. HAProxy: pure LB performance. Envoy: cloud-native sidecars + service mesh.

API Gateway

A reverse proxy + auth + rate limits + transforms + analytics. Every gateway is a proxy, not every proxy is a gateway.

Connection Pooling

Proxy reuses a small pool of connections to backends. 10K client connections → 50 backend connections. Massive resource savings.

X-Forwarded-For

Header that preserves the real client IP through proxy chains. Without it, your backend only sees the proxy's IP. Always validate.

CDN = Distributed Proxies

Cloudflare, Akamai, CloudFront are networks of caching reverse proxies at edge locations worldwide. Proxies all the way down.

L4 vs L7

L4 proxy routes by IP/port (fast, blind). L7 proxy reads HTTP content (smart, slower). AWS NLB=L4, ALB=L7. Most reverse proxies are L7.

Service Mesh

Sidecar proxy per service + control plane. Envoy + Istio. Handles mTLS, retries, tracing. Powerful but complex. For 50+ services.

Think First: If you remember only one thing from this page — in system design interviews, always mention a reverse proxy in your architecture. It shows you understand production infrastructure. "I'd put an Nginx reverse proxy in front for SSL termination, load balancing, and caching" is a sentence worth memorizing.
Section 14

Quick Q&A

Q1: Can a server be both a web server and a reverse proxy?

Yes! Nginx is the most common example. It can serve static files as a web server AND act as a reverse proxy for dynamic content at the same time. In a typical setup, Nginx serves your React/Vue frontend files directly and proxies API requests to your backend servers.

Q2: Is a load balancer the same as a reverse proxy?

Not exactly. A load balancer distributes traffic — that's its main job. A reverse proxy does that AND more (caching, SSL termination, header manipulation, compression). Every reverse proxy can load-balance, but a pure load balancer (like an AWS NLB operating at Layer 4) doesn't inspect HTTP content, cache responses, or terminate SSL. Think of a load balancer as a subset of what a reverse proxy can do.

Q3: What's the difference between L4 and L7 proxying?

Layer 4Layer 4 operates at the transport level (TCP/UDP). The proxy sees IP addresses and port numbers but doesn't look inside the actual data. It's faster because there's less to inspect. (transport) proxying routes based on IP and port — it doesn't look at the actual content. Layer 7Layer 7 operates at the application level (HTTP, gRPC, etc.). The proxy reads the full request — URL path, headers, cookies, body — and can make smarter routing decisions. It's slower but much more powerful. (application) proxying reads the HTTP request — URL, headers, cookies — and makes routing decisions based on content. L4 is faster (less inspection), L7 is smarter (content-aware routing). HAProxy does both. Nginx primarily does L7. AWS NLB is L4, AWS ALB is L7.

Q4: Do I need a proxy if I'm using a cloud provider?

Cloud providers give you managed proxies (AWS ALB/NLB, Azure Application Gateway, GCP Cloud Load Balancing). You're still using a proxy — you're just not managing the hardware. The concepts are identical: SSL termination, load balancing, health checks, routing. The difference is you configure it through a dashboard instead of an nginx.conf file.

Q5: When would I NOT use a reverse proxy?

For small, personal projects or prototypes where simplicity matters more than scalability. If you have one server, no SSL needs, no caching requirements, and no security concerns — direct access is fine. Also, for internal development servers that aren't exposed to the internet. But the moment your app faces real traffic or touches the public internet, a reverse proxy becomes essential.

Q6: What happens if the proxy itself gets overwhelmed?

You scale the proxy layer! Run multiple proxy instances behind DNS round-robin or a Layer 4 load balancer (like AWS NLB). The proxies themselves can be horizontally scaled just like your backend servers. At massive scale, companies like Cloudflare run thousands of proxy servers across hundreds of data centers.

Q7: What is SSL passthrough vs SSL termination?

With SSL termination, the proxy decrypts HTTPS and forwards plain HTTP to backends — the proxy "terminates" the encrypted session. With SSL passthrough, the proxy forwards the encrypted traffic as-is to the backend, without decrypting anything. The backend handles the certificate and decryption. Passthrough is used when the backend must see the original TLS connection (e.g., for client certificate authentication), but you lose the ability to cache, inspect, or modify the request at the proxy level.

Q8: How does a proxy handle WebSocket connections?

WebSockets start as a regular HTTP request with an "Upgrade" header. The proxy detects this, upgrades the connection, and then maintains a persistent, bidirectional tunnel between the client and backend. Nginx supports this with proxy_set_header Upgrade and proxy_set_header Connection "upgrade". The key difference from regular HTTP is that WebSocket connections are long-lived — the proxy must keep the connection open, not close it after the response. This means your timeout settings need to be much longer for WebSocket routes.

Q9: What's the difference between a proxy and a firewall?

A firewall operates at the network level — it allows or blocks traffic based on IP addresses, ports, and protocols. It doesn't understand HTTP or application-level content. A proxy operates at the application level — it understands HTTP, can read URLs, headers, cookies, and make intelligent routing decisions. They complement each other: the firewall blocks obvious bad traffic (wrong ports, known bad IPs), and the proxy handles application-level security (authentication, rate limiting, request validation). Most production setups use both.

Q10: Can I use multiple reverse proxies in a chain?

Yes, and this is common at scale! A typical chain: Cloudflare (CDN/DDoS) → Nginx (SSL/routing) → Application. Each proxy in the chain adds its processing. The main gotcha is that X-Forwarded-For becomes a comma-separated list as each proxy appends its own IP. Your backend must know how many proxies are in the chain to extract the real client IP correctly (trust the Nth entry from the right, where N is the number of trusted proxies).
